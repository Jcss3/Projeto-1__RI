EDH/Commander Deckbuilding 101: Packages, Synergy, and Theme: Featuring Marath Token Landfall

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

By Craig Sidorowicz

As a middle school social studies teacher with over a decade of experience, I’m always looking for ways to improve or refine my practice, improve lessons, and maximize the opportunities for my students to “get it.”  Best practice often requires breaking content knowledge into manageable chunks so that students are not overwhelmed with a tidal wave of concepts and information all at once.  Through many experiences in building, rebuilding, tuning, and critiquing EDH/Commander decks over the last five years, and other Magic the Gathering decks over the last twenty, I’ve found that most well-constructed decks can also be broken down into smaller chunks, or packages, just like those middle school history lessons…

Packages are groups of cards that serve specific functions in a deck, such as: get more mana (ramp package), reduce threats (removal package), or end the game (threat/combo package).  Often, these packages are interchangeable between decks of similar colors.  Counter magic is the type of spell often added to blue decks in packages.  Considering how ineffective it would be to run just one Counterspell in a deck of 100 cards, I often play 5-8.  They don’t necessarily have to be the best and most expensive counterspells available in the format.  While I could protect Edric’s Elves with Mana Drain, Force of Will, Pact of Negation, Cryptic Command, and Counterspell, I could just as easily do it with Plasm Capture, Hinder, Spell Crumple, Arcane Denial, and Counterspell.

Equipment is another card type often found in packages, and is enabled by creatures like Stoneforge Mystic, Stonehewer Giant, Puresteel Paladin, and encouraged by commanders like Nahiri the Lithomancer and Kemba, Kha Regent.  Sunforger is a piece of equipment that can actually have an entire package of instants built around it.  So if I include both packages in the same deck, my Stoneforge Mystic or Stonehewer Giant suddenly becomes an incredibly valuable draw because they grant access to my equipment package cards, and if I so choose, the Sunforger package cards.

When cards and packages overlap like this, they produce synergy.  Garruk Wildspeaker could be part of a ramp package, a token generator package, and finally, a game-closing package.  This is why the “O.G.” (Original Garruk) is such an amazing card—he is a synergy machine!  Synergy, or the potential for cards to functionally interact with one another and work together, adds virtual space to a deck, increasing the number of slots that can be filled with additional high-quality cards.  Furthermore, a high synergy card that has multiple functions can be seen as being worth “more than one card” because of its many applications.  This means that Garruk Wildspeaker can be seen as potentially worth “three cards,” while Overrun can only ever be worth one.  This should explain why Demonic Tutor (or ANY tutor) is so powerful:  It represents virtual access to “X” cards on any given turn, where X= the number of relevant cards in your deck you can cast that turn after paying 1B.  If your tutor finds another high synergy card, like a Planeswalker, it could be worth X+3…  (Here’s where skill comes in: If you don’t know which card to get with your tutor, or when to do it, then X might as well equal 0!  Check out my Treatise on Threat Assessment for some general pointers on gameplay.)

When packages produce enough synergy, they combine to form a theme.  Themes are often used to describe what a pilot expects the deck to do, guides card choices, and general style of play.  Examples of popular Magic/EDH themes include:

  • Aggro– Wins by playing creatures and attacking.
  • Control– Kills, counters, or prevent the things that attack from doing damage. Usually wins with a singular large threat after exhausting opponents resources.
  • Attrition– Wins by letting opponents kill each other, or produces continuous attackers/defenses. This could also be called aggro/control.
  • Reanimator– Uses the graveyard(s) as a resource.
  • Pillowfort– A form of control that uses defensive creatures and enchantments to form a wall that prevents opponents from attacking.
  • Stax– Wins by attacking resources, imposing upkeep taxes, or sacrifice effects.
  • Mill– Wins by placing opponents libraries in the graveyard.
  • Voltron– Wins by making a giant creature or commander, perhaps killing with 21 commander damage.
  • Tokens– Wins by making large swarms of token creatures.
  • Ramp– Wins by producing more resources than opponents, then using them to establish an overwhelming board presence that allows them to kill everyone at once, or in quick succession.
  • Chaos– Wins (maybe) by causing as much chaos and collateral damage as possible, usually by disrupting the general flow of play.
  • Group Hug– Wins (maybe) by causing as much chaos and collateral damage as possible, usually by disrupting the general flow of play but disguising it as being helpful!
  • Group Thug– Wins by using “punisher” mechanics: those that cause damage for playing lands, casting spells, or drawing cards.

This type of thematic deckbuilding is what I like to do, because it helps keep my decks focused, keeps me from building and rebuilding too many decks, or building decks that are too similar to one another.  The theory is that if I build my deck with these concepts in mind, my deck will function the way I want it to in a consistent manner.  While It’s impossible to plan for everything my opponents will do, I want to make sure that I at least execute my plan correctly and consistently.  The opposing theory is that I could just play all of the strongest cards, and disregard synergy and theme.  While this may yield “results” in the form of “more wins,” it becomes boring for this author rather quickly.  The compromise for me is to find and include the high-power cards that are also synergistic.

Now that we have a grasp on the concepts of packages, synergy, and theme, let’s apply this concept to my Marath, Will of the Wild deck to see how I put this strategy to good use.

 

About Marath Token Landfall:

The game plan is to acquire resources as fast as possible, as Marath is an endless vessel into which mana can be poured.  Marath also creates tokens, so the plan is also to maximize the amount of cards that positively interact with this primary theme, while maintaining high card quality in a vacuum (Think Cathar’s Crusade, as opposed to Intangible Virtue).  The secondary theme is Landfall, which is inspired by many of the new cards with this mechanic released in Battle for Zendikar.  Fortunately for my game plan, these two themes are both powerful and synergistic.  To top it all off, I’ve added a few non-infinite combos (with the “may ability” option to go infinite with Mana Echoes, Marath, and Cathar’s Crusade for those of you who can’t live without the prospect of a “sideways 8”).  These include several ways to make Purphuros, God of the Forge extremely lethal, and five Planeswalkers to abuse Doubling Season with.  I often find myself casting Marath on turn three, and if there is nothing better to do, getting aggressive with him and shooting a few elves or making a few elementals.

Marath gets better each time he is cast, so it is to our advantage if our opponents kill him, or if we lose him in an early blocking exchange.  His versatility in the early game is very high, and it’s hard to mis-spend mana on any of his abilities.  As turns progress, we can establish a board of creatures on parity with our opponents, and when the time is right we can begin to go on the offensive.  If someone plays something too threatening, I do not overextend into the inevitable board wipe, instead, I begin attacking them with existing threats and hope that other players follow suit or wipe the board.  I save my precious few removal spells for things that absolutely shut down my game plan, such as Moat, Ensnaring Bridge, Crawlspace, or any number of Pillowfort effects.  If all goes well, I hope that an alpha strike with an overwhelming horde of large and evasive tokens places Marath’s “Famous Buffalo Sauce” in the winner’s circle once again.

Package One:  Ramp

This ramp package may seem pretty complicated as I have a mix of creatures, spells, artifacts, and Planeswalkers, but these cards were specifically included to maximize landfall synergy.  Harrow, Kodama’s Reach, and Cultivate are as efficient as possible for their mana cost (I like Harrow over Skyshroud Claim because I’m playing Crucible of Worlds, as well as Titania, Protector of Argoth).  I’ve included Crop Rotation as a second copy of Gaea’s Cradle, and perhaps a second copy of Kessig Wolf Run in situations where I make a large amount of mana with Ashnod’s Altar, Mana Echoes, Mirari’s Wake, or Mana Reflection.  The ramp creatures are straightforward, and Knight of the Reliquary’s best trick to find our coveted Cradle, then to find fetch lands to produce additional landfall triggers.  Oblivion Sower is one of our new best friends, and can siphon massive amounts of land away from players who have fallen victim to graveyard removal.  I’ve been jamming the O-Sower into all of my decks as of late, and I’ve been treating him as the new Solemn Simulacrum.  My suggestion would be to pick up copies on the cheap, especially the duel deck foils while they are widely available.  Garruk and Xenagos can be trusted to take care of themselves with tokens relatively easily, and Marath can protect them from ground assault or keep small fliers from disrupting their loyalty.  Neither of these are essential to combo off of Doubling Season, so I don’t mind losing them if they die early.

Package Two: Defense

The deck has fifteen cards I would classify as “defensive.” All are interactive, potentially aggressive, but none are passive.  You will not find Ghostly Prisons in this deck!  First we have some hard removal in the form of Vandalblast, Beast Within, Terastodon, Scavenging Ooze, and Aura Shards.  The soft removal of Admonition Angel and Song of the Dryads is next, and is present to help against indestructible permanents and pillowfort walls. Finally we have some mass creature removal in the form of Elspeth, Sun’s Champion and Ezuri’s Predation.

Flying creatures have invaded my metagame lately, so I’ve added a bit more protection against them with Arachnogenesis, Hornet Queen, Emeria Angel, and Spike Weaver.  Dragonlord Dromoka and Trostani help keep my life total intact if I become the archenemy, and provide some insurance that I don’t fall victim to Mirror Match or Illusionist’s Gambit shenanigans if I attempt to alpha-strike a blue player.

Package Three: Creature Enhancement

This nine-card package focuses on providing the haste (Hammer of Purphoros, Ogre Battledriver, Urabrask the Hidden), evasion and/or pump (Champion of Lambholdt, Vigor, Craterhoof Behemoth, Thunderfoot Baloth, Eldrazi Monument) needed to close out long games.  They are skewed toward the end of the mana curve because I want to play them in the late stage of the game, when breaking a stalemate becomes necessary.  Cathars’ Crusade is likely the MVP of the deck because of its broken interaction with Marath, which produces a staircase of increasingly larger tokens provided that you have mana (and dice) to invest in this endeavor.

Package Four: Offense

These five creatures are purely offensive and have one function: Produce tokens and attack.  Decks do not need many of these, and one is often enough to finish an unprepared or weakened opponent.  It’s best to wait until there is a haste enabler on the field so that these can be cast as a surprise, because leaving a Rith or a Nacatl War Pride on the board floating in the wind can be a pretty threatening prospect for your opponents to stare at, and they may suddenly shift their collective focus toward you.  Omnath Locus of Rage is a different story, because he makes wiping your board full of elementals (we have five elemental creatures, three of which make token elementals) a difficult prospect, and occasionally results in a surprise victory.  Perhaps I should commission an altered-art vault-tec insurance salesman Omnath…complete with a fedora and briefcase!

Package Five: Recursion and Utility

This deck seldom uses the graveyard, but it is always helpful to have a way to bring back lands (Titania, Protector of Argoth, and Crucible of Worlds).  Greenwarden of Murasa is costlier than Eternal Witness, but the potential for two cards, its elemental synergy with Omnath, and the larger attacking body made him a better inclusion for this particular deck.  Emeria Shepherd can really win attrition wars, and can speed recovery in a hurry after a board wipe.  I always try to save Plains for last when utilizing fetch lands for this reason.  Evolutionary leap is another great piece of insurance: paying a green to draw the next creature card in my deck plays really well in a format where creatures are fragile, and can let me overextend a bit without fear.

On the utility side, Top, Skullclamp, and Enlightened Tutor are self-explanatory.  Chord can find a few great tricks like Spike Weaver, Scavenging Ooze, Titania for a landfall trigger, or Omnath in response to a board wipe.  It’s also used to find Purphoros, a haste enabler, or Terastodon.  It’s a very versatile card that makes it worth knowing the mana costs of creatures in the deck.  Bow of Nylea is our Swiss Army Knife.  The modes may make it seem kind of dull, but the fact that this card does FIVE things makes it worth looking twice at.  The most relevant part of the card is the provision of deathtouch to attackers, second would be the graveyard recursion in response to drawing from an empty library against a mill deck.  It can also put an extra fog charge on your Spike Weaver, kill a Bird of Paradise, or at worst, gain you a few life.

Package Six: Value

This 6-card package represents our Planeswalkers and our combo pieces.  Doubling Season and Primal Vigor are outstanding in this list, and two additional Planeswalkers are ready to take advantage of Doubling Season’s synergy with their ultimate abilities.  Domri Rade was originally in the deck, but his abilities were comparatively lackluster, and ultimate was powerful, yet even out of Doubling Season’s reach.  Sarkhan Vol is on-plan with his +1 haste and pump, but please take notice of how powerful his -2 ability is.  This has broken several unbreakable board states, including those ruled by opposing Avacyns, Platinum Empyrions, and Eldrazis!  Garruk Primal hunter is generally a 5-mana draw spell, while Purphoros and Marath can team up to make short work of any table.  Storm Herd is like the scene in Lord of the Rings where Mt. Doom is erupting, about to swallow everyone, and *spoiler alert* the Eagles come and save them from what should have been a horrific end…It just steals games.

Synergy Analysis

The chart below shows the synergy analysis between our two themes: Landfall and Tokens.  While only 7% of the deck has “complete synergy,” I’m still very happy with how the two halves of the deck work together.  Notice the cards that were not highlighted.  Most of them were lands and mana producers (they have synergy with everything in the deck that they produce mana for) and removal and defensive mechanics (because they’re meant to have synergy with the opponent’s deck!).  Greenwarden’s regrowth effect and Top’s library manipulation also synergize with just about everything.

Mana Base Notes:

This shouldn’t be too tricky to understand if you’ve read my articles about constructing a manabase for EDH, Part 1 and Part 2.  I played tiers 1 and 2 (Duals and Shocks) as well as 8 of 9 possible fetches.  I replaced Scalding Tarn with Krosan Verge because it fetches more relevant lands.  Bloodstained Mire may also get shaved for an additional plains, or a R/W TangoLand if it is released in Oath of the Gatewatch. I chose to skip Checklands and Painlands in favor of Shadowmoor Cycle filterlands, because they net 4 mana when tapped with Mana Reflection in play.  The deck contains 68 Green mana symbols, 16 Red mana Symbols, and 22 White mana symbols, which led me to the ratio of 7 Forests, 1 Mountain, 2 Plains for my basic lands.  I cut 2 forests: one for Gaea’s Cradle, and another for Dryad Arbor, since it can provide me with a Cathar’s Crusade trigger if needed.  Kessig Wolf Run, Strip Mine, Yavimaya Hollow, and Horizon Canopy are tricks I can utilize with Knight of the Reliquary to kill opponents, kill lands, save creatures, or set up a way to draw a card before I cast Enlightened Tutor.  Hopefully it is exceedingly clear how important basic lands are to EDH decks, especially when they provide access to the best spell-based ramp in Kodama’s Reach and Cultivate, as well as Sword of the Animist, Keep in mind that Nonbasic hate cards are always a possibility.  I’ve often considered how much devastation I could reap with Wave of Vitriol and landfall triggers if I scaled back on my nonbasics (perhaps in constructing a “budget friendly” version of this deck)

Review

Hopefully this article provided insight into how I build decks, and provided fuel for some of your own ideas.

Remember that:

  • All decks can be seen as a collection of packages.
  • Packages are cards that provide a similar function.
  • Cards that have potential to work positively with one another have synergy.
  • Well-executed synergy across a theme can lead to a successfully executed game plan.
  • Look for: Cards that have multiple uses or applications in a number of situations
  • Avoid: Cards that have singular uses or are extremely situational.  (I’m planning an article on “what to avoid” in deckbuilding and gameplay next year!)
