Introduction to Cube Drafting

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath
 

Cube drafting is one of the most unique experiences available in Magic. It is a mixture that combines drafting skills with a power level normally seen in Constructed.  Today, I’m going to go over some of the basic strategies for cube drafting.

Hold up. What is a cube?

I’m glad you asked that, hypothetical straw man. A cube is a collection of Magic cards that has been assembled to draft with. Usually, a cube is between 360 and 720 cards with no duplicates. Players make three piles of 15 cards, and then draft them as if they were sealed packs.

There are countless ways to construct a cube. Anyone designing a cube can include themes, unusual restrictions, and anything else he or she can think of. Cube is not a sanctioned format; there are no tournaments where you can go to cube draft (normally). The only goal is for a group of people to get together and have a good time.

The most common type of cube is a generic good-stuff cube. Most of the best cards throughout Magic’s history are combined for this, with a few exceptions. Did you really enjoy playing with Pack Rat a couple years ago? Maybe you miss the days where you were attacking with Hero of Bladehold. Have fond memories of Fact or Fiction (EOTFOFYL) or Plow Under from your early years? These are all common inclusions in this type of cube.

A power cube, as indicated by the name, includes the power 9. These are some of the most iconic cards in Magic and powerful enough to be excluded from most generic cubes (hence the name power cube). The term power cube covers more than just the power 9, normally including several other artifacts that can quickly generate large amounts of mana (Sol Ring, Mana Crypt, Mana Vault, etc…). These cards are often a step above most other cards in power level, and good arguments are needed for passing these cards in a draft. However, they create some of the most memorable games.

Pauper cubes are a step down from a generic good-stuff cube. The restriction here is based on the rarity of cards. Rare and Mythic cards are not allowed in pauper cubes. Usually, this results in a cube that is less focused on powerful bombs, and more focused on commons and uncommons that generate value. This can be a great option for cube builders that really enjoy that kind of interaction and it can be easier to construct if money is a concern.

The good-stuff cube, power cube, and pauper cube are probably the most common types of cube. However, the limits of cube are really only curbed by a person’s creativity. Magic Online recently introduced the legendary cube, where every creature was legendary. I’ve seen cubes where nothing has a converted mana cost higher than one. Players have built cubes consisting of cards from a single set. I’ve even heard of a cube that only had green cards.

Ok, back to basic cube strategy.

That was an awful lot of words to cover what can be in a cube. The real lesson behind that is to make sure to ask a few questions before sitting down to draft a cube. “What kind of cube is this?” or “Is there anything I should know about this particular cube?” should cover the basics and get the cube owner talking about anything special. Mostly, you don’t want to get blindsided by your first pack of magic cards looking really strange. You don’t need to read over the whole cube list or anything, just make sure you know about things like that particular cube being mono green.

Before I go any further, I need to make a disclaimer. Because cubes vary so much, everything I go over should only be considered a rough guide for drafting. It’s impossible to discuss every type of cube, and it would mostly be a waste of time anyways. From now on, I’m going to focus on generic good-stuff cubes and briefly touch on power cubes. Those are the most common ways for people to get introduced to cube, and they happen to be the cubes normally available on Magic Online.

 

Have a theme

This is probably the single most important point when drafting a cube. Most cubes consist of powerful cards, so drafting a pile of good cards isn’t hard or challenging (just pick the cards with the right mana symbols). It also means your deck isn’t going to be very good. The best cube decks are focused on a specific plan, and cards are drafted with that plan in mind. Some of the most common themes are red aggressive decks, blue based control decks, and green ramp decks. Those are found in pretty much every cube I’ve ever seen.

Trust that cards like Tendrils of Agony and Metalworker are in a cube for a reason. If you see them early, you can assume they are properly supported in the cube and draft accordingly. These are red flags indicating that certain archetypes are supported in that cube. You won’t find Deceiver Exarch in a cube that doesn’t contain Splinter Twin or Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker.

Obstinate Baloth and Thrun, the Last Troll are my favorite examples of drafting without a plan. Something has likely gone very wrong if those cards have made your starting deck. Most green decks are ramp based or built around certain cards (like Survival of the Fittest, Opposition, Edric, Spymaster of Trest). Obstinate Baloth and Thrun, the Last Troll don’t add anything to those decks, existing as sideboard cards or really bad filler cards for your deck.

 

Draft lands

Drafting a cube is not the same as drafting regular packs of magic cards. You don’t have a handful of Demolishes or Kitesail Scouts filling out the last five or six cards in a pack, all of the cards are good. Like I mentioned above, drafting a cube has some parallels with constructed level magic. Most constructed decks aren’t filled with basic lands, they play as much mana fixing as they can get. Likewise, if your cube deck is 9 Islands and 9 Forests, you probably did something wrong.

Fetch lands are the most powerful color fixing lands in cube. They offer a wide variety of colors when combined with dual lands or shock lands, provide a shuffle effect for cards like Brainstorm or Sylvan Library, and can fill a graveyard for mechanics like Delve. Dual lands and shock lands are the next best, due to their synergy with fetch lands. There are also some corner case synergies, such as the land type Forest mattering for Rofellos, Llanowar Emissary. Other lands are a step down in power level, but still sufficient for decks interested in multiple colors.

While multicolor decks should be focusing on color fixing lands, single color decks should keep an eye out for utility lands. Mono red aggro would much rather have a Rishadan Port than some random filler card. Be sure to keep an eye out for these if your deck looks like it can support them.

 

Draft cheap cards

Every card in cube is a good card. Finding enough good cards isn’t going to be a problem. Filling out your blue deck with cards like Ponder, Impulse, and Miscalculation gives you ways to deploy cards in the early turns. If you are trying to decide if you should improve your finisher from Frost Titan to Aetherling or pick up an Impulse, the Impulseis the correct pick. Wasting mana in the first few turns of a game is a great way to die before casting all of your sweet spells.

 

Signaling is overrated

If you’ve done a lot of regular drafting, you probably understand the concept of signaling. For anyone that hasn’t done a lot of drafting, the concept is fairly simple. If you pass a bunch of good white cards, you are sending a signal to the person next to you that white is open to draft. If a bunch of good blue cards come to you, the person next to you is sending a signal that blue is open.

In cube drafting, that concept is far less useful. You could see a third pick Control Magic and infer that blue is open. Unfortunately, the two players before you took Jace, the Mind Sculptor and Treachery, and you aren’t every going to see another good blue card. Or maybe white is completely open in a draft, but the first several packs just don’t have any white cards in them. You can incorrectly deduce that white is being heavily drafted and try to change colors. Most packs of Magic cards are printed so that the cards inside are spread roughly even through the different colors. There is no process like that for cubes, where the only randomization happens by players shuffling stacks of cards before making packs.

 

The *best* cards

I can’t really make a pick order for cube, since there isn’t a consistent list. The idea of a pick order is fairly useful for a beginning cube player that doesn’t know most of the cards. Like I mentioned earlier, having a theme for your deck is important, and that should guide your draft picks. However these are most of the cards I’d be hard pressed to pass from my first pack:

Jace, the Mind Sculptor – Planeswalkers are fantastic in cube, and this is the best of the lot. Board control, card draw, and victory condition all packed into a four mana card.

Treachery – This is a zero mana mind control. It’s really hard for this to be a bad card

Upheaval – This card combines with mana acceleration to do some truly unfair things.

Recurring Nightmare – This is a build around card, but the result is worth the effort.

Opposition – See Recurring Nightmare

Coalition Relic – This is the best mana rock (artifact ramp) available in a non powered cube.

Karn Liberated – This is the right colors for any deck, and cures what ails you. You should play this in roughly anything not running Goblin Guide.

Ugin, the Spirit Dragon – See Karn Liberated, but Ugin can solve lots of problems at once.

Griselbrand – This is the best fatty to Show and Tell, Reanimate, or otherwise cheat into play.

Umezawa’s Jitte – This is the best equipment against opponents playing creatures. That should be almost all of your opponents.

Sword of X and Y cycle (Sword of Fire and Ice, Sword of Feast and Famine, etc…) – These all get out of hand quickly. The mill trigger on Sword of Body and Mind is particularly busted against draft decks.

Sulfuric Vortex – This card is usually my pull into mono red. This is my signal that mono red is an open archetype in a draft.

Demonic Tutor – This is the gold standard for tutor effects. I don’t think I’d take Demonic Tutor over any of the cards above, but it is the best way to add a second copy of those cards to your deck.

 

The *best* cards in a power cube

Power cube adds a few more cards that almost universally top any of the cards I just listed.

Just don’t pass these: Sol Ring, Black Lotus, Ancestral Recall, Time Walk, Mox Emerald, Mox Ruby, Mox Jet, Mox Sapphire, Mox Pearl, Mana Crypt, Mana Vault, Time Vault – These are the best cards in Magic, and really shouldn’t be passed under any circumstances.  They warp the game in extreme ways.

Mind Twist – Hitting an opponent with this early usually obliterates any plans they had for the next several turns of the game.  This also says random, so the opponent won’t be picking the cards to keep (if any).

Skull Clamp – This draws an obscene number of cards. Particularly good in red aggro or green ramp with mana creatures

Library of Alexandria – This is a colorless land that just happens to win you the game if you play it on the first turn. This just generates an incredible number of cards when it is active.

Mana Drain – This takes all the power of a counterspell and tacks some free mana onto it. Saying No doesn’t feel much better than this.

 

Time Vault was actually removed from the Holiday cube on Magic Online because it was too powerful.  If you are trying to figure out the best card in a normal, non-powered cube, it is probably Jace, the Mind Sculptor.  The best card in powered cube is Time Vault (if it is present and supported).  Otherwise, the best card is Sol Ring.

 

Wrapping up and final advice

I’ve pretty much covered the important concepts for beginning a cube draft. There is one last bit of advice I have for someone starting cube, and it’s probably the most important bit. Make sure to have fun. Sulfuric Vortex might be the best card in a pack, but send it along if you really dislike drafting red aggro. Cubes with people at home are rarely for any prize, so it’s all about bragging rights and having fun. Magic Online cubes cost a small amount of money, but it’s really nothing in the grand scheme of things.

If you are looking for a chance to cube draft, the Vintage cube is available on Magic Online right now. This cube is only available around Christmas, and it has all of the most busted cards available. Magic Online has a Legacy cube (mostly a good-stuff cube) available right around every new set release. If you want to take a crack at cube drafting in real life, ask around your local shop. Someone probably has one. I usually have one together if you happen to know me.

Good luck! May all your cubing experiences be a blast!
