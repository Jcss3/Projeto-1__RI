Exploring Frontier

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath
 

What is Frontier?

Frontier is a new, community driven Magic format that currently includes the following sets:

Magic 2015
Khans of Tarkir
Fate Reforged
Dragons of Tarkir
Magic Origins
Battle for Zendikar
Oath of the Gatewatch
Shadows over Innistrad
Eldritch Moon
Kaladesh

Frontier is a non-rotating format, so sets Magic 2015 will always be the starting point.  Aether Revolt will be legal in a few weeks, and new sets will be included as they are released.

 

Should I care about Frontier?

That question is really up to you, but I’ll quickly cover the positives and negatives.

Frontier is essentially a tweaked version of Modern.  Modern, and originally Overextended, were created as an alternative, non-rotating format to Legacy that would not have to deal with the reserved list problem.

Frontier is cheaper than Modern.  Even with the increased rate of reprinted cards, Modern staples remain expensive.  Frontier only covers the last few years of cards.  The magic playing population has not dramatically changed in the last few years, so scarcity of cards will be a much smaller concern.

Frontier does not have a ban list.  The banning process in Modern remains vague and difficult to predict.  The loss of Birthing Pod and Splinter Twin hurt many invested players, and Wizards of the Coast does not make it easy to predict what changes the future will hold

Following up with the lack of a ban list, Frontier consists of Magic sets with a uniform design philosophy.  There are no Urza’s Towers or Blood Moons lurking in the Frontier card pool.

Standard decks from the last few years are not looked upon in the most favorable light, and Frontier is currently populated with a bunch of these decks with minor tweaks.  Collected Company, Rally the Ancestors, and Siege Rhino are prevalent throughout the format.

Frontier has a much less defined place in Magic than Modern has.  Modern was founded on the desire for a non-rotating format that could avoid the reserved list.  Frontier exists because of things players do not like about Modern.  Wizards of the Coast is far more likely to change any number of those factors over abolishing the reserved list.

 

What can I play in Frontier?

If you are looking for Frontier decks, you have come to the right place.  There haven’t been a lot of Frontier events, but I have dug around looking for a good mix of decks to start with.  A lot of these are unrefined, and I try to make note of potential problems when I see them.  That said, there is a lot to look at here.  There are plenty of things that are not going to be optimized.

I only included sideboards for some of the decks.  Some simply didn’t come with sideboards.  Others included sideboards that looked heinously bad.  The ones that I did include either looked serviceable, or they included cards that people might forget are legal in Frontier.  Jorubai Murk Lurker was certainly not a card on my initial list of sideboard options.

With those disclaimers out of the way, lets take a look at Frontier.

Khans of Tarkir’s Standard aggressive deck is back for another round.  Updates to this deck are minimal, as most of the best red aggressive cards were all legal at the same time.  Foundry Street Denezin, Monastery Swiftspear, and Goblin tokens should carve out a big enough chunk of life that Stoke the Flames, Atarka’s Command, and other miscellaneous burn can finish off the opponent.

The only innovation here is adding Smuggler’s Copter, which is a common theme for the all the decks in this section.  Alternatively, you could remove Smuggler’s Copter for the Become Immense/Temur Battle Rage combo.  Temur Battle Rage and Smuggler’s Copter fight for a limited number of non-creature, non-interaction deck slots, making it practically impossible to run both.

There is room for a lot of minor customization in this deck.  Wild Slash, Fiery Impulse, Lightning Strike, and Incendiary Flow are all reasonable burn options.  Zurgo Bellstriker, Falkenrath Gorger, and Lightning Beserker could up the count for one drop creatures.  Abbot of Keral Keep and Goblin Rabblemaster can provide some gas to keep the pressure up.  Become Immense/Temur Battle Rage gives the deck a combo finish.  The numbers in this deck are still fluid, as players haven’t put the hours into MTGO to find an optimal build.

Fun Fact: Wizards of the Coast was concerned about the power level of Atarka’s Command, so red cards suffered for the next few sets.

This is a deck that goes beyond an old Standard deck and strives to be something new.  Ensoul Artifact and Shrapnel Blast were strong enough to hold together a Standard deck filled with draft commons.  Kaladesh has a surplus of aggressive cards focusing on artifacts including Toolcraft Exemplar, Smuggler’s Copter, Scrapheap Scrounger, Inventor’s Apprentice, and Bomat Courier.

There are a few choices in this particular list that make me a little nervous.  There is zero interaction with the opponent beyond Shrapnel Blast, and that comes with a steep cost.  Built to Smash is probably worse than another creature or a burn spell.  Otherwise, I like the look of this list.

This is a decklist that won one of the first Frontier tournaments.  Clearly the pilot had some targets in mind, considering the full set of Hushwing Gryff, Anafenza, the Foremost, and a complete lack of Siege Rhino.  Those aren’t decisions that one makes lightly.  Those are all choices that specifically target Rally the Ancestors, a deck that will feature prominently in the early Frontier metagame.  As a whole, the deck features a good assortment of disruptive elements and a solid curve of threats.  I would like to see some copies of Gideon, Ally of Zendikar in the list to punish slower decks, but that is really a sideboard change.

Overall, this is a deck designed to combat a metagame filled with Rally the Ancestors.  If you don’t want to target that deck, simply remove some of the Gryffs for Rhinos or Gideons.

There was a fringe Standard prowess deck, and the Frontier card pool helped out in a significant way.  The prowess deck suffered from a lack of premium threats, and it would usually stumble if the first creature died.  Thermo-Alchemist and Thing in the Ice bump the threat count up high enough that you can be selective instead of playing everything available and winding up a threat or two short of a solid decklist.

The list I found is not particularly refined.  The spell choices are not optimized, and I’m actually offended that there aren’t four copies of Treasure Cruise.  This is one of the best Delve decks available, and it should probably be reaching for Dig Through Time for additional copies.

I can’t remember if this deck ever splashed green for the Become Immense/Temur Battle Rage plan, but I could certainly see it as an option.  At a minimum, I would expect a copy or two of Titan’s Strength.  The current list looks like it could fizzle out before dealing 20 damage a little more than I would like.

Thalia’s Lieutenant alone warrants a consideration for a Humans tribal deck.  This list is a pretty stock Collected Company list with blue for Jace, Vryn’s Prodigy, Reflector Mage and some counters out of the sideboard.  Abzan Falconer works well with Thalia’s Lieutenant to give your entire squad flying.  Thalia, Heretic Cathar can punish slower creature decks and helps mitigate a Rally the Ancestors fueled block.

There are a few other ways to push a Humans deck.  Chord of Calling can effectively add more copies of Thalia’s Lieutenant to your deck if you are already going down the Collected Company path.  It also gives options for a more toolbox sideboard if you want to add some silver bullets like Hushwing Gryff.

If that’s not your style, Kytheon, Hero of Akros and friends are happy to fuel a hyper aggressive mono white deck.  Obelisk of Urd and Always Watching give that strategy some additional gas.  Flooded Strand, Windswept Heath, Prairie Stream, and Knight of the White Orchid probably give you a free splash for Reflector Mage too.

One of our current Standard’s boogeymen has made its way to the Frontier.  Energy is a pretty parasitic mechanic, meaning it really only works with a very restricted set of cards.  That leaves a very small amount of room for innovation.

There are some things that can be optimized, and the litteral biggest one is Ugin, the Spirit Dragon.  Aetherworks Marvel has been cheating out Emrakul, the Promised End and a host of lesser targets in Standard for a while now, but Ugin, the Spirit Dragon was the payoff for ramp decks of old.  Eight strong targets make it difficult to whiff on an Aetherworks Marvel trigger, and both payoff cards are capable of winning a game on the spot.

After that modification, there isn’t room for much else.  Fetch lands add some consistency to the mana, and they can add a few extra triggers to the energy production of Aetherworks Marvel.  They also allow a fairly painless black splash.  Live Fast isn’t common in Standard, but it is a welcome addition for a deck that can support it.  Glint-Nest Crane might make for a better splash.

The Hornet Queen is probably a little overkill in this list, but it seems like a great sideboard card for Siege Rhino decks.

Now we start moving into the realm of the truly bizarre combo decks.  Dromoka’s Command kept Sphinx’s Tutelage from seeing any serious play in Standard, but Frontier is a little more wild and unrefined.
As with several of the lists that I have found, the numbers here are pretty rough.  A 3/2 split on Cathartic Reunion and Tormenting Voice is almost assuredly wrong.  Dig Through Time seems great and Fateful Showdown seems more cute than good.  Collective Defiance seems like it might not be necessary, but it can function as a bad removal spell and a boost for Tutelage.  I have no idea how someone determines two copies of Fevered Visions to be correct, but maybe the pilot knows something that I don’t.

I would really appreciate some number of counters in this deck, since Rally the Ancestors loves to get milled and can win at instant speed.  Dispel seems great, but there are plenty of three mana options available too.  One or two copies of Torrential Gearhulk wouldn’t seem out of place either.

Despite all the changes that I’ve talked about with this deck, I do think there is something here.  I like the core concept.

One of the first things I wanted to explore in Frontier is Jeskai Ascendancy.  It feels like the kind of combo deck that could be forgotten with Rally the Ancestors around.  Plus, there are a bunch of new cheap cantrips to explore with the deck.  I didn’t find a successful decklist for Jeskai Ascendancy, but I dug around Reddit long enough to find someone else working on it.

That designer moved towards a Cryptolith Rite deck fueled with tokens, which made sense when I remembered that Sylvan Caryatid wasn’t legal in Frontier.  This list just uses Jeskai Ascendancy as a gigantic pump spell, and Cryptolith Rite can let you explode around the fourth turn.

This particular list should definitely switch the numbers on Cathartic Reunion and Tormenting Voice.  Expedite and Take Inventory are the two cards I’d really like to see added to the deck, but I’m not sure if they were tested and discarded before the designer made the Reddit post.

I saved the best for last; this combo deck looks great.  Last Standard season had a fringe prison deck, but the Frontier additions are great.  Dig Through Time is a huge bonus for finding copies of Part the Waterveil or whatever answer you are missing.  Aetherspouts is an incredibly powerful effect that was lacking a home in most traditional control decks, and it is a heinously difficult card to play around.  Torrential Gearhulk is better than Snapcaster Mage in this deck, rebuying most of the spells in the deck and serving as a real win condition.  It can even get value by bouncing itself with Engulf the Shore.  Lastly, Metallurgic Summonings out of the sideboard gives another win condition that is difficult to interact with.

This version of Abzan trades in Anafenza, the Foremost and an aggressive curve for a suite of stronger spells and more late game potential.  This particular version seems a little weak to any deck with Atarka’s Command, but it should go over the top of most other Siege Rhino decks.

I’ve probably already mentioned this, but I don’t like Tireless Tracker in Frontier.  Jace, Vryn’s Prodigy is a premier kill-on-sight target, and removal spells will be chosen with him in mind.  Playing a three drop, really a four drop, that dies to all the same removal seems unnecessarily risky.  It is a good sideboard card against midrange and control decks, but there will be too many copies of Wild Slash and Fiery Impulse to put Tireless Tracker in your starting 60 cards.

Liliana, the Last Hope seems like an egregious omission in this deck.  Honestly, this deck should probably take a cue from the current Standard format and pursue a Delirium theme.  Emrakul, the Promised End can go over the top of almost anything this deck does, and Ishkanah, Grafwidow seems like another strong body for grindy games.  Once you pursue Delirium, Traverse the Ulvenwald is one of the best tutors in Frontier, and it allows some silver bullets like Noxious Gearhulk.  Or you can just use it to make a stream of Goblin Dark-Dwellers.

This seems like a pretty subpar midrange deck glued together by the strength of Thunderbreak Regent.  Crackling Doom is the only removal spell that really kills it without triggering it, and this deck will flourish or flounder depending on the popularity of that spell.

I do like this deck more than the Mardu/Abzan hybrid above.  The aggressive curve makes it possible to race Emrakul, the Promised End decks.

This list definitely needs some tuning.  A singleton Transgress the Mind and Nahiri, the Harbinger seem more like experiments than the results of testing.  I’d like to see some cheaper spells in the mix.  The more this deck pushes into midrange, the less I like it.

This list is a little scatterbrained, but I like that it covers a wide variety of options.  Blue adds a few good options for a Delirium strategy, and the extra color doesn’t cost much with fetch lands.  I really want to see Liliana, the Last Hope and Emrakul, the Promised End in here, but there are a lot of good supporting cards in this list.

If the mana stretches far enough for double blue spells, I like the options that Torrential Gearhulk and Dig Through Time present.  Otherwise, Dragonlord Silumgar, Sultai Charm, Jace, Vryn’s Prodigy, and Sidisi, Brood Tyrant are all worth some consideration.  Grim Flayer, Garruk, Apex Predator, Grapple with the Past, Vessel of Nascency, Noxious Gearhulk, and Gilt-Leaf Winnower could all make their way into the deck too.
If you want the biggest selection of spells to play with, this is probably a good starting point.
