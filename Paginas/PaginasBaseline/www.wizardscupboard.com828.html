Improving in Draft

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath
 

I’m going to take a break from constructed this month to discuss some common mistakes I see in draft. I don’t claim to be an expert in limited, but I’ve done enough of them to have some knowledge worth sharing. If you don’t get the chance to draft, I’ll have a small section down at the bottom where I briefly discuss a Commander deck I’m building.  Feel free to scroll down to the bottom to check that out.

One thing I want to mention before we get started: I use examples to support some of my points today. I don’t mention anyone by name, and I honestly can’t even remember who everyone was.  I do want to be clear that I’m not using these stories to hurt feelings. I just felt that they added something to the points I’m trying to make.

 

Understand Power Levels

Not all Magic Cards are created equal. Some of them have power levels over 9000. Other cards are only useful for starting fires and filling in all the little nooks and crannies around your desk/backpack/house. Unsurprisingly, most cards fall somewhere in between these.

At the beginning of a new draft format, people need to try a bunch of the cards to see what works. The black-green sacrifice deck in triple Battle for Zendikar was not a particularly successful archetype. However, that wasn’t apparent until several people tried it. Usually, pro players share their knowledge of the format after the Pro Tour has finished. I recommend reading as much as possible, but Frank Karsten usually writes an article on his team’s pick order. If you don’t have time for anything else, be sure to skim that list.

Any list of pick orders won’t be perfect. Nobody can completely dissect a format in just a few weeks. However, it is a great way to get a general idea of what the best cards are. This knowledge should prevent you from passing superior cards like Complete Disregard for mediocre ones like Bone Splinters.

Frank Karsten Pick Order – Battle for Zendikar

Frank Karsten Pick Order – Oath of the Gatewatch

This knowledge is the most valuable at the beginning of a draft, where cards are picked based solely on power level. You won’t get a chance to play with all of the rares in a given set, so knowing that a bunch of pros think the one you just got is awful can help save your draft.

If you enjoy going to prerelease events, I would also suggest listening to the podcast Limited Resources or looking at any of several set reviews prior to the event. Those are rough predictions, but they can help prevent you from playing with cards that have a long precedent of being bad.

 

Sideboard in Draft

I’m not going to go very deep on this subject; countless people have already written about it before. Try searching any popular Magic website if you are looking for more information.

The cards that do not go into your draft deck are not trash. It shouldn’t be a few full art basic lands, maybe a couple rares, and bunk commons and uncommons you never look at again. You should be looking at that pile of cards between each game. In all likelihood, you have at least a few cards in your color that didn’t make your deck. Maybe you had a few too many three cost cards, so you cut a few of them. Maybe you had a few more combat tricks than you really needed, so the worst of the bunch are sitting on the bench for game one. When your opponent casts a Boiling Earth and a Sparkmage’s Gambit in game one, you should really be looking to remove as many X/1 creatures as you can to invalidate those cards. Maybe your opponent has a bunch of Searing Lights, and you’ve got some replacement creatures that have more than two power. These are some easy switches you can make to improve the rest of your match.

 

 

 

 

 

 

Even if you can’t see something that makes you want to change your deck, just consider the colors your opponent is playing. Is it a blue-red deck? Jwar Isle Avenger is a good payoff card in that deck, and several of the good blue creatures fly.  Maybe you should consider that Plummet or Tears of Valakut sitting in your sideboard.

 

Counter Drafting (Hate Drafting)

I can no longer count the number of times I’ve heard of players taking a card simply to avoid playing against it. I can understand the thought process that leads to this and I can sympathize; playing against really strong cards can be a disheartening experience. However, taking a card simply to prevent someone else from having it is a horrendous plan.

Story time

 

The real inspiration for this article came from a draft last week, where a player passed me an Omnath, Locus of Rage in the third pack. He was firmly set in a blue-black deck, and clearly had no use for it. I happened to be a red-green deck, and happily scooped it up. During deck construction, nobody had moved from their seat, and the player who opened Omnath commented on how he wished he had opened something more useful for him. The two players on my other side immediately responded that he should have taken the Omnath. Comments like “It’s unbeatable in draft” and “You don’t want to play against that thing” flowed for a few minutes, and I felt a little out of place arguing with them since I clearly benefitted from the decision. Now that I’m removed from the scenario and not arguing with people negatively affected, I have more to say on the subject.

Counter drafting (hate drafting) a powerful card simply to prevent someone else from having it has two effects. You are going to make someone else’s deck worse. I’ll get into that more in a second, but that does nothing to directly benefit your deck. It actually makes your deck worse, since you likely passed a playable card for your deck when you counter drafted a different card.

A fresh pack is the best time to scoop up great cards for your deck. Premium removal spells and exceptional creatures will quickly be snapped up by other players, so the first few picks of each pack are the best chance to significantly increase the power of your deck. Wasting one of those picks on a counter draft is awful, even if you are only getting an average quality card for your own deck.

I might sound like a broken record at this point, but counter drafting gets even worse for you. Your one pick has made two people’s decks worse (you and one other person). The other six people are completely unaffected by your choice. That counter draft pick you made is a complete waste for your deck. It is simply reducing the number of cards in your pool that can go into your deck by one. The person you counter drafted from is not similarly affected. They don’t get a powerful card, but they still get the chance to take something out of the pack that they can use. So, that leaves your counter draft with you down a full card, one person down part of a card, and everyone else unaffected. You are clearly the most affected player at the table by your counter draft. Your deck will suffer the most from your choice.

Now that I’ve typed a ton of words beating this dead horse, I’d like to take a minute to try to sooth your disappointment over passing absurd cards to opponents. The person that takes it is one of seven other people at the table. Since you won’t be playing more than three other people in the draft, you’ve already got more than a 50% chance of never playing against the deck with that card. If you do get paired against the opponent with that card, they only see about a third of their deck in any given game. That Omnath I mentioned in the story? I cast it one time in seven games. It did win me the game, but it was game three and my opponent had used his removal before knowing I even had the Omanth. One last point for passing the rare; you know the card exists. Even if the opponent doesn’t draw it, you can play in a way to minimize its impact on the game. You can save a removal spell if it’s a Kalitas, Traitor of Ghet or work to minimize the impact of a Planar Outburst.

 

Stages of a Draft

You should have different goals depending on what part of the draft you are in.  Your priorities should shift throughout the draft to ensure that you end up with a cohesive deck.

This is a concept that was really clarified in a recent Limited Resources podcast, but I’d like to summarize it here. This framework should give you a general idea of what you should be doing during each segment of the draft. Each draft is different, so this should not be taken as doctrine, but it is a good place to start.

Pack 1 – Pick 1

Take the best card. There isn’t really an excuse to do anything else here

Pack 1 – Pick 2

Take the best card. This should be the case 99% of the time, regardless of the color of your first pick. You should only consider the color of your first card if the second best card in this pack matches and the power level is only slightly below the best card in this pack. When in doubt, just take the best card.

Pack 1 – Pick 3 to roughly Pick 7

At this point, it’s time to start looking for the good cards that are getting passed to you. Signals aren’t really a consideration before this, since they are almost impossible to read. Some packs are just naturally strong or weak. Now you should be looking for the above average cards that can signal a color is open. Ondu War Cleric, Blinding Drone, Saddleback Lagac, and Boulder Salvo are good examples of these kind of cards. Some decent uncommon cards should still be floating around too, so keep an eye out for those.

Do not pigeonhole yourself into colors based on the first couple cards you are passed. If you open a Deepfathom Skulker and get passed a Mindmelter, don’t just lock into blue-black and ignore everything else. If the only blue cards you see in the next three or four packs are Slip Through Space and Comparative Analysis, you are going to need to abandon blue.

Pack 1 – roughly Pick 7 onward

At this point the strong color signal should be mostly gone. You should hopefully have a decent idea of what is flowing in your direction, and you should try to pick up any playable cards that are available for the rest of the pack.

Pack 1 review

Power level is the name of the game for pack one. You should be looking for strong cards to pull you into a particular deck. This is the point in the draft where you have some room to be flexible. You can take a few speculative picks to see if a color is open. There will still be time to recover if it doesn’t work out.

Places you can be at this point are:

  • Roughly eight or nine decent cards in two colors. This is where a lot of decks should wind up after the first pack. A few picks will get wasted on rares in colors that weren’t open, speculative picks, and some trash picks at the end of the pack. This is a fine place to be.
  • Around eight decent cards in one color. This is pretty much the ideal scenario. You have clearly identified an open color, and you should get a bunch more playable cards in pack three. This also leaves you open to taking any bomb card you see in the beginning of pack two, instead of needing it to match with your deck.
  • Roughly six or seven playable cards in two colors. One of the most common scenarios that winds up here is a switch due to identifying an open color. It is reasonable to end up with three or four playable cards in three different colors. That isn’t a disaster. You should have a solid idea of what is open (hopefully you already switched into it), and it’s not too late to be determining the color of your deck.
  • It’s tough to end up with less than six playable cards between two colors.  If this happens, you really need to commit to something quickly.  The draft can still be salvaged, but you’ll really have to work at it.
Pack 2

Things get a little murky at this point, since each draft is different. There are fewer benchmarks at this point, and you start working on constructing an actual deck instead of simply taking powerful cards. You should start considering the curve of your deck, identifying that you might already have enough or too many three cost cards, or simply not have enough two mana cards. Powerful cards are still important here, don’t start passing great cards simply to pick up filler quality cards.  You should just start considering cohesion over raw power for close decisions.

You should decide on your colors by about pick three or four of this pack. If you wait longer than that, you simply won’t have enough cards to build a complete deck.

Pack 3

This is where you fill in the holes in your deck. Some common holes that you can identify are a lack of removal, a lack of finishers (creatures that can actually win a game), or a lack of cards at a certain converted mana cost (two and three cost cards are the most common concerns). This is the point in the draft where you sigh and take Demon’s Grasp over a good creature for your deck, simply because you need it. Your deck needs to function at the end of this pack, so this is your last chance to fix problems.

 

Miscellaneous Draft Notes

You should be able to look at the cards you have picked between each pack. Be sure to flip through them and get an idea of what you have. This is the best time to realize your deck is all four cost creatures or that you really could use something that can fit in the top end of your curve. Some stores won’t mind if you look at your cards between picks in the draft. This is more convenient, but make sure you don’t spend too much time consulting your cards. The draft will grind to a halt if this happens too often.

I referenced color pairs a lot when explaining draft. This is not always the case depending on which format you are preparing to play. In Khans of Tarkir draft, you wanted to identify a clan (three color combination) and stick with that. Limiting yourself to two colors restricted your access to some of the most powerful gold cards. Try to make sure you know about any set gimmicks like this before drafting a set, because it might prevent you from wrecking your first draft.

However, I’ve got some friends that are interested, and this looks like a spicy brew. I got the inspiration from this article, and worked on it until I liked it. Drawing cards is fun, and the deck should do a decent job of scaling in power based on my opponents.
