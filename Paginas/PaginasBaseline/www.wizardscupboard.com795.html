Modern Musings: Burn Primer Part 1

Hey guys, welcome to my first article writing for The Wizard’s Cupboard! My name is Justin Alderman and I’ve been in love with the Modern format since the beginning and have spent the last couple of years championing Modern Burn, both online and in paper. Today I’m going to share with you some of the things I’ve learned about the deck in order to help you build it and pilot it correctly.

Describing Burn’s game plan is relatively simple: deal 20 points of damage as fast as possible to your opponent before they can get their strategy going. Burn does this by playing the most efficient damage spells and haste creatures that the color Red has to offer. Since most strategies in the Modern format require the pilot to damage themselves to fix their manabase, playing Burn is a great metagame opportunity. Each point of damage your opponent deals to himself is one less life point you need to worry about. Resolve 6-7 Lightning Bolt effects (e.g., Lightning Bolt, Rift Bolt, Shard Volley, and Lava Spike) and your opponent is dead. No muss, no fuss. And if budget is a consideration, then Burn is not an overly expensive deck to assemble.

As simple as playing Burn sounds, the deck is very susceptible to narrow hate cards such as Leyline of Sanctity, Kor Firewalker, Dragon’s Claw, and Phyrexian Unlife. Devoted lifegain strategies, like Soul Sisters, and faster combo decks, such as Splinter Twin or a good draw from Amulet Bloom are also a thorn in Burn’s side. Over time, Burn has evolved to splash into other colors to fight these menacing “silver bullets” and bad matchups. The color White gives Burn added damage, flexibility and speed against most of the hate, while Green enables Burn to play one of its best sideboard cards, Destructive Revelry.

Since the addition of the White and Green splash, Burn has almost reached something resembling a stock list:

DECK CURVE

Burn thrives by being very aggressive. As such, it must cast multiple spells in a turn to win, burying the opponent in a flurry of spells. Burn has a low curve, playing more 1 cost than 2 cost spells. The sample deck listed above has a 24/16 split between the 1 and 2 cost spells. Land counts are also kept low in the 19-20 range. This low curve increases the chance of keeping more 7-6 card hands since mulligans can be very unforgiving. In Game 1 especially, each card is essential to winning the game before the opponent can stabilize. Postboard, Burn branches out into the 3 cost spells for metagame bullets like Molten Rain, Flames of the Blood Hand, and Volcanic Fallout. This changes the ratio of spells in the deck, but you also have the advantage of siding against whatever strategy you’re facing.

THE ROGUES GALLERY

While the core of the deck is primarily damage spells, the creature suite is incredibly important for the deck to operate. Having permanent sources of recurring damage is essential for Burn’s game plan to move smoothly. Each creature has its function and is pretty much irreplaceable. Here’s a brief rundown:

Goblin Guide – Your ideal opener includes 1 or more of this critter. A turn 1 Goblin Guide is essential to getting the ball rolling with the damage race. This 2/2 haste is so important that his drawback isn’t even one at all most of the time. In fact, use him to gain information on your opponent’s draws and adjust your game plan.

Monastery Swiftspear – You’ll notice a huge difference in games during which you open with a Goblin Guide and when you don’t. Swiftspear is essentially Goblin Guides 5-8, with the power to grow out of control when you’re casting multiple spells a turn.

Grim Lavamancer – This is your ultimate card advantage engine and a must-answer threat for creature-based decks. Utilizing your spent Burn spells and cracked fetchlands from your graveyard will provide you with the extra gas to close out a game or control the board.

Eidolon of the Great Revel – This little bear is the linchpin of the deck and is arguably the arbiter of Burn’s rise to success in the last year. He is ideally your 2 turn play, punishing your opponents whenever they cast a low cost spell. The goal is always to get ahead in life totals and then keep the lead by attacking with Eidolon and Company. Falling behind and getting locked out of the game is a legitimate risk you run for playing with the Fire Bear, but the benefits he provide definitely outweigh this drawback. From being able to push damage through a Leyline of Sanctity to single handedly shutting down Storm decks, there is absolutely no replacement for this card.

Kor Firewalker – Kor Firewalker is strictly a role player to fight the mirror, as it is essentially a Dragon’s Claw with legs that is almost impossible to remove. Some mirror matches are decided simply by who can land this guy on the battlefield first. Very little gets rid of him, but the 3 main tricks are using Path to Exile, Pyrite Spellbomb, and playing Skullcrack for the “damage cannot be prevented” clause should your opponent decide to block with their own Firewalker. In my experience, it is much better to be the guy playing the Kor Firewalker than to be the one trying to answer him. And when you want him, you want to make damn sure you see him.

PLAYING WITH FIRE

Now, describing how to play Burn is simple, but actually doing it can be tricky. The secret is in the sequencing. Most of the creatures are time sensitive and should be played as soon as possible (with the exception of Grim Lavamancer) since they become outclassed very quickly. The Burn spells will always do what they do, which is deal damage upstairs, and can be saved for the winning flurry after the creature assault has tuckered out. As a rule of thumb, it’s always best to play your “slower” spells first and save your instant speed spells for last, since timing is very important. Spells such as Lightning Bolt, Shard Volley, and Boros Charm are your usual finishers, while Rift Bolt and Lava Spike are sent out first due to their sorcery speed nature.

As an aggressive deck, Burn needs to be proactive in executing its game plan. Against some of the top decks in Modern, this is something you can easily do. Facing decks like Scapeshift, Abzan, and Splinter Twin usually involve a straight path to victory: go for the dome and don’t let up. However, facing certain creature decks demand that you switch roles and play as the “control” player. In these games, you’ll lean heavily on cards like Grim Lavamancer, Searing Blaze, and Lightning Helix for removal while using your other creatures to chump block if need be. Just be careful to not run out of gas, as resource conservation is an integral part of winning these games. You have to fight for every point of damage.

BREAKING THROUGH THE SHIELDS

Oftentimes, your games will be engineered to leave you in a position to get “lucky” by topdecking your winning spell. Just know one thing: “Luck” is just probability made personal. You’re merely in the position to play the percentages (which are in your favor) to draw your winning out. You won’t always “hit” but if you do, expect to hear how lucky you are.

With that said, you will most definitely have games that are drawn out, usually against decks playing counterspells (e.g., Splinter Twin, Mono Blue UrzaTron, and Scapeshift). Good Burn players are usually thinking about 1 turn ahead to begin with, doing damage math to find the winning turn, but these games really involve next-leveling your opponent. When you’re playing draw-go and dancing around countermagic, you have 2 options: Baiting or waiting.

During the draw-go games, you need to be patient. There is no rush to win, as the game has now turned into a Western shootout. Whoever blinks first dies. As you patiently refill your hand, expect anything to happen. At some point you’ll reach critical mass such that you can finish your opponent off with a flurry of spells. You can either cast a spell to bait a counter, enabling your other spells to go under the shield, or wait until your opponent “blinks” when attempting to cast their winning spell. Either way, the end result is the same: Your opponent is bottlenecked on mana. Being able to cast multiple instants in a turn is crucial, as is being able to play on your opponent’s turns. You untap, they die.

SIDEBOARDING WITH BURN

The decks you’ll most likely encounter in the Modern format are Splinter Twin, Abzan Midrange, Affinity, and Burn, as they make up a large portion of the metagame. With that said, other decks you can expect to see are Amulet Bloom, UrzaTron, Scapeshift, Infect, and other aggressive creature strategies such as Merfolk, Hatebears, and Zoo. Since Modern is wide open, you really can’t prepare to fight everything the format will throw at you, but as long as you’re prepared to fight the premiere aggro (Affinity), midrange (Junk/Jund), combo/control (Splinter Twin), and mirror strategies, you’ll do fine. The key is finding cards that are versatile so you can use some of the same cards for different strategies, as opposed to silver bullets, thus simplifying your plan.

A major rule to remember is that you’ll want most (if not all) of your spells in the sideboard to deal damage. After all, this is still your winning strategy. If your spell doesn’t deal damage, then it should either draw you an extra card (such as Relic of Progenitus or Pyrite Spellbomb) or be able to stop a deck from killing you before you can kill them (such as Path to Exile against Splinter Twin or Amulet Bloom’s Titans). Simplicity is the key.

It’s very easy to over sideboard against certain decks, especially when you “think” your opponent might bring in something like Leyline of Sanctity or Dragon’s Claw. It’s often a mistake to do this, as you’d be potentially drawing dead cards in an otherwise winnable matchup. You want all your cards to be live since a lot of games boil down to just ripping a burn spell off the top to win. If you see a Dragon’s Claw in Game 2, then it’s perfectly acceptable to bring in Destructive Revelry for Game 3, since you know you’ll see it. Adding extra cards that aren’t truly necessary will dilute your plan of being proactive. The more you steer into the reactive camp, the more you’ll find yourself losing once you run out of gas. There’s nothing worse than dying with a grip full of cards you can’t cast. In my experience, the “sweet spot” for sideboarding is typically 4-7 cards. All you have to do is take out the cards that you know will have the least amount of impact and add in cards that will always be live.

So how do you know which cards are going to be low impact? I usually ask myself a question: What would I hate to draw from the top during this match? For example, drawing more Eidolon of the Great Revels against Affinity is pretty terrible since Affinity is usually ahead on the board after turns 1-2 and an active Eidolon will punish you more than your opponent. Against decks with Spellskite, Shard Volley usually comes out if I’m on the draw. Which leads me to my next point…

Position (on the play or draw) is everything. Molten Rain, for example, is a much better card to be playing when you’re going first, as opposed to being on the draw. The same goes for Eidolon of the Great Revel in the Burn mirror. And while tempo plays aren’t usually something that Burn is known for making, any edge you can get helps, so use your position wisely when sideboarding.

Lastly, you have to accept that Burn has some close to unwinnable matchups. A fringe deck like Soul Sisters, which specializes in gaining massive amounts of life, is a horror story for a Red Mage as it is, and wasting valuable sideboard slots won’t get you anywhere. You just have to accept the fact, move on, and prepare to face the top decks that you do have a legitimate chance of beating.

FROM EMBER TO INFERNO

That’s about it for the first part of my Burn primer. I hope this has enabled you to either take a plunge into Modern with a somewhat affordable, competitive deck or broaden your understanding of how Modern Burn works. Burn’s power is determined by the speed of the format and greediness of popular manabases -exploiting that is its secret to success. In my next article, I’ll discuss the various modes of attack regarding the Big Four decks and how you can find the holes in their armor.
