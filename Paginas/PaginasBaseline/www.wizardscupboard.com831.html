The Modern Compendium – Part 5

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath
 

I want to go ahead and get this out of the way right from the start. Modern is undergoing a pretty big shakeup. The banning to Splinter Twin, and to a lesser extent Summer Bloom, will have some significant effects on the format. The combination of Eldrazi Temple, Eye of Ugin, and the new Eldrazi creatures has brought forth a deck that appears to be a serious contender. Lastly, the Modern Pro Tour is right around the corner. Last year, the Pro Tour brought Amulet Bloom squarely into the limelight, pronouncing it one of the best decks in the format. Burn was also propelled into the upper echelons of Modern decks. It seems fairly safe to say that the Pro Tour this year will also influence the format.

With that said, I think I am ready to put a hold on this series for a few months. I was planning on finishing the last set of decks next month, and then writing a closing article the month after. Now, I think it would be better if I waited for the format to settle for a few months, then go back and finish up at a later date.

So, if you have anything you would like to hear me write about, please let me know. I don’t play much limited or EDH, but I’m happy to write about something new in Standard, Modern, or Legacy. Let me know what you’d like to read about in the comments, on Facebook, or on Twitter (@DoneTheMath). I’m currently leaning towards a piece on teaching new players how to play Magic, but that can always wait if there are requests for other topics.

Storm (UR Storm)

Metagame Percentage: 1%
Deck Category: Combo
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview/Game Plan

Storm is one of the mechanics that Wizards of the Coast considers a mistake. However, that decision was not made until after a few new cards were printed in Time Spiral, so the Storm has a place in Modern. There is no Tendrils of Agony for a quick ten storm kill, but both Grapeshot and Empty the Warrens are capable of finishing the game.

Storm is a simple deck to understand, if difficult to pilot and play against. Cards with the Storm mechanic are copied for each other spell that was cast that turn. The goal of Storm is to cast 19 spells and then Grapeshot, nine spells and two copies of Grapeshot, or as many spells as possible and then Empty the Warrens for a bunch of tokens.

Storm has its roots in Legacy, but the Modern adaption has some fundamental differences. Legacy has access to tons of fast mana, like Dark Ritual and Lion’s Eye Diamond. Modern Storm doesn’t even have access to all the best ritual spells from the Modern legal sets. Seething Song, Rite of Flame, and Chrome Mox were all banned to keep the speed of the format at a manageable level. Since only the dregs are left for ritual spells in Modern, the deck has found other ways to cope.

That brings us to the second difference between Modern Storm and Legacy Storm. There is no realistic replacement for Infernal Tutor in Modern. Well, Infernal Tutor is legal in Modern, but there is no Lion’s Eye Diamond to pair with it. Without a cheap tutor effect, Modern Storm has moved to Pyromancer Ascension and Past in Flames. Storm simply runs the maximum number of each of them and hopes to Serum Visions or Sleight of Hand into one of them. Those two cards super charge future draw spells to quickly dig through the deck. Eventually, the deck should find a Grapeshot or Empty the Warrens to win the game.

Strengths

Like most combo decks, Storm will win the game on about the fourth turn if unmolested. If Storm can make the first few land drops and keep a full hand of cards, the deck is reliable and powerful. Dealing with 20-30 goblins from Empty the Warrens or multiple copies of Grapeshot with a high storm count is pretty much impossible for any deck in the format.

Storm is difficult to interact with on the battlefield, and that is probably its greatest strength. Goblin Electromancer is the only target for Lightning Bolt, Path to Exile, or Terminate, and Storm doesn’t even need it to go off. Abrupt Decay is more versatile, destroying both Goblin Electromancer and Pyromancer Ascension.

Counterspells are fairly weak against Storm too. Pyromancer Ascension can come down before an opponent even has the mana to cast a counterspell. Past in Flames has Flashback, making it naturally strong against counterspells. Most of the other spells in the deck are redundant. A properly timed counterspell can still damage a Storm deck when it is going off, but it is difficult to identify the proper targets without a lot of practice.

Weaknesses

While Storm is very strong against interaction on the battlefield, it is quite weak to discard. Storm needs a critical mass of spells to win the game, so each removed card reduces the chances that Storm can successfully execute its combo. Thoughtseize, Inquisition of Kozilek, and Duress are all strong options against Storm. Liliana of the Veil is a nightmare to win against, as it can singlehandedly remove all the cards in an opponent’s hand.

As with most combo decks, there are plenty of sideboard cards that can be a challenge to overcome. Graveyard hate, such as Rest in Peace and Grafdigger’s Cage, can significantly alter the effectiveness of Pyromancer Ascension and Past in Flames. Eidolon of Rhetoric pretty much stops Storm in its tracks until it can be removed. Gaddock Teeg and Scavenging Ooze are obnoxious, but beatable.

Variations

There isn’t much room for variation in Storm, as there are only a few ritual spells legal in Modern. Some players like Faithless Looting, while others do not. That’s about as varied as Storm gets. There isn’t a strong incentive to playing an additional color.

Dredgevine

Metagame Percentage: 0%
Deck Category: Aggro/Combo
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview/Game Plan

Dredgevine is the deck of choice if you like aggressive decks and appreciate value plays.  The deck is constructed around a base of recursive creatures in Gravecrawler, Bloodghast, and Vengevine. The deck dumps large numbers of cards into the graveyard with Grisley Salvage and Satyr Wayfinder. Lotleth Troll and Gurmag Angler are a couple extra zombies for Gravecrawler that synergize quite well with the rest of the package.

Some versions of Dredgevine run a package of Varolz, the Scar-Striped and Death’s Shadow. Scavenging a Death’s Shadow gives 13 +1/+1 counters to a creature, which is good for all of the obvious reasons. Either Varols or Death’s Shadow are proof that the combo is in a particular version of Dredgevine, because neither sees play on its own.

Strengths

Most removal spells are almost laughably bad against Dredgevine. Path to Exile is still good against the various threats, but Lightning Bolt and Abrupt Decay don’t permanently kill a single threat beyond Satyr Wayfinder. Terminate can handle Lotleth Troll and Gurmag Angler, but Gravecrawler, Bloodghast, and Vengevine can just shrug that off too.

Dredgevine has access the same discard spells that make Jund and Abzan good against various combo decks. Abrupt Decay and Murderous Cut can handle most of the decks that commit creatures to the board.

Weaknesses

Dredgevine has access to the same discard and removal as Jund and Abzan, but it has less room in the deck to play them. Gravecrawler and Vengevine require a decent number of creatures, which reduces the space for interactive spells. Since the deck isn’t guaranteed to draw a discard effect, it is possible to just get run over by the non-interactive combo decks before it can cobble together lethal damage.

The loss of percentage points to combo decks should be balanced against an improved matchup against Jund and Grixis. However, Abzan is already stronger against Dredgevine due to Path to Exile taking the place of Lightning Bolt.

Graveyard hate is, unsurprisingly, quite strong against Dredgevine. Recursive threats aren’t very good if they can’t be recurred. Rest in Peace is easily the best option, but Grafdigger’s Cage, Relic of Progenitus, and Scavenging Ooze are all strong against Dredgevine.

Variations

Dredgevine decks usually black and green, but will sometimes splash red. Red provides Faithless Looting for a massive boost in draw power, along with a suite of powerful sideboard options (Crumble to Dust, Ancient Grudge, Slaughter Games, etc…).

A white splash can provide access to Lingering Souls and other sideboard options, but it is not a popular splash color.

Gifts Ungiven

Metagame Percentage: 0%
Deck Category: Control
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview/Game Plan

This might be the hardest deck in Modern to completely describe. The deck’s namesake, Gifts Ungiven, encourages a deck full of singletons and leaves room for limitless customization. There are a few fairly consistent packages to expect in the deck, but it is otherwise just a good stuff deck.

Unburial Rites is a virtual guarantee in Gifts. Searching for just a giant creature and Unburial Rites with Gifts Ungiven will put them both in the graveyard, where the creature can be easily reanimated the next turn. Iona, Shield of Emeria and Elesh Norn, Grand Cenobite are the two most common targets, since they are completely devastating against the appropriate deck.

Life from the Loam is usually the lynchpin for a value package in the Gifts deck. Raven’s Crime and Urborg, Tomb of Yawgmoth can grind out an opponent’s hand. Ghost Quarter can deal with various utility lands, and eventually all of an opponent’s lands. Academy Ruins can recur Engineered Explosives, Batterskull, or whatever other artifact the deck might play.

Bring to Light has added another dimension to the deck. If nothing else, it can act as additional copies of Gifts Ungiven.  However, Bring to Light shines in a deck that is already filled one-ofs. It isn’t a guarantee in a Gifts Ungiven deck, since it stretches the mana even further. However, it shouldn’t be an unexpected surprise either. Bring to Light can also prompt the use of Glittering Wish, which just adds to the deck’s ability to tutor for specific answers.

Strengths

Given enough time, a Gifts deck can pretty much grind any other deck into dust.

I’ve mentioned customization as a strength in several other decks, but it’s on a completely different level in a Gifts deck. Any card that has a power level sufficient for Modern can be a consideration for a Gifts deck, and it can be difficult simply trimming a list down to 60 cards.   Most lists will have a few standard “Gift piles,” things like Unburial Rites plus a giant monster and a value pile that doesn’t really care which cards go to the hand or graveyard (think Life from the Loam). Beyond that, Snapcaster Mage, Eternal Witness, and Noxious Revival can all guarantee access to a specific card.

Removal is often quite varied in Gifts decks, for the obvious reasons. This can occasionally be a benefit, since some cards are naturally resistant to certain removal. Phyrexian Crusader is a great example in the recent G/B Infect decks, dodging both Path to Exile and Lightning Bolt. Cards like Dismember and Go for the Throat provide effective answers that don’t appear in more streamlined decks.

Gifts decks can make better use of sideboard cards, since the deck is filled with ways to find singleton cards already. Gifts Ungiven and possibly Bring to Light add virtual copies of sideboard cards, so the deck is stronger against linear strategies than it might appear at first glance. That doesn’t mean the first game won’t still be a struggle.

Weaknesses

Gifts can take a while to get going, so it can be weak to streamlined aggressive or combo decks. The deck can play a mixture of discard and counterspells to attempt to slow the game down, but that won’t always work.

The nature of Gifts decks means that there are plenty of dead cards for any given matchup in game one. Sideboarding can fix this, but there will be games where an Iona, Shield of Emeria or Raven’s Crime shows up that just doesn’t matter against a given opponent.

The most efficient removal spells in Modern are pretty easy to identify. Because of the need for variation for Gifts Ungiven, this deck can end up with some suboptimal. This can occasionally be problematic, since the non standard spells see less play because of prohibitive mana costs (Detention Sphere) or inconvenient restrictions (Go for the Throat). Drawing a Go for the Throat against Affinity is pretty much the worst feeling ever.

Sideboarding fixes most of these problems, but the deck will always be a little clunky. Gifts is looking to play a long game, so any stumbling will often knock the deck out before it ever has a chance to get going.

Variations

I don’t really want to go into variations much, since I feel like that was covered in depth already. I don’t think there are any two Gifts decks that actually look the same, so I guess they are all variations.

Grishoalbrand Reanimator (Instant Reanimator)

Metagame Percentage: 1%
Deck Category: Combo
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview/Game Plan

I heavily dislike the name of this deck. I hate typing it and it sound stupid. For the sake of my sanity, I’m just calling it Reanimator. Gifts Ungiven is the closest other Modern deck to a Reanimator deck, so I feel safe that using the name here won’t cause confusion.

Reanimator wants to get a Griselbrand into play as fast as possible. That is the only consistent goal of all the various Reanimator decks. A discard outlet and the card Goryo’s Vengeance are the fastest way to accomplish this, but these decks often run either Footsteps of the Goryo or Through the Breach for redundant methods to cheat creatures into play.

Once these decks have a Griselbrand in play, they route to victory can be both swift and varied. Original versions of the deck used Fury of the Horde to have Griselbrand deal 21 damage. Newer versions use Nourishing Shoal and Worldspine Wurm to generate massive quantities of life that Griselbrand can convert into cards. The kill condition is usually a Borborygmos Enraged (also reanimated) with a hand full of lands. That deck generated the nickname Grishoalbrand Reanimator, but mtgtop8.com combines all Goryo’s Vengeance decks into one category.

Strengths

This deck can win the game on the second turn. Go back and read that again. I’m not sure it’s possible to come up with a point in favor of any deck in Modern. Even in the games that don’t end on the spot, Reanimator can put itself so far ahead that winning is inevitable.

Reanimator can utilize Blood Moon in sideboard games if it seems appropriate. Simian Spirit Guide, a standard addition to the Reanimator deck, can help ramp it out earlier than expected.

There isn’t a ton else to say about the strength of Reanimator. The busted draws are the true strength of the deck. The Nourishing Shoal version is incidentally strong against Burn, since gaining 8+ life is almost enough to win on the spot. But scenarios like that are corner cases and not a real draw to the deck. It’s just about the fast wins.

Weaknesses

Goryo’s Vengeance decks have always been inconsistent.   They generate some of the flashiest wins, capable of achieving victory on the second turn. The rest of the time, the deck just spins its wheels and doesn’t actually do anything. That’s just a symptom of the format. Serum Visions and Sleight of Hand are the only deck manipulation spells available, and they are miles worse than cards like Preordain and Ponder. That problem is compounded by the lack of any kind of additional effect similar to Goryo’s Vengeance. Since you can’t actually rely on finding Goryo’s Vengeance in every game, the deck has to jump through hoops like Through the Breach as a backup plan. Overall, this can lead to hands full of Pentad Prisms and Izzet Charms that just don’t do anything, since Goryo’s Vengeance and Through the Breach need different setup cards. The deck still exists because of the potential for explosive hands, but this is not the most stable weapon to bring to a tournament.

Scavenging Ooze is a significant problem for Reanimator, but it can’t handle the fastest starts the deck can produce. Ooze can’t actually start working on the graveyard until the third turn, so

Relic of Progenitus is a larger problem, as it can come down fast enough to hinder even the fastest start from Renaimator. However, R/G Tron is the only deck that plays Relic of Progenitus in the starting 60 cards.

Nihil spellbomb, Rest in Peace, and any other graveyard hate are effective against Reanimator. The deck has a backup plan with Through the Breach, but it is more mana intensive and weak to Thoughtseize.

Variations

All Goryo’s Vengeance decks really focus on getting Griselbrand into play as fast as possible. The shell surrounding that can change some. There are the Nourishing Shoal decks and Fury of the Horde decks mentioned above. Other versions are packed with Through the Breachs and Emrakuls to just decimate an opponent. Goryo’s Vengeance is powerful enough that it can fit its way into other decks. Some fair decks look to reanimate Obzedat, Ghost Council, since it can circumvent the exile clause on Goryo’s Vengeance. That starts to stray into a different deck, albeit one capable of a combo finish.
