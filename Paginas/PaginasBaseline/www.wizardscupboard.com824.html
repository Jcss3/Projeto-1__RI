Impressions from Pro Tour Shadows over Innistrad

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath

 

It’s a nice change having a diverse Top 8 at the Pro Tour.  After months of four color, mono gold card monstrosities, I was starting to forget what innovation truly looked like.  PTSOI (Pro Tour Shadows over Innistrad) delivered in spades, giving us tons of new decks to look at.  Instead of reviewing the decks as a whole, I want to look at some of the individual cards.  The PT breakout decks were all brewed expecting to fight Bant Company and Humans, and they will need to evolve to combat the other breakout decks.  I’m going to cover a list of cards that stuck out during the first few weeks of Shadows over Innistrad Standard.  The reasons can range from breakout stars to complete duds to potential technology.  In no particular order.

I reference several decks from the Pro Tour during this article.  I have the lists written out at the bottom of the article if you are unfamiliar with one or more of them.

 

Collected Company

This one isn’t going away anytime soon, and you probably already knew it.  Despite being directly in the cross hairs of every testing team going into PTSOI, Bant Company still made the finals.  The B/G Sacrifice deck that Channel Fireball brought also runs the powerful instant.  Expect to see Collected Company decks succeeding in tournaments until Dragons of Tarkir rotates.  Collected Company is one of the most powerful card in Standard.

 

Pyromancer’s Goggles

The Goggles are probably the biggest engine card to come out of the early weeks of Shadows over Innistrad Standard.  Blue is lacking a strong card draw spell, so players have started looking elsewhere.  The U/R Goggles deck from the SCG Tour was the first home for Pyromancer’s Goggles, and players were able to adapt the Goggles core to other decks.  Tormenting Voice and Magmatic Insight are the primary beneficiaries of the Goggles breakout, as they each draw four fresh cards when copied.   Due to the synergy with either Goggles or Tormenting Voice/Magmatic Insight, some number of Drownyard Temple, Fall of the Titans, Fiery Impulse, and Madness spells round out this core.

PTSOI showed that the Pyromancer’s Goggles core could be placed in several decks.  The R/G Goggles Ramp deck used this core for a combination of ramp (Drownyard Temple/Pyromancer’s Goggles) and card advantage.  The W/R Eldrazi Goggles deck used it as a source of card advantage, doubling up on Tormenting Voice/Magamtic Insight and various red removal spells.

The Goggles core outperforms almost any other card drawing option in Standard, with only Seasons Past putting up a fight.  That is, unless Kolaghan’s Command makes a resurgence.  If it does, I wouldn’t want anything to do with a five mana artifact as part of my engine.

 

Drownyard Temple

It looks like I was wrong about this card in my set review, mainly because of the synergy with Tormenting Voice and Magmatic Insight.  Most decks that run the red suite of card draw spells are planning to get value from Pyromancer’s Goggles or Madness spells.  However, those two color decks can afford a few colorless lands, and Drownyard Temple makes for a solid card to discard for value.  Most of those decks are also running Chandra, Flamecaller, making the ramp aspect of Drownyard Temple appreciated.  Pretty much any deck running Pyromancer’s Goggles is going to wind up wanting some number of Drownyard Temples.  It is the easiest way to gain value off of the Tormenting Voice/Magmatic Insight duo if you can’t find a copy of Pyromancer’s Goggles.

 

Fall of the Titans

If your opponent is playing a Pyromancer’s Goggles deck, expect this card.  Fall of the Titans is a great way to just die on a stabilized board from a high life total.  Plan accordingly.

 

Kolaghan’s Command

Right now, B/R suffers from a lot of overlap.  Red and black has tons of redundancy in their removal spells.  Fiery Impulse fills most of the same roles as Grasp of Darkness and Radiant Flames, Kozilek’s Return, and Languish all provide similar Wrath effects.  Both colors offer some kind of card advantage.  Black has Read the Bones and possibly Dark Petition.  Red has Tormenting Voice and Magmatic Insight backed by Pyromancer’s Goggles.  Beyond that, both colors are lacking for good suite of threats.  Each color has a couple heavy hitters (mainly Kalitas and Chandra) and a bunch of cards that are laughable for constructed.  Lastly, the mana in this format punishes unnecessary splashes.  That makes for a color combination that should not be paired together.  Black or red should be paired with another color’s threats to build a solid deck.

Kolaghan’s Command might be worth the effort.  If enough decks incorporate the Goggles core, having access to artifact destruction in your main deck will be incredibly powerful.  I still believe that black-red is a poor deck choice, but I could easily see white-red or white-black splashing for just Command if Goggles remains popular.

 

Dark Petition

Dark Petition was all over last weekend during PTSOI.  The reason for this is fairly straight forward.  Humans and Bant Company were the decks to beat going into the event.  Those decks are both heavily damaged by Languish.  Languish punishes the use of creatures.  Languish decks can’t rely on a stream of creatures to win games, so they use spells.  If you are playing enough spells, Dark Petition is almost Demonic Tutor, and Vintage and Commander are the only places you can cast that card.

The Pantheon’s Seasons Past control deck was the most noteworthy home for Dark Petition.  You can use a Dark Petition to go find a Seasons Past, use it to recur the Dark Petition, and then use that same Dark Petition to go find the same Seasons Past sitting on the bottom of your library.  Oh, and Seasons Past probably drew you another four cards in the process.

 

Seasons Past

I already discussed this one in respect to Dark Petition, but it does a little more.  Blue really has a lack of strong card advantage in this format.  Blue’s power is actually pretty concentrated in Jace, Vryn’s Prodigy, Reflector Mage, and maybe Dragonlord Ojutai/Silumgar’s Scorn.  None of those are particularly good at drawing cards, so players have started looking in other places.  If you are looking for quantity with your card draw, it’s hard to get more cards than Seasons Past will draw for you.  Beyond that, most large card draw spells will be about 40% lands.  Seasons Past will only draw one land at most, filling your hand with spells instead.

If you are in the market to draw a bunch of cards this Standard season, Dark Petition into Seasons Past is the way to do it.

 

Gryff’s Boon

Gryff’s Boon might not be pretty, but it gets a very specific job done.  When your Humans deck hits a roadblock after getting through almost 20 damage, Gryff’s Boon is there to help you limp across the finish line.  White doesn’t have options for something like Exquisite Firecraft, so Gryff’s Boon has got your back.

Gryff’s Boon provides a form of insurance for your Always Watching when you play against Dromoka’s Command.  It also protects your Silkwraps and Stasis Snares if you have copies of either.  On the other hand, Gryff’s Boon is a serious liability if you play against Tragic Arrogance.  You want to remove all of your enchantments that aren’t Always Watching if you expect copies of Tragic Arrogance.  That’s an unfortunate mix of cards, since both Bant Company and W/G Tokens play 3-4 Dromoka’s Command and usually board into Tragic Arrogance for the Humans matchup.

 

Tragic Arrogance

Mostly sitting in sideboards this weekend, Tragic Arrogance is one of the best ways to clear up the mess made by Bant Company, Humans, B/G Sacrifice, and anything else that wants to flood the board.  Some of the midrange white decks have started moving copies to their starting 60, since it is one of the best wrath effects available to them.  Tragic Arrogance demands pretty much the same skill set as any other Wrath effect to play against.  The only exception might be Thraben Inspector.  I might try to avoid playing that when you already have a favorable board state, since it is always going to be your worst creature.

 

Cryptolith Rite

So, it’s kind of a fixed Earthcraft?  Original brews involved Ulvenwald Hydra, Dragonlord Atarka, and Ulamog, the Ceaseless Hunger as ramp targets.  These decks had explosive potential, but were pretty reliant on actually finding a copy of Cryptolith Rite.  The B/G Sacrifice deck pretty tossed all of those cards in favor of something a little more reasonable.

 

Nantuko Husk

In reality, this is a split between Nantuko Husk and Zulaport Cutthroat.  Even if you aren’t playing B/G Sacrifice deck yourself, you should know how to do Husk math.  Make sure you are blocking if necessary, and make sure to account for the activation of Hissing Quagmire and Blisterpod’s Scion token.  Also pay attention to a possible Collected Company.  Those can generate up to four additional creatures to sacrifice or up to two additional Zulaport Cutthroat.

 

Kalitas, Traitor to Ghet

I really didn’t like Kalitas going into Shadows over Innistrad.  It seemed easy to kill between Ultimate Price, Grasp of Darkness, and Lightning Axe.  The exiling trigger didn’t seem that relevant either, with Rally the Ancestors rotating.  I was mostly interested in Lifelink on a midrange creature.  Turns out, Kalitas is just as good as it always was.  Multiple different controlling decks used Kalitas as a win condition, not just as a threat to run out on turn four.  The G/B Sacrifice deck has zero answers to a Kalitas in its main deck, and is almost completely unable to win through one.  If your deck is running black, it should have some number of Kalitas as a proactive answer to the B/G Sacrifice deck, either in the main deck or sideboard.

 

Westvale Abbey

Looks like the survey is in, Standard players are ready to accept Ormendahl into their lives.  The colorless lands of choice appear to be Westvale Abbey for creature decks, Drownyard Temple for Pyromancer’s Goggles decks, and some mix of Westvale Abbey and Blighted Fen for control decks.  Both the W/G Tokens deck and the B/G Sacrifice deck from the Top 8 of PTSOI are fairly dedicated Westvale Abbey decks, but Abbey also made an appearance in the W/R Eldrazi Goggles deck.  Westvale Abbey is also a common sideboard plan for Humans decks, when they need additional lands for Gideon, Ally of Zendikar.

Deck construction going forward should include some sort of plan for Westvale Abbey.  Reflector Mage, Declaration in Stone, Silk Wrap, and Stasis Snare are all solid answers.  Black has some options like To the Slaughter or Fleshbag Marauder.  Blue has Thing in the Ice and a bunch of bad options like Disperse.  Green has World Breaker or Reclaiming Vines if you are desperate.  Red has… Act of Treason?  Better hope your red deck can race someone trying to summon Ormendahl.

 

Duskwatch Recruiter

I’m confused why this is an uncommon, but my wallet isn’t going to complain.  There really isn’t any reason that a Collected Company deck shouldn’t play this card.  It increases the creature count for Collected Company while providing you a fantastic option for drawing cards.  If Duskwatch Recruiter ever flips, he turns into a solid aggressive threat that discounts the cost of all the creatures you just drew.

Duskwatch Recruiter is the real deal.  Expect to see tons of him over the next six months while we still have Collected Company.  Expect to see him for the next year after that too, because he will find a home somewhere.

 

Evolutionary Leap

The PTSOI winning decklist played two copies of Evolutionary Leap, and they served a good role in the deck.  First, the deck is very capable of generating low value creatures to feed the enchantment.  Second, it offers an outlet to sacrifice Hangarback Walker, reducing its vulnerability to Reflector Mage and the various white exile effects.  Lastly, the W/G Tokens deck relies on Archangel Avacyn in several matchups, and Evolutionary Leap can both find and flip the mythic angel.

A sideboard strategy that I recently heard discussed involved cutting all the creatures besides Hangarback Walker and Archangel Avacyn to ensure finding one against the B/G Sacrifice deck.

 

Rise from the Tides

I don’t actually expect this one to break out in a big way, but I sure want to see it.  If you’re looking for a budget brew for FNM, this one certainly has some appeal.

Eldrazi Displacer

I really expected more of the Bant Company decks to try to incorporate Eldrazi Displacer as an answer for the mirror.  It makes the mana worse, but I’m still willing to debate that moving away from Jace could alleviate that stress.  Jace is a glorified Merfolk Looter that eventually draws a Collected Company or Dromoka’s Command for free.  I won’t argue that it’s strong, but I’m willing to believe Bant Company can do better.

Eldrazi Displacer with Archangel Avacyn did not make a strong showing during PTSOI.  Several of the white based Eldrazi decks tossed around in the first few weeks of the season used Displacer as a way to protect your team after triggering Avacyn, the Purifier’s damage ability.  Those white based decks apparently couldn’t make it to the top tables during the Pro Tour.

 

Nahiri, the Harbinger

During my set review, I discussed the difficulty in finding a home for Nahiri.  That is still her biggest problem, but some people have been up to the challenge.  The first time I actually saw her in play was on the second week of the SCG Tour, where Joe Lossett was splashing white in the U/R Goggles deck for Nahiri, the Harbinger and Radiant Flames.  Since then, she has appeared in several Boros and Jeskai decks in various quantities.  The most success she saw might have been the Jeskai Control deck that Rob Pisano played to an 8-2 finish at PTSOI, where she was an integral part of the deck’s success.  As the bitter realization of Arlinn Kord’s lack of a home sets in, Nahiri appears to be rising up to take her place as a successful planeswalker from Shadows over Innistrad

This is a little off topic, but I really want to play Nahiri with Doubling Season in Modern.  This isn’t new to me, I’ve been wanting to play Jace, Architect of Thought with Doubling Season in Modern for years.  It certainly isn’t good with Zoo and Burn being so popular, but smacking people with Emrakul has a certain appeal.  The appeal of mass destruction maybe, but I can’t help what I want.  It’s the little things in life, right?

 

Rending Volley

If you are playing red, this is probably the easiest way to improve your matchup against Humans.  The Jeskai Madness Control deck that featured four copies of Nahiri, the Harbinger even ran a copy in the main deck.  That is just another benefit of all the discard outlets available in that deck.  You have a premium sideboard card against the right decks and several ways to get rid of it against everything else.  This plan isn’t something you can go to extremes with, or you run the risk of drawing multiple blanks in a game.  In moderation, it adds a powerful dimension to your deck.

 

Arlinn Kord

The stars just did not align for this girl.  Red aggressive decks are pretty much outclassed on every level by the Humans deck.  Ramp decks gravitated towards strategies that did not incorporate Arlinn Kord.  Green’s non-ramp decks often push towards Collected Company, and red has nothing to offer for a Company deck.  Red’s only real expensive threat, Chandra, Flamecaller, has no real synergy with Arlinn Kord.  Maybe she will find a home eventually, but now does not look like the time to be playing with the werewolf planeswalker.

 

Olivia, Mobilized for War

We got enough cards for a Vampire deck, it just didn’t end up being any good.  The format has a lot of 2/3 creatures that can be hard to push through without help.  Stensia Masquerade isn’t quite as good as Always Watching when the real concern is pushing through slightly large creatures.  Falkenrath Gorger, Olivia, Mobilized for War, and Heir of Falkenrath look great, but Eldrich Moon will have to pump some new blood into this archetype for it to see some results.

 

Diregraf Colossus

Unlike the Vampire tribe, we just didn’t get close to enough Zombies for Diregraf Colossus.  Relentless Dead is pretty much the only supporting cast, and that thing didn’t see any play either.  Not even in the B/G Sacrifice deck, which was Relentless Dead’s only other hope.  Once again, Diregraf Colossus might be worth a revisit when Eldrich Moon comes knocking.

 

Gaea’s Revenge

I’ve heard a few players mention Gaea’s Revenge as a possible answer for the Seasons Past mirror match.  It seems alright, provided you already have an answer for their Hissing Quagmires.

 

Hallowed Moonlight

I hope you didn’t get rid of these when Rally the Ancestors rotated out of Standard.  Collected Company decks are still alive and ticking.  Gideon, Ally of Zendikar and Nissa, Voice of Zendikar just won PTSOI.  End of turn Secure the Waste into transforming Westvale Abbey is a reasonable plan.  Hallowed Moonlight still has a home in your sideboard if you are a slower white deck.

 

Reflector Mage

Bant Company and W/U Humans are going to make up the baseline of this format.  If decks start to get too inbred against each other, these decks are going to come back and bring people back into line.  Both Reflector Mage decks had a good weekend, despite the giant target on them.  Several teams found different ways to combat those decks, but now they will all have to adapt to the existence of each other as well.  Just don’t forget to plan for the decks everyone already knew about.

 

Demonic Pact

There was a pretty sweet Demonic Pact deck that was running Dark Petition for Crush of Tentacles to prevent losing the game.  I have absolutely no idea if it is any good, but I’m positive that it is sweet, sweet value.

 

 

I’m not sure I can find anything better than that to close on, so that’s it for this month.  If you’ve got any questions or comments for me, feel free to post them in the comments.  If that’s not to your liking, you can find me on twitter at @DoneTheMath.
