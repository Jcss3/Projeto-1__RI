Mana in EDH Part 2: Choosing the Correct Ramp Package.

Mana in EDH Part 2:  Choosing the Correct Ramp Package.

By Craig Sidorowicz

In Part one, we discussed the importance of playing the correct quantity of lands. We also evaluated each cycle of Playable dual lands to ensure we build decks with the best lands available.  Now, we’ll take a look at another deceivingly complicated aspect of deckbuilding: the “Resource Amplification Management Package, also known (to magic professionals) as: R.A.M.P!…

 

…Just kidding! “Ramp” is actually just a shorthand for movement to a higher point on your “available mana curve,” and is slang derived from one of the original spells to help us do this: Rampant Growth

I. Feed Me!

Originally printed in Mirage, Rampant Growth has been featured in fourteen different magic sets, and has usually made an impact on the standard format.  It’s main purposes are to fix mana and to make a midrange deck faster than other midrange decks (think “Jund” in Alara-Zendikar Standard) or to help “big” decks accelerate into the high cost cards on their mana curve (Eldrazi Green in Zendikar/Scars Standard).  I wouldn’t be surprised if it reappears on the “big stage” in Battle For Zendikar, where we are likely to see the Landfall and “Big Dumb Annihilator Titan” mechanics revisited.

 

In magic’s history there have been a few hundred cards printed for the purpose of (R)amplifying our available mana resources by doing one of the following things:

  •  Allowing an additional land to be put into play (sometimes from the hand or top of library).
  • Searching the library for lands and putting them onto the battlefield untapped.
  • Searching the library for lands and putting them onto the battlefield tapped.
  • Searching the library for a land and putting it into the player’s hand. (This is not technically ramp, because you do not gain a resource unless you put the land into play that turn, but you do gain consistency/color fixing.) This is also known as a “tutor.”
  • Existing as a nonland permanent that produces mana (usually creatures or artifacts but sometimes enchantments).
  • Existing as a nonland permanent that modifies existing lands by allowing them to tap for additional mana.

 Since there are hundreds of options, our question/problem becomes:

“Which ramp spells should we include in our deck?”

“How many” of these effects does a deck need to have, without overdoing it?

 This is actually a multifaceted problem, calling for analysis of:

  • Which are the most powerful/efficient?
  • Which have the most synergy with the plan/strategy of the deck?

How Many?

Just like our land discussion from last article, it makes sense to start with: “how many?”  If you use too few, you really sacrifice any reliable chance of consistency.  Too many, and you run the risk of saturating your deck with cards that are horrible topdecks when drawn late-game.  Once we know the correct number of slots to devote, we can begin to plan.

Lets get to work!

First off, our deck is (should be!) 35-40% land.  Of the 60-65 cards remaining, I like to dedicate about 10-20% (so 6-12 cards) of that to mana enablers.  This would roughly be the equivalent of 1-2 playsets (4-ofs) in a 60 card deck in any other format.  Green decks usually have more efficient enablers, so its reasonable to conclude that I need fewer of them than I would in a non-green deck.  For example, Cultivate and Wayfarer’s Bauble are both great mana enablers.  They effectively cost 3 mana to utilize their effects, but cultivate nets twice the benefit (1.5 if you’ve already played a land.)  Unfortunately, a mono-red deck can’t include Cultivate, so it makes sense to include another mana enabler in the red deck if I want to keep up with a green deck.  As you can see, this puts the colors with the fewest mana enablers (red and blue) at a disadvantage when it comes to ramping.  Luckily, though, there are some amazing artifacts available (Sol Ring, Solemn Simulacrum) to help get the job done.  In the next section, we will discuss which mana enablers have the most synergy with a selection of deck strategies.

 II: It’s Easier Being Green

Color combinations that include green are at an advantage, to the point that it might be considered an embarrassment of riches.  We have so many options to choose from, so we must think strategically.  Let’s examine a few example scenarios and diagnose the best possible mana-fixing options for each:

 Mono Green Ezuri Overrun (Click to enlarge picture)

You are familiar with this deck from last article, so we will start there.   The deck’s strategy is to amass a collection of mana-producing elves, and then use Ezuri’s abilities to protect, and then overwhelm, our opponents.  The elves serve a dual purpose here: they give us mana in the beginning stage, then provide soldiers for our finishIng moves. In this list, we only run (x) lands, and have many elves available to produce green or untap our green producing lands.  We run fewer lands because we have a low curve, but this leaves us open to board wipes or anti-creature strategies like Humility.

Added Synergy: permanent-based (especially tribal creature) mana producers, elves that search for lands and put them into play, Land tutors: Sylvan Scrying and Crop Rotation can grab Gaea’s Cradle, or Yavimaya Hollow)

Decreased Synergy: mana rocks (we play green), extra land enablers (we play too few lands). Spell-based ramp effects (we can do this with just our creatures), mana doublers: (they don’t have legs, thus cannot attack!)

 Edric’s Elves

This deck has a similar plan to Ezuri, but it is less straightforward.  It seeks to build a massive army via Edric’s card drawing ability and eventually overrun opponents.  The twist is that it does so with spell-based overruns (Overwhelming Stampede, Triumph of the Hordes) and backs it up with 8-10 of the best Counterspells.

Added Synergy: Permanent-Based (tribal creatures that tap for mana), Spell Based: Cultivate, Kodama’s Reach, and Skyshroud Claim go a long way in helping us secure double-blue for countermagic), Land Tutors for Cradle are also applicable here.

Decreased Synergy: mana rocks (we play green), extra land enablers (too few lands), mana doublers (two colors)

 Azusa Turboland

As we remember from last article, this list wants to drop as many lands as possible using the most efficient means possible.  Azusa is the strongest, and has cousins like Oracle of Mul Daya and Courser that can help her with her work.  She also uses mana-doublers, which are best applied in Mono-Color decks, (although Mirari’s Wake is amazing too!).

Added Synergy: Extra Land enablers, spell based ramp, mana doublers, Land tutors for many specialty lands.

Decreased Synergy: Mana Rocks (exception is Mind Stone/ Commander’s Sphere for card draw), Tribal creatures (we want to go BIG as opposed to having a wide line of small creatures.

 Marath Token Landfall

The goal here is to utilize Marath, token producers, and planeswalkers (and break them with Doubling season effects).  Cards that bring lands from the library onto the battlefield are especially useful here. This decklist uses a combination package: basically anything on theme with landfall.  I would avoid using mana creatures (except Lotus Cobra) because they have the potential to be collateral damage when an opponent board-wipes my big creatures.  The deck is a bit greedy with the red mana producing lands, however red is a minor splash color for this predominantly Green/White build.

Added Synergy: landfall ramp, mana doubler (mirari’s Wake)

Decreased Synergy: mana rocks (chromatic lantern is a strong card in any tri-color deck though!), extra land enablers (we don’t play an high enough quantity of land).

 

III. The Sad Robot

For years I struggled with durdly esper, azorius, rakdos, and orzhov decks.  For some reason they just couldn’t compete with the Animars, momir Vigs, and Ghave decks of my playgroup, and after quite a few losses I finally realized that I was building these color combinations with subpar Ramp Packages.

 Then a sad robot showed me the way…

 

Like Sol Ring and Sensei’s Divining Top, Solemn Simulacrum ranks as a 99% auto include in any EDH deck. By it’s design it’s pretty straightforward, but it’s value is defined by its overabundance of…well…VALUE!  So many players initially brush this card off, but later realize it serves multiple functions: Ramp, Attack, Block, and Card Draw.  It also interacts well with artifact synergies.

 First, it ramps your mana, then it blocks for you (or gives you a creature to sacrifice) then it draws you a card.  Further, it does so for colorless mana.  It is also used as a political bargaining chip more than any other card I know.  What other creature are you happier to have blocked, sacrificed, or indiscriminately killed? How many times has an opponent politely asked you to block their Sad Robot so that they can have another chance to draw the extra card that could save the game from a degenerate board state?

I include him in almost every deck.  He’s just that good.

 Mana Rocks

There are more and more of these being printed in each set.  They too have a distinct hierarchy, so I’ll be a pal, and break it down for you:

 Tier 1: Enters untapped, produces more than one mana, or more than one color, with as few drawbacks as possible, low to moderate (0-3) mana cost.

 If I saw these in any deck (including a green deck) I wouldn’t question them.  They all function independently: do not require other cards or requirements to perform their function.

  • Sol Ring
  • Mana Crypt
  • Gilded Lotus
  • Thran Dynamo
  • Mirrodin Talismans
  • Chromatic Lantern
  • Coalition Relic
  • Mind Stone
  • Commander’s Sphere

 

Mana doublers: best in mono-color decks because they reward you for having basic lands.

  • Caged Sun
  • Gauntlet of Power
  • Extraplanar Lens

 

Tier 2: These produce more than one mana or color, but have drawbacks such as: enters tapped, activation costs, additional casting costs/requirements, not enough “bang” for the cost, or stays tapped after use.

 Depending on deck synergy, they can become top tier.  (Tezzeret loves the monoliths!). This automatically implies that they rely on other cards or additional mana investments to be top tier, thus relegating them to “tier two.”  Yes, I know that “Mox” and “Tier Two” are usually not synonymous, but you’ll have to trust my experience on these.

  • Mox Opal
  • Mox Diamond
  • Chrome Mox
  • Mana Vault
  • Grim Monolith
  • Basalt Monolith
  • Darksteel Ingot
  • Worn powerstone
  • Ravnica block signets
  • Return to Ravnica Keyrunes
  • Wayfarer’s Bauble
  • Burnished Hart
  • Sphere of Suns
  • Pristine Talisman
  • Everflowing Chalice

 

Tier 3: at this point they start to cost 3 or more but are considerably underpowered for their price.  I would have to exhaust most of the options from tiers 1 and 2 before I started looking here.  As previously mentioned, I only want about 10-20% of my nonland slots to be devoted to mana ramping and fixing, so these just get outclassed by the previous tiers.  If playing on a budget, these are better than nothing.

  • Dragon’s Maze Cluestones
  • Obelisks
  • Borderposts
  • Tarkir Banners
  • Dragonstones

 Mana Rocks in Non Green Decks:

The next three decks illustrate how dedicating 20% or more of your deck to ramping and fixing can be helpful when your Commander hails from the deserts or frozen wastelands of magic, places where there are no “Tree Symbols…”

 Oloro esper control

In this deck I used mana rocks from tiers one and two, in addition to land tax, which is one of the strongest white magic cards ever printed.  I also have access to the top three Black tutors, which can always get Land Tax, Sol Ring, or a land.  This deck has only three creatures, Oloro, Solemn Simulacrum, and Sun Titan, the King of Value, just for their abilities to generate tempo.  The devious ulterior motive is to destroy all lands, thereby punishing the green decks that ramped all game, while we live on a pile of mana rocks.

 Narset, the Rock Star

A literal avalanche of mana rocks by none other than EDH rockstar Rob Burgess.  It actually replaces a third of its land slots with mana rocks, which almost guarantees that Narset will hit for 75-100% of full value each swing.  The deck is built to allow multiple attack steps per turn, allowing narset to finish the job before you can say “Austere Command.”  Of course, if the artifact hate is used against you, the low land count can spell problems in the rebuilding stages, especially if Narset gets killed (by mass removal) to the point where her mana cost is prohibitive to recasting her.

 Mono Red Marton Stromgald

Created by Isbpathfinder (a popular moderator from Mtgsalvation) This deck takes advantage of the colorless mana rocks by using a ton of artifacts and X spells.  It also uses powerful red card draw with all of the draw-sevens.   The plan is to make a lot of tokens, and then finish by attacking with Marton, who is the ancient version of Craterhoof Behemoth.  It’s similar to the Azusa deck in that it wants to dump its hand on the board, refill the hand after it’s exhausted, then make big dumb things to end the game as fast as possible.

The End of the Ramp

Now that you have some ideas and decklists to consider, It’s time to bring this episode to a close.  I hope that this series on Mana in EDH brought you closer to understanding one of the findamental concepts of deckbuilding in this format, and will lead to an increased level of fun when you play this format.  Next time, I’ll be looking at cards from the Magic Orgins spoiler, and evaluating their place in EDH.  I’ll also include my thoughts on what I’ll pre-order from this set.  There are so many legends spoiled already, as well as some juicy standalone cards… I know that Marton Stromgald (above) is really excited about these two red cards:
