Puresteel Paladin Storm “Cheerios” Analysis

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath
 

After a month fooling around with Sram, Senior Edificer and Puresteel Paladin, I’ve realized a few things about the deck.  I discuss my deck for SCG Regionals and what changes I would make.  Further down, I cover the cards I ran and the cards I considered and dismissed.  Lastly, I have a mildly amusing tournament report from SCG Regionals in Orlando.

This is the list I ran at Regionals

Puresteel Paladin Storm

4 Sram, Senior Edificer
4 Puresteel Paladin

4 Accorder’s Shield
4 Bone Saw
1 Grapeshot
4 Kite Shield
4 Mox Opal
2 Noxious Revival
4 Paradise Mantle
4 Retract
4 Serum Visions
2 Swan Song
4 Spidersilk Net

4 Arid Mesa
2 Hallowed Fountain
1 Horizon Canopy
4 Marsh Flats
2 Plains
2 Seachrome Coast

Sideboard
4 Echoing Truth
1 Favor of the Mighty
1 Laboratory Maniac
4 Leyline of Sanctity
1 Paradoxical Outcome
4 Silence

The Mechanics of the Deck

The deck absolutely has to have Sram or Puresteel Paladin in play, so step one is finding a copy of either.  Aggressively mulliganing and Serum Visions are the only ways to accomplish this, making the deck somewhat inconsistent.  After a creature is in play, zero mana equipment are cast to draw cards.  Once all of the available equipment are cast, a Mox Opal and Retract will return them all to continue drawing cards.  This continues until a Storm count of 20 is reached, 2 Mox Opals are drawn, and a Grapeshot is found.

I usually aim to combo off on the third turn.  Getting an additional land drop is important, as it removes the pressure to find a Mox Opal until a second Retract needs to be cast.

Risks and Benefits

Benefits:

  • The deck can win as early as turn 2
  • The deck is relatively unknown (constant attention is changing this)

Risks:

  • The deck can be inconsistent
  • Removal, discard, and counterspells are all problematic

This deck is not for the faint of heart.  The draw to the deck is undeniably the potential for free wins.  Ending the game on the second turn is something worth stopping to consider.  The downsides are real; this deck is a glass cannon.  Disruption is beatable, but you need to locate specific sideboard cards while still assembling a critical mass of Equipment and a creature.

I was a bigger fan of Puresteel Paladin Storm in the early days after Sram, Senior Edificer was spoiled.  After Aether Revolt hit the shelves, several streamers and articles showcased the deck.  This ruined a lot of the surprise value of the deck, and that was a major draw for me.

What I Changed

Added Sacred Foundry for a Marsh Flats
There are a couple reason for this.  Thinning the deck with fetch lands is more important here than usual, but I constantly found myself wishing for another land.  I definitely lost a match because I could not find an additional land on my combo turn (Ulamog had destroyed two of my four lands).  I am opting for Sacred Foundry over another Hallowed Fountain or Plains because of Stony Silence.  There was no way to generate red mana in my deck through a Stony Silence unless a Paradise Mantle was already equipped.  Horizon Canopy or a Seachrome Coast might need to go for that Marsh Flats, but I’m not sure about the best configuration yet.

Moved Swan Song to the board
They are useful when they can be found, but there isn’t a reliable way to find them.  I would rather try a couple Sleight of Hand and board them in if necessary.

Removed Paradoxical Outcome
I’ve already got a ton of cards to board in for the Grixis, Jund, and Abzan matchups.  I never got to cast Paradoxical Outcome, but I have difficulty imagining it playing out well.  Those decks apply pressure fast enough that reaching four mana might be an issue.  I could imagine that I am wrong here, but I haven’t had many opportunities to test these matchups.

Removed Laboratory Maniac
Laboratory Maniac is an answer to Surgical Extraction, Slaughter Games, or Lost Legacy.  Knowing when this card needs to come in is the biggest issue.  Unless you already have insight into your opponent’s sideboard plans, you won’t know if you need an alternative win condition.  You won’t know if you need it until you see a reason in game two and go to game three. It is a good option if you have an extra sideboard slot, but I’m not quite ready to abandon the Swan Songs yet.

 

Card Choices

4 Sram, Senior Edificer
4 Puresteel Paladin
These numbers are not negotiable.  Sram’s legendary status is a negative, but the deck simply doesn’t function without one of them in play.  Beyond that, these creatures are the easiest part of the deck to disrupt, so having backups is not a downside.

 

20 Equipment
Nobody has the right number of equipment for the deck yet, but 20 is fairly close.
This number varies pretty dramatically on the different lists I have seen online, but I prefer a higher number.  The deck has enough trouble comboing off that I want to minimize the risk of fizzling out as much as possible.

 

4 Retract
Retract is the card that really makes this deck work.  If it didn’t exist, Sram or Puresteel Paladin would only draw a handful of cards before hitting a string of lands and creatures.  Retract makes a Storm count of 20 realistic.

 

4 Mox Opal
Another critical combo piece, Mox Opal casts Retract when you are generating Storm count.  Mox Opal also provides the necessary mana to cast Grapeshot at the end of the chain.  Beyond that, it also helps compensate for the low land count in the deck.

 

1 Grapeshot
This is the win condition for the deck.  After generating 20+ Storm from various equipment, you can Grapeshot the opponent for lethal.

 

2 Noxious Revival
Noxious Revival adds some virtual copies of Retract to the deck.  Once you start drawing large numbers of cards, the only risk is failing to find enough copies of Retract.  Noxious Revival helps bolster the number of Retracts in the deck.  It also has some random utility like rebuying fetch lands before your combo turn and fizzling a Snapcaster Mage trigger.

 

4 Serum Visions
Serum Visions gives the deck a proactive way to set up for the combo.  It also gives the extra Mox Opals some utility as you start the chain of card drawing.

 

2 Sleight of Hand
These were added in place of the Swan Songs.  A little more consistency for this deck should go a long way.  These are probably the first things that should be boarded out for protection spells.

 

15 Lands
Reaching two lands is really the only critical mana requirement for the deck, so the low land count is reasonable.  Fetch lands are extremely important, since pulling lands out of the deck matters more when you plan to see 80% of your deck every game.  The deck can afford a mix of Seachrome Coast and Horizon Canopy, but I’m still not sure of the specific numbers.

 

4 Echoing Truth
This is the catch-all answer for whatever permanents the opponent has that impede the combo.  Cards like Eidolon of the Great Revel, Thalia, Guardian of Thraben, Leyline of Sanctity, Chalice of the Void, Rule of Law, Engineered Explosives, and Stony Silence are difficult or impossible to win through.  Echoing Truth takes care of all of them, and I can’t imagine running anything less than four.

 

1 Favor of the Mighty
A protection spell against Jund and Abzan.  I don’t like it against Grixis, since Tasigur, the Golden Fang and Gurmag Angler invalidate it.  This is a flex spot, and could easily be changed for something else.

 

4 Leyline of Sanctity
As Echoing Truth is the answer for permanents, Leyline of Sanctity is the answer for hand disruption.  This comes in against the Inquisition of Kozilek, Thoughtseize, and Collective Brutality decks.  I want it against Burn too, but I don’t have that sideboard plan worked out yet.  This seems like another four slots of the sideboard locked in place for the foreseeable future.

 

4 Silence
Like the other 4x cards in the sideboard, Silence is the answer for opposing counterspells and removal spells.  Even if it gets countered, the opponent is unlikely to have another along with the mana to cast it.

 

2 Swan Song
I wasn’t quite ready to cut Swan Song from the deck, so they got moved to the sideboard.  I can see this changing in the future, but they serve as passable copies of Silence along with protecting against discard spells and Eidolon of the Great Revel.

 

Cards Not Played


These could take the place of Seachrome Coast, but I don’t think they help.  If the opponent has a counterspell, there are plenty of other targets that will still cause the deck to fizzle.  Spell Snare is the only card that Cavern of Souls can blank.

 


Pretty sure this deck doesn’t need more speed in exchange for volatility.  Only tapping for colorless land is also a negative with Puresteel Paladin’s double white cost.

 


Goblin Gaveleer is part of a package from before Sram, Senior Edificer was printed.  It is fragile and adds a lot of unnecessary extra pieces to the combo.  Grapeshot is the only win condition the deck needs.

 


This is competing with Noxious Revival as backup for Retract.  The benefits of Noxious Revival are that it doesn’t cost mana and has several utility features.  Hurkyl’s Recall costs an extra mana, requiring an extra Mox Opal or delaying the combo by a turn.  On the plus side, Hurkyl’s Recall doesn’t require you to find a Retract before it does anything.  It also gives you an out to Chalice of the Void and can randomly wreck Affinity.  This might deserve a place instead of the second Noxious Revival.

 


Another part of the old backup win conditions, Mass Hysteria exists to give Goblin Gaveleer, Monastery Mentor, and Monk tokens haste for an immediate win.  This is another relic of previous builds, and not necessary now.

 


Monastery Mentor is the best alternative win condition, but it still isn’t necessary.  Things have to go extremely well for Monastery Mentor to deal lethal damage, needing 4-5+ prowess triggers on two consecutive turns.  That isn’t going to be possible without Retract, since casting Equipment without Sram or a Paladin doesn’t draw a replacement card.  Card selection like Serum Visions to facilitate the main combo is a better use of deck slots.

 


For people who really like to play fast and loose, Pact of Negation offers free protection on the combo turn.  I don’t have enough faith in my combo turns that I would be willing to lose the game if it doesn’t work.  I could believe the deck actually wants copies of this card, but I need a few hundred more games under my belt before I would even consider it.

 


Repeal offers some protection against Eidolon of the Great Revel, Thalia, Guardian of Thraben, Chalice of the Void, and any other problematic permanent that your opponent can come up with.  It can also bounce a Mox Opal to replace itself for free.  I think Sleight of Hand is more useful setting up the combo than Repeal is at offering protection, but I could be wrong.  I haven’t gotten to try Sleight of Hand in the deck, and Repeal might offer the deck enough versatility to merit a slot or two.

 

Unused Sideboard Options


This deck should be faster than the graveyard decks.  If it isn’t (Goryo’s Vengeance), you might not have had time to cast Rest in Peace anyway.  I’d stick to Swan Song to fight this kind of deck.

 


A useful card for the burn matchup.  It doubles as protection for Sram or a Paladin or turning off an Eidolon of the Great Revel for a turn.  However, there are already a ton of cards to board in against burn, and only so many cards that can come out.

 


If you don’t already have three artifacts in play, Dispatch will cause you to lose Equipment for your combo turn.  Path to Exile is more reliable for creature removal.

 


Dispel is really just a worse version of Swan Song.  The bird token shouldn’t ever matter.

 


Erayo, Soratami Ascendant is in a weird place for the deck.  Flipping it is great, but you essentially need to burn all the equipment in your hand to do it.  Since they won’t replace themselves without a Sram or Paladin, you delay the combo by several turns.  If you start to combo off before you flip it, you won’t get the benefit until after they could counter Sram or the Paladin.  There just isn’t a good place to weave Erayo into the deck without disrupting the main plan.

 


If there is an aggressive deck that can race Puresteel Paladin Storm, there are Fog options in white.  I don’t think the deck needs them.

 


An alternate win condition, but a rather fragile one.  There isn’t a way to remove blockers, so it seems doubtful that Geist of Saint Traft will survive to swing multiple times.  It is also weak to Liliana of the Veil, a planeswalker that is already good against the deck.

 


I could believe that Ghirapur Aether Grid is the backup card that Paradoxical Outcome wants to be.  On the other hand, getting enough artifacts in play to deal with something like a Tarmogoyf is probably too much to hope for.  This might be a goodoption against controlling decks, but there aren’t many that don’t run Tarmogoyf, Gurmag Angler, or Tasigur the Golden Fang.  Ghirapur Aether Grid is slower than all of those, and it can’t realistically answer them.

 


Pretty much the same as Rest in Peace, but it also gets bounced by Retract.

 


This is the best option if you need creature removal.  I feel like Echoing Truth does this job already, and it covers a wider range of problems.  Path to Exile does handle a problem permanently, so it might make my sideboard if I move the Swan Songs back to the main deck.

 


Worse than Swan Song against the cards you care about.

 


Spellskite doesn’t really offer protection, since it will get bounced when you cast the first Retract.  I was excited until I realized this.  Stay away from this card.

 

 

A Case Study on the Readiness of Bonesaw
– a SCG Regionals Tournament Report

 


Sram, Senior Edificer has dominated my brain and displaced my sound judgment.  Visions of Bone Saws and assorted draft trash consume my every waking though, and I just can’t seem to let go.  After days of Aetherflux Reservoir brewing, it is time to descend into the true madness of Modern.

 

After scouring the depths of mtgsalvation.com and returning mostly unscathed, I have a starting point.  Aether Revolt prerelease is still a week away, but some Sharpie art can let me begin the testing process.  I quickly switch over to printed proxies as I struggle to identify proxies and cards actually in the deck that I would normally use for proxies.
I think there is actually something here.  Thoughts of turn two wins, once just a mere fantasy, actually begin to materialize.  The deck isn’t perfect, but I’m ready to believe.  Time to pull the trigger.

Oh, and a few extra stops.  Why does nobody have Sigil of Distinction?  That can’t possibly be a thing in any other deck.  Does Commander even want that card?  What am I doing with my life?
About a week out from Regionals, disaster strikes.  Zac Elsik, GP winner with Lantern Control, tweets about a successful run with Cheerios.  As attention to Cheerios grows, my confidence falls.  A SCG deck tech and CFB article ensure that most players won’t suffer from the confusion I was relying on when I made my deck choice.  Still, I don’t have a backup deck that feels better positioned, so I register a full bowl of Cheerios.

Round 1: Lost to RG Tron 1-2
I split the first two games with an easy win and a loss to my deck failing to deliver.  Game three culminated with an Ulamog, the Ceaseless Hunger destroying two lands and I lost because there wasn’t a 5th fetchable land in the deck.  My goal of running hotter than the sun has already been dashed.
0-1

Round 2: Lost to Blue Tron 0-2
I thought I was supposed to breeze through all the Tron decks that have flooded the room.  Not so after a mulligan to oblivion in the first game and a fizzled combo turn combined with an Oblivion Stone in the second game.  I am becoming nervous that the rumors are true; Bonesaw just wasn’t ready this morning.
0-2

Round 3: Won against Merfolk 2-0
In game 2, I had to Grapeshot a Lord of Atlantis to avoid death to buy time, but I faced no interaction in either game for a couple easy wins.  While the match was easy, it poses hard moral questions.  What kind of monster have I become?  My opponent shifted from bored to annoyed to visibly upset over the course of the match.  What was the real cost of my victory?  I wonder if I should even care, sitting at the back of the room aware of my nonexistent chance of making the elimination rounds.  Whatever, I’m on the scoreboard.
1-2

Round 4: Won against Jeskai Nahiri 2-0
Swan Song in game 1 and Silence with a luck topdeck in game 2 wrapped things up nicely.  Bonesaw apparently slumbers before noon and might finally be ready.
2-2

Round 5: Lost to Abzan 1-2
Elation after a quick win fades as removal, Engineered Explosives, and Stony Silence take the match.  Afterword, my opponent mentions significant testing against my deck as a teammate of his is playing it.  Earlier reporting was a false alarm, Bonesaw isn’t ready.  There might still be time to serve someone a balanced breakfast of Cheerios.
2-3

Round 6: Won against Infect 2-1
Lost in spectacular fashion in game one as a Spellskite attacked for 20 damage on turn three (2xMight of old Krosa, Twisted Image, Become Immense, and 2x Noble Hierarch triggers). Game three was won off the back of double Silence and Sram/Puresteel Paladin beatdown. A minor recovery might be in the works, Bonesaw put on an impressive display.
3-3

Round 7: Won against Mono Green Elves 2-1
My deck failed me in game one, but my faster clock easily won the next two games.  Positive Record!  Bonesaw is Ready!!!
4-3

Round 8: Lost to Grixis control 0-2
My draws were mediocre draws and my opponent was prepared with plenty of disruption.  False alarm, Bonesaw wasn’t ready.
4-4

Final results: A full day appears to be too much, Bonesaw’s readiness is still in question.

    Hey! Dunno if you’re still playing around with the deck at all, but I had some tips =)

    1) Your initial analysis of Paradoxical Outcome is off, but the end result in the current meta is the same. Against non-Shadow BGx, it actually turns the matchup from unfavorable to favorable. It dodges IoK, blanks removal, and is a live topdeck vs Lily. Resolving one EoT is essentially game over as you just rebuild so quickly and powerfully that they can’t continue to fight it 1-for-1.

    HOWEVER, against Death’s Shadow…it’s too slow. Their clock is much faster and more reliable (as far as reliably having a strong clock), and Outcome is just not a viable option. Leyline is slightly better against them, but it still has the issue of being a dead draw when going off, and making mulligan decisions terrible. My advice is to stick to Swan Songs, and maybe Silences if you feel that they are particularly removal-leaning rather than discard.

    2) I play 6 fetchables, personally; I’ve seen streamers run into trouble forgetting that they only had 4 or 5 targets before cracking their fetch. I intend to add a Sacred Foundry soon, but for now it’s just the full 4 Fountains and 2 Plains. Never run a land that doesn’t cast Paladin.

    3) You need to play one maindeck Hurkyl’s. I was on and off it for a while, but Taufik Indrakesuma, who made an impressive Day 1 finish in Brisbane, broke down the math behind it in a very good article here: http://www.mtgmintcard.com/articles/writers/taufik-indrakesuma/calculating-cheerios (This also covers your own speculation on equipment numbers, in case you wanted to see some calculations on that)

    It’s incredibly important as a 5th Retract, and has niche applications in bouncing Affinity boards, problem permanents such as Chalice and Explosives, and the occasional 10/10 Inkmoth Nexus. Also, it is NOT competing with Revival; this is very important. At least one, probably 2 Revivals are always necessary. It’s ridiculously flexible and allows for surprising tech plays, redundancy against removal and discard, and as you mentioned, cute land drop shenanigans for those silly one-land keeps.

    4) I’m personally still playing with Swan Songs maindeck and quite happy about it, but moving it to the board is not unreasonable. If you’re fiddling with numbers, I would also try bumping it up to 3 or 4 maindeck and seeing how that feels; I don’t think it’s “right”, just, you know, see what’s best for you.

    5) You don’t talk much about your sideboard plans, but my advice (if you aren’t already doing this) is to sideboard very little. ALWAYS bring in one Echoing Truth. Bring in more against decks packed with problem permanents. Almost always side out Serum Visions. I know it sounds crazy, but the smoothing is not worth it in games 2 and 3 unless you’re up against decks with heavy hand disruption. (In your build, side out Sleights first obviously.) As a general rule, you can side down to about 18 equipments, or 17 if you really, really need something. I very, very rarely bring in more than 4 or 5 cards, and the 5th is usually a very tough cut.

    6) Monastery Mentor is always my go-to secondary wincon. Going wide is so powerful when everyone has turned their decks into turbo-precision lasers aimed at one dude. The math also gets out of hand a lot quicker than you’re giving it credit for; swinging for lethal with monks is almost hard not to do the turn after you drop him. The important thing is to figure out how many cards you need to drop now (i.e. how many monks you need) and how many to hold for next turn (i.e. how big to make them). If you make 4 monks, and then drop your remaining 2 equipments next turn, that’s 16 damage with 8 power on board next turn.

    The other huge advantage to Mentor is that you are guaranteed a Monk token, meaning they have to 2-for-1 themselves to deal with your guy. The monks aren’t as threatening as their teacher, but they still represent a frightening clock.

    Anyways, hope this has been at least a little helpful, and I hope you enjoy this deck a lot =)
