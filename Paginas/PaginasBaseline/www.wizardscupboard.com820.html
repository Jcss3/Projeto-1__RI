Eldritch Moon Brews

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath

 

My original plan was to write about a Humans brew I was working on for Modern, but it ran into a few problems.  Thalia, Heretic Cathar was spoiled about halfway through my initial testing, and I wasn’t sure if I should wait for more spoilers.  Nothing else exciting came about, but there were some problems in the first few rounds of testing.  I decided to shelf it for a while to give myself some time for some new ideas.

Next, I started working on a Spirits deck.  This original idea has since been done in approximately 1.86 billion other articles, also known as every article about brews since the full spoilers were released.  I decided a full article about card choices for a Spirits deck wasn’t very interesting.

Since then, I’ve been thinking on several deck concepts using Eldritch Moon cards.  Instead of going deep on a specific deck, I’m just going to go over several of my ideas.

 

Standard

Spirits

First up, let’s start out with the obligatory Spirits list.

1 Bygone Bishop
2 Elder Deep-Fiend
4 Mausoleum Wanderer
2 Neblgast Herald
4 Rattlechains
4 Reflector Mage
4 Selfless Spirit
4 Spell Queller
2 Archangel Avacyn
 
3 Clash of Wills
3 Ojutai’s Command
1 Unsubstantiate
1 Essence Flux
 
4 Prairie Stream
4 Port Town
2 Meandering River
7 Plains
8 Islands

 

This is the list I expect to play for the first week or two of Eldritch Moon Standard.  I’ve always regretted not being able to play Faeries when it was in Standard (couldn’t afford it then), and this looks to be the next best thing.

For those that were wondering, I spent that season casting Wizened Cenn while other people were casting Cryptic Command, Bitterblossom, and Cruel Ultimatum.  I did not do much winning that season.

Back to the Spirit deck.  I’ve trimmed down on some of the heavy Spirit themed cards like Essence Flux and Neblgast Herald since I’ve moved the top end towards Elder Deep-Fiend and Archangel Avacyn.  I want the deck to play like a Faeries deck, so I can’t imagine skipping those cards.

There are a couple cards I wanted to include, but I didn’t know what to cut.  One or two copies of Stasis Snare seems really appealing, but I’m hoping the counter magic will help alleviate that concern.  Anafenza, Kin-Tree Spirit works well with Mausoleum Wanderer, but I don’t really want to push that far into White.  Especially since Thalia, Heretic Cathar will already be somewhat punishing to the manabase and Flash aspect of this deck.

I’m the least sure about omitting Anafenza, Kin-Tree Spirit from my initial list.  Liliana, the Last Hope tears through a ton of creatures in this deck.

There is probably another version of this deck that goes harder on Elder Deep-Fiends.  I expect that list plays Matter Reshaper and Eldrazi Displacer, stretching the mana for <> sources.  I really like Thraben Inspector and Knight of the White Orchid in that kind of deck.

 

Elder Deep-Fiend

I’ll just go straight to the deck.

2 Pilgrim’s Eye
4 Elder Deep-Fiend
4 Eldrazi Skyspawner
4 Matter Reshaper
1 Thought-Knot Seer
3 Wretched Gryff
4 Jace, Vryn’s Prodigy
 
4 Kozilek’s Return
4 Spatial Contortion
2 Titan’s Presence
3 Tormenting Voice
 
4 Islands
2 Mountains
2 Battlefield Forge
1 Evolving Wilds
4 Sanctum of Ugin
4 Shivan Reef
4 Wandering Fumarole
3 Yavimaya Coast

 

Gerry Thompson wrote about this deck last week, and I will be surprised if this isn’t a powerful option going into the early weeks of Eldritch Moon Standard.  This list makes great use of Elder Deep-Fiend, Kozilek’s Return, and Sanctum of Ugin.   Chaining Elder Deep-Fiends should go a long way towards shutting down a ton of decks.  If the decks are aggressive enough to get under that, Kozilek’s Return (and it’s trigger) should be enough to get the job done.

Expect to see a lot of Elder Deep-Fiends in the next year and a half.

 

Bant Collected Company

Spell Queller is a messed up Magic card.  Collected Company decks will have to adapt to the new threat, likely along with playing it themselves.  The Humans variants probably can’t afford the loss of synergy, but the other version is under no such restraints.

3 Bounding Krasis
4 Duskwatch Recruiter
1 Lambholt Pacifist
4 Reflector Mage
4 Spell Queller
4 Sylvan Advocate
3 Tireless Tracker
2 Thalia, Heretic Cathar
2 Archangel Avacyn
 
4 Collected Company
4 Dromoka’s Command
1 Tamiyo, Field Researcher

 

I’ll need some games with Tamiyo, Field Researcher before it’s clear what the correct number of copies should be.  Most everything else is fairly clear.  Spell Queller seems awesome, so I want a full set of them.  Thalia, Heretic Cathar is good against opposing Collected Companies, so a couple should be a good fit here.  They are better in the Bant Humans deck, but this list makes sufficient use of the abilities.

 

Grim Flayer

I’m not sure what to think of this card.  Sylvan Advocate completely bricks Grim Flayer, which seems like an terrible obstacle for Grim Flayer to overcome.  For Grim Flayer to be good, there needs to be a downturn in popularity of Collected Company and Tokens decks that run Sylvan Advocate.  Both of those are proven decks, but it is possible that Thalia, Heretic Cathar has something to say about both decks.  Collected Company decks have a weak manabase for Thalia to exploit.  Nissa and Garruk are also much more susceptible if an opposing Thalia is making each token enter the battlefield tapped.

If all that happens and Sylvan Advocate’s presence starts to diminish, Grim Flayer might find a home.  That home needs to be fairly aggressive, since there are better ways to load up a graveyard if that is the only goal.  Grim Flayer is only good if you really want that underpriced 4/4 creature.  That means you want a deck that focuses on more of a Mindwrack Demon theme instead of Ishkanah, Grafwidow grindy game.  That seems like its all well and good, but Reflector Mage has a lot to say about Mindwrack Demon’s chances of success.

Overall, I don’t like Grim Flayer’s prospects in the near future.

 

Emrakul, the Promised End

It’s been several years since you could Mindslaver someone in Standard.  Lots of people have forgotten, or just don’t know, exactly what that feels like.  I’d love to be the one to remind or enlighten them.

That said, I’m less excited now than I was at the start of the spoiler season.  Emrakul seemed great in a format where people were stalling out games with the Tokens deck.  Between Elder Deep-Fiend and Spell Queller, there will be a lot more games where one person just doesn’t actually get to play spells.  Emrakul takes some work to cast, and losing a turn or two might be too much to handle.

Green and Black seems like an ideal place to start with Emrakul, the Promised End, but I want to look elsewhere.  Many of their Delirium cards are focused towards midrange cards, and there really isn’t a way to get rid of clunkers like extra copies Emrakul, the Promised End.  I’m interested in looking towards a different kind of deck

1 Gisela, the Broken Blade
1 Bruna, the Fading Light
1 Emraukl, the Promised End
 
3 Magmatic Insight
2 Terrarion
4 Fiery Impulse
4 Tormenting Voice
3 Declaration in Stone
3 Radiant Flames
4 Hedron Archive
4 Nahiri, the Harbringer
2 Pyromancer’s Goggles
2 Chandra, Flamecaller
1 Descent Upon the Sinful

 

Well, there are some flaws with that list.  Nahiri, the Harbringer and Tormenting Voice are doing some pretty heavy lifting for Delerium.  The Instant and Creature counts are both a little low for Delirium too.  Jace, Vryn’s Prodigy helps solve both of those problems

4 Jace, Vryn’s Prodigy
1 Gisela, the Broken Blade
1 Bruna, the Fading Light
1 Emraukl, the Promised End
 
4 Fiery Impulse
4 Tormenting Voice
3 Declaration in Stone
3 Radiant Flames
4 Hedron Archive
4 Nahiri, the Harbringer
2 Pyromancer’s Goggles
2 Chandra, Flamecaller
1 Descent Upon the Sinful

 

I think these decks are playable, but they focus less on Emrakul, the Promised End than I would like.  Maybe an Ishkanah, Grafwidow shell is a better place to start.

Sam Black played this deck in the MOCS earlier this year.  There was a lot of buzz around the deck initially, but nothing ever materialized from it.  He wrote about the deck later, mainly commenting that the Lightning Bolts were poor removal spells for the decks he faced.

I’m interested in taking this shell in a slightly different direction.

4 Tarmogoyf
3 Courser of Kruphix
2 Sakura-Tribe Elder
1 Eternal Witness
1 Haakon, Stormgald Scourge
1 Shriekmaw
2 Emrakul, the Promised End
 
4 Mishra’s Bauble
2 Thoughtseize
4 Inquisition of Kozilek
4 Traverse the Ulvenwald
3 Nameless Inversion
2 Abrupt Decay
1 Seal of Doom
4 Liliana of the Veil

 

This one might be a little rough around the edges, but I like where it is going.  I might have gotten a little too cute the Traverse the Ulvenwald targets, but there are reasons for each.  Thragtusk might be better as a copy of Kalitas, but I was concerned that the deck might need an immediate life boost.  Shriekmaw just lets Traverse tutor for a Doom Blade.  Lastly, Haakon, Stormgald Scourge + Nameless Inversion is miserable to play through if the game goes long enough.  The deck was already interested in a Tribal card, so it seemed like a great excuse to play it.

I think Emrakul is a good enough draw without Traverse the Ulvenwald to play multiple copies.  If that doesn’t prove to be the case, I will immediately shave a copy.  Either way, I like the foundation of this deck.  It has almost all of the power of a regular Jund deck with a much more impressive top end.

Lastly, if Whispers of Emrakul is ever going to find a home in Modern, this will be the deck.  I didn’t want to include it in the initial run since I used so much space for the Traverse package.  If I trim that down, Whispers of Emrakul will be next up for a few test runs.

 

Bedlam Reveler

Delver has been a strategy people continually try in Modern.  It was incredibly good when Treasure Cruise was legal, and usually poor otherwise.  I’ve played a lot of Delver in Legacy, and the Modern equivalent always felt flawed.  In Legacy, you would resolve a cheap threat and use counters to protect it or disrupt your opponent long enough to deal twenty damage.  Modern doesn’t have free counter magic, so protecting your threats will delay you at least a turn.  That delay isn’t really acceptable in Modern.

I was hoping that Bedlam Reveler could fix that problem.  Ideally, you wouldn’t mind your Delver of Secrets dying since Bedlam Reveler could fill your hand back up and carry you across the finish line.  In actuality, my deck felt like a subpar Delver deck mixed up with a subpar Burn deck.  Bedlam Reveler might belong in a dedicated Delver deck, but I’m not the guy to build that deck.  I want something a little more resilient than Delver of Secrets if I want to play a “Protect the Queen” kind of strategy.

 

Tamiyo, Field Researcher

I’ve been joking about a Doubling Season brew for Modern for years now.  I wanted to ramp out a Doubling Season and then Jace, Architect of Thought into an Emrakul, the Aeons Torn and the best card out of my opponent’s deck.  Or combine that with Wheel of Sun and Moon to recycle my Jaces and take every single spell from my opponent’s deck.

The majority of the “work” that went into that brew was years ago.  Nahiri, the Harbringer adds a whole new dimension to the deck.  Oath of Nissa looks real appealing too, even if it can’t find Doubling Season.

I wasn’t the only person considering Doubling Season in Modern.  Corbin Hosler recently featured it in a video series.

I won’t lie, parts of this deck look like a hot mess.  I like the idea of pushing Blood Moon, but he only ran two copies.  I won’t even begin to touch some of the Planeswalker choices.  I’ll trust that he had a plan, but I certainly don’t know it.

I’m going to borrow his frame for the mana creatures, but I’ll mostly be going my own way.  My first draft of a Doubling Season deck would look a little more like this.

4 Birds of Paradise
4 Noble Hierarch
4 Lotus Cobra
2 Sylvan Caryatid
1 Emrakul, the Aeon’s Torn
 
4 Oath of Nissa
4 Doubling Season
4 Blood Moon
 
4 Nahiri, the Harbringer
4 Jace, Architect of Thought
2 Tamiyo, Field Researcher
23 Lands (No Nykthos, Shrine to Nyx or Alchemist Refuge)

 

Doubling Season with either Nahiri or Jace will result in an immediate Emrakul attack, so those cards are all maxed out.  Doubling Season is pretty awful in multiples, but there aren’t any reasonable ways to tutor for it.  Nahiri is phenomenal, even without Doubling Season.  She can pitch any redundant copies of cards while naturally building towards an ultimate.  Jace is less exciting on its own, but it is still a perfectly serviceable Magic card.

Even though this deck is supposed to feature Tamiyo, Field Researcher, I only put two in the deck.  That’s just a casualty of how mediocre the card is going to be without a Doubling Season.  This deck does not have good attacking creatures, so you are depending on the combo to generate some value.

Sylvan Caryatid might be better as Devoted Druid.  Caryatid is the more explosive ramp card, and Lotus Cobra has already set us in that direction.  Wall of Roots is another option if some early game defense is needed.

Overall, this deck looks more fun than good.  It doesn’t have room for many interactive cards, so decks like Affinity and Infect are just going to roll over this brew.  A barrage of Thoughtseizes and Liliana of the Veil activations might be difficult to overcome too.  Maybe Blood Moon can overcome all of those problems.  Maybe the deck wants a couple copies of Fog.  Plenty of testing will be needed if anyone wants to brave Modern with this brew.

 

After all of that, I wound up with a few decks I am actually interested in trying.  I’ll be playing the  Spirits deck for the first few weeks of standard, because it seems like a great choice filled with strong cards.  In Modern, I’m very interested in the Golgari Traverse the Ulvenwald deck.  I’ll be looking to sleeve that one up for some local shenanigans.  I’ll probably be going into that list and possibly Humans in Modern in my next article.  If you want to hear about those, let me know!  You can always find me at @DoneTheMath
