Kaladesh Rares and Mythics

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath
 

Welcome to the Kaladesh Mythics and Rares review.  In my previous reviews, I have covered every Rare and Mythic card from a new set.  This time, I’m going to go ahead and skip the chaff so you don’t have to scroll as far.  I’ll be hitting just the good ones and the ones that need a little extra discussion.  Let’s get started.

 

There have already been attempts at making a Storm deck with Aetherflux Reservoir.  Paradoxical Outcome is the other key component, letting you go off with about 4 zero cost cards.  Cathar’s Shield and Bone Saw feature prominently in these lists.  Crush of Tentacles gives another decent win condition, or at least a way to stall the board for a few turns.  I don’t expect much success above the FNM level, but it seems fun.

 

White really didn’t get much to work with for Energy.  Aetherstorm Roc might see play as some kind of midrange trump, but there are just so many better options at four mana.  The best hope for Roc is the Energy generating trigger, but Decoction Module is probably better at the same job.

 

Aetherworks Marvel needs so many things to go right if it is going to go off.  All of the following need to happen

-Find a copy of Aetherworks Marvel in your deck
-Generate four mana to cast Aetherworks Marvel
-Generate six energy
-Have enough expensive threats to reliably hit off of Aetherworks Marvel’s ability
 

The real trick is generating the six energy.  You can use Aetherworks Marvel’s ability for cards going to the graveyard, but there aren’t a lot of synergies for that in Standard currently.  If you don’t use Aetherworks Marvel’s trigger, you have to play other cards with the intent to gain energy from them.  At that point, isn’t it just easier to play ramp spells and cast the expensive cards in your deck?  This all just seems like a poor way to reinvent the wheel for ramp decks.

 

There are formats where Angel of Invention would be sweet, but I don’t think this is one of them.  There are a ton of great five mana cards in Standard at the moment, so the competition is fierce.  More importantly, losing a five mana creature to Liliana, the Last Hope’s plus trigger seems horrendous.

 

The biggest draw of this card feels like protection for control decks after casting Fumigate.  Authority of the Consuls will prevent any new creatures from Crewing a Vehicle, so you will actually get a turn to recover.  The extra life gain is just a bonus to help justify spending a card on this effect.

 

All of the fast lands are great.  They might be a slight downgrade from the pain lands from Magic Origins, since the Eldrazi need <> mana.  Even so, lands that produce multiple colors of mana and can come into play untapped always see some amount of play.  These will all be important for the next 18 months in Standard and even longer in Modern.

 

I suppose this is an upgrade on Village Messenger, but I’m not exactly sure.  Bomat Courier is somewhere between decent and very good on turn one.  On about turn three or later, it’s embarrassing.  If you are looking to end the game after about five to seven turns, Bomat Courier could be worth a consideration.  At that point, it is good about 50% of the time, or whenever it is in your opening hand.

The discard has synergy with Madness, but Bomat Courier isn’t going to be good unless you draw some cards when you discard.

 

This card is great.  See Blooming Marsh

 

Red and green have several aggressive Energy cards.  Bristling Hydra might be the top end to a deck like that.  A four mana 4/3 that blanks a removal spell is a pretty good deal, and extra energy can make it into a gigantic threat.  This might be good enough as a stand alone card for any green deck, but I do expect it to find a home in an Energy deck.

 

There has been a lot of hype around Cataclysmic Gearhulk.  Personally, it seems like the kind of card you can play one or two copies of without being embarrassed.  It isn’t great unless your opponent is going wide like the old G/W Tokens deck.  Tragic Arrogance was strong because you got to pick the opponent’s targets.  Leaving an opponent with their best creature instead of their worst might not be worth the 4/5 body left behind from Cataclysmic Gearhulk.  It does make a better maindeck card, since you can still cast it for the creature if the ability isn’t that good.

 

Wizards of the Coast clearly wanted to push the similarities between Chandra, Torch of Defiance and Jace, the Mind Sculptor.  Similar art, four abilities, and power pushed in unsubtle ways make the comparison difficult to ignore.   Chandra isn’t quite at the same level as Jace, but it is going to be a defining feature of Standard for the next 18 months.

I was going to discuss each ability on Chandra, but that’s going to stretch on forever.  The card is great.  I want to go over a few important points instead.

Chandra, Torch of Defiance doesn’t slot into every single deck.  Highly aggressive decks won’t be able to defend Chandra, making it difficult to use the -3 ability.

Chandra’s -3 ability is going to have a large impact on cards in Standard.  If you are playing a creature with four or less toughness that doesn’t do something spectacular when it enters play, you should reevaluate your choice.  Kalitas, Traitor of Ghet is going to look awful in the coming months.  Consider the effects of Reflector Mage on midsized creatures (Mindwrack Demon).  Several of them have been pushed almost completely out of the format.  Chandra could easily have a similar effect.

Chandra’s damage trigger on the first ability is great for getting chip shots on planeswalkers.  Don’t forget that.

 

The average converted mana cost in the Temur Emerge decks is a little over 2.  That means that, a lot of the time, Combustible Gearhulk will be a six mana 6/6 that deals six damage when it enters play.  The damage will vary wildly, but that is a good starting point.  If your deck doesn’t want that, you probably don’t want Combustible Gearhulk.

Filagree Familiar will likely be covering the artifact quota in the Emerge/Delerium decks, so Combustible Gearhulk won’t be getting a free pass based on card types.

Don’t ever play Combustible Gearhulk with the expectation of drawing cards.  The opponent will almost always opt to take the damage.

 

This card is great.  See Blooming Marsh

 

The last time a true Mind Control was legal, it didn’t see much play.  Standard was a very different beast at that time, and card evaluations have changed.  This is close enough to Mind Control that it could see some play.  It isn’t strong enough to warrant a deck being built around it.  However, it could be the answer a deck is looking for if some midrange creature or artifact (Vehicle) starts to dominate the format.

 

I don’t think this is good enough for Standard, but there could be a deck out there that really needs some mana acceleration.

 

If you are playing a deck with Swamps and Traverse the Ulvenwald, you should be playing a copy of Demon of Dark Schemes somewhere in your 75.  It is a great answer for aggressive decks, particularly since some decks can tutor for a single copy.

 

Vehicles look to be a Standard contender, and Depala, Pilot Exemplar seems like an easy include.  She can crew just about any Vehicle on her own, pumps all of your Vehicles, pumps at least some of your creatures, and provides a mana sink if you start to flood out.  All of that on a 3/3 creature for three mana.  I’m not sure what else I can say about Depala, Pilot Exemplar, the card seems great.

 

My overall verdict on Dovin Baan boils down to a fine role player card, but he will never be the flagship card in a deck.  Dovin Baan is going to work like Ob Nixilas Reignited in decks, providing card advantage and creature removal, albeit in different ways.  The mana cost is shaved by one, but the creature removal is far less permanent.

There are a lot of similarities to Kiora, the Crashing Wave, but Dovin Baan fits in a typical Azorius control deck.  Simic decks don’t have a typical mold to follow, and often exist because of a specific set of cards or interactions.  That means that Dovin Baan has a more obvious home than Kiora, the Crashing Wave had.  That said, Reflector Mage and Spell Queller are the best Azorius cards right now, and neither of them are at their best in the typical Azorius control shell.

 

Any home for this will be dubious at best.  Stay far, far away.

 

Still more wacky rare artifacts.  Dynavolt Tower does a good job of generating Energy in a spell heavy deck.  It also provides a decent outlet for that Energy if you plan on spell based damage killing your opponent.  I’m not sure if this is the most efficient direction for the Thermo-Alchemist deck for the new format, but it is worth considering.

 

Fateful Showdown has some potential as a burn spell, but it will need a specific home.  It enable Madness, but Fiery Temper is really the only beneficiary from that angle.  On turn four, this will probably be dealing about five damage, and you will be replacing your hand with something approximately the same.  If your hand is already good, it can just wait for you to burn through your good spells and help with any additional lands.

I imagine that Fateful Showdown will at least make an appearance in whatever Fevered Visions deck appears in Kaladesh Standard.

 

Fleetwheel Cruiser is the real deal.  I don’t think it is the best Vehicle in the set (Smuggler’s Copter), but it should be a close runner up.  Five power worth of haste damage is an important number with Chandra, Torch of Defiance entering Standard.  More than that, five damage to the face is a great recovery from something like Fumigate.  Unlike every other Vehicle, Fleetwheel Cruiser doesn’t need a pilot on it’s first turn.  This Vehicle seems like a reasonable top end for any aggressive deck.

 

This will be the default Wrath effect in the new Standard.  Planar Outburst will still be legal, but the Awaken ability was never very relevant.  Fumigate’s life gain will let you push the limits on how long you can wait to Wrath a board away, so it should be able to clean up more things.  That said, Wraths are looking to be pretty poor in a world filled with Vehicles, Elder Deep Fiend, and a suite of powerful new planeswalkers.  If Wraths are a reliable strategy at all, I am confident that Fumigate will be replacing Planar Outburst.

 

If you are a grindy black deck, Gonti, Lord of Luxury is the guy for you.  This is a 2/3 Deathtouch creature that draws you a card for four mana.  That’s a pretty great deal.  Gonti, Lord of Luxury is pretty much a guaranteed two for one, not too far off from Baleful Strix.  The card selection from the top four of your opponent’s deck should make up for the small chance to hit all lands on top of getting spells from their deck instead of yours.

If you are looking for a Read the Bones replacement and don’t have a ton of four cost cards (or have too many three costs cards), Gonti will be a good fit.

 

This card is great.  See Blooming Marsh

 

Lands that do things are always worth a look, since the opportunity cost for playing them is so low.  Inventor’s Fair will help you cast your cards early, since that’s what lands do.  Later in the game, you can cash Inventor’s Fair in for an artifact.  Getting the necessary number of artifacts to trigger Inventor’s Fair will be a challenge, but Inventor’s Fair will probably be the best land in decks that can manage it.  Even if you don’t need another artifact immediately, the life buffer is a nice bonus for saving Inventor’s Fair for later.

People will probably try Inventor’s Fair in Modern Affinity, but it won’t be good there.  The lifegain is not very relevant in Affinity, and the tutor ability costs too much mana.  However, Lantern Control will be happy to play Inventor’s Fair.  Both abilities are extremely relevant for that deck’s core plan.

 

I guess this is a safety valve for Aetherflux Reservoir?  I’m not sure why Standard really needs this effect.  It is a decent hate card in Modern, but the three toughness makes it an easy target for Lightning Bolt.

 

Key to the City looks like a good addition to aggressive decks.  There are some obvious synergies with Madness cards, but there is than that.  Essentially, Key to the City gives you the option to redraw a bad card in your hand for two mana, while giving you a bonus upfront.  The timing on this is pretty strange, so I can see scenarios where you don’t actually draw cards off the untap trigger.  I expect this to see some play, but it is strange enough that I’m not positive.

 

This is four power worth of haste damage for three mana.  The only downside is that it dies after two turns naturally, provided you don’t find another supply of energy.  It’s not like the game is going to last too much longer if you get to attack with this thing twice.  This card is great, and I don’t feel like I need to say much more.

 

Tons of people will put this card in their sideboards, and almost all of them will be horribly wrong.  If you are playing a slow black deck in Standard and can’t beat Emrakul, the Promised End, a copy or two could be acceptable.  If you are playing it to strip an opponent’s Gideons or Chandras from their deck, that is bad.  Do not do that.  Lost Legacy is even worse than cards like Infinite Obliteration, since you are guaranteed to not get card parity by hitting a card in your opponent’s hand.

Modern might be slightly different, since there are some decks (Scapeshift, Storm) that you can neuter by stripping a single card.

 

This is a combo with Platinum Emperion in Modern.  That might be good enough to see some play.  It won’t be showing up in Standard.

 

You can cast this thing on turn five with just a Hedron Archive.  Having zero forms of evasion is kind of rough, but Metalwork Colossus might still be good enough.  It comes out surprisingly early, and you can rebuy it after your opponent is forced to deal with it.  Super Affinity seems pretty good.

You can also Eldritch Evolution this into an Emrakul, the Promised End if you really want to go off the deep end.

 

Nissa, Vital Force is a great value card for midrange green decks.  The plus ability is great for offense or defense.  Five damage should stop almost anything around that point in the game, and it is enough damage to kill most undefended planeswalkers.  The minus ability is good, but very similar to Grapple with the Past.  It will be used, but it won’t be the big draw to the card.  The ultimate is pretty much Tireless Tracker, drawing a ton of cards if the game goes long.  Nissa is great against midrange and control decks, and mediocre to bad against aggressive decks.  If nothing else, it will see a small amount of play in the Delerium decks.  It might also see play in the Emrakul decks if they need a planeswalker.

 

Noxious Gearhulk (Mechrataal) seems incredible to me.  Nekrataal might be worth a consideration if it was reprinted, and this gives a ton of extra bonuses for two more mana.  There are no restrictions on what this can target, it acts as a source of lifegain, and it can serve as a respectable win condition.  I expect Mechrataal to see play as a control finisher, Traverse the Ulvenwald target, and a possible reanimation target.

 

See Aetherflux Reservoir, possibly a Storm combo card.

 

Pia Nalaar has a pretty wide variety of options for three mana.  Firstly, she Crews Vehicles very well.  I expect Smuggler’s Copter and Fleetwheel Cruiser to be the most popular Vehicles, and Pia Nalaar can pilot either without wasting a point of power.  Beyond that, she is a serviceable creature that comes with a bunch of incidental bonuses.  I wouldn’t play her for either of the activated abilities, but they make a nice bonus when you are looking for options.

 

I hesitate to dismiss a three mana planeswalker, but I really don’t know where Saheeli Rai will find a home.  The obvious fit seems to be the Thermo-Alchemist deck, but Fevered Visions seems like a better option at the same mana cost.  I really think Saheeli Rai needs a deck that can utilize the -2 ability, so the deck needs to have red mana, blue mana, and expensive creatures or artifacts.  Those are tough requirements to meet.

In Modern, you can win the game on turn three with Saheeli Rai, Liquimetal Coating, and Altar of the Brood.  It seems sweet at the FNM level, but not really competitive.

 

There are going to be plenty of homes for Scrapheep Scrounger in Standard.  The most obvious one is anything already running Prized Amalgam from last season.  The four color Emerge deck and the Zombie deck will both look to incorporate Scrapheep Scrounger.  It is also just a great aggressive card in a deck that can even occasionally activate the recursion ability.  Toolcraft Exemplar and Inventor’s Apprentice are both interested in cheap, aggressive artifacts.  Fleetwheel Cruiser and Smuggler’s Copter are just interested in creatures for Crew cost, and Scrapheep Scrounger can come back to do it again.  I expect to see a lot of this artifact in Standard.

 

The flagship is in a weird place.  The high Crew cost means you have to build your deck with it in mind.  It being a Legendary Artifact means you can’t really afford to play a full set in your deck.  If you have a midrange creature deck, Skysovereign, Consul Flagship can act as a colorless Inferno Titan.  The power is certainly there, but it puts some weird constraints on your deck.

 

I’ll join a long list of people touting just how incredible Smuggler’s Copter is going to be.  This is a colorless Watchwolf for every aggressive deck that turns another creature into Merfolk Looter.  It has synergy with Madness Cards, it naturally handles the problem of drawing too many Vehichles (you can discard them), and it can smooth out your draws as early as turn three.  Expect to see Smuggler’s Copter smashing face constantly for the foreseeable future.

 

This card is great.  See Blooming Marsh

 

Essentially this is a four mana 4/4 with trample.  We can do better in Standard.  If it triggered off of each Energy entering play, Territorial Gorger might have a real shot.

 

Smuggler’s Copter and Scrapheep Scrounger are probably enough on their own to activate Toolcraft Exemplar, but there are some more options.  Thraben Inspector, Stitcher’s Graft, Inventor’s Goggles, and other Vehicles are other options to activate this white Wild Nacatl.  The creature type is relevant too, as both Dwarves and Artificers are supported creature types in Kaladesh.

 

Torrential Gearhulk might be good enough to see play based purely on its size and Flash.  The ability is a great bonus, but the number of great instants in Standard is fairly low right now.  Realistically, Anticipate or Murder are probably good enough to make Torrential Gearhulk playable.  It will just depend on whatever control shell shows up in Standard.

 

This is probably the easiest Gearhulk in the cycle to evaluate.  This card has a ton of power.  If you don’t have any creatures, Verdurous Gearhulk has your back.  If the battlefield is cluttered and you need to punch through, Verdurous Gearhulk has your back.  If you need your team to survive Kozilek’s Return, Verdurous Gearhulk has your back.  I expect any deck that runs Sylvan Advocate should also be running Verdurous Gearhulk.

 

That will wrap up my review for Kaladesh.  Once again, I did skip some of the cards in the set.  I either thought they were unlikely to see play in Standard, or their applications were obvious enough that I didn’t need to dig into them.  If you think I missed something, let me know at @DoneTheMath on Twitter.  See you next month!
