Preparing for GP Charlotte

By Byron King

Modern GP Charlotte is less than a week away, and I’m still trying to pick a deck. I have it narrowed down to a few different choices, but sharing my decision making process seemed like something people might enjoy reading. So far, this is where I’m at.

There are only three decks in Modern that I feel like I have much experience playing. Piloting a deck with minimal experience is a recipe for disaster at the GP, and I’ve only got minimal time to test for this event. That means I need something that I have experience with to rely on. If I pick up the new hotness (such as Grixis Delver), there will be tons of decks in the format (Hexproof, Ad Nauseum, Tokens, Elves, etc…) that I can’t realistically spend time practicing against. I want a deck that I feel comfortable with.

The decks I have experience with are G/R Tron, Burn, and various midrange Golgari decks (Jund, Abzan, G/B rock). I piloted Tron in GP Columbus (2012) and multiple PTQs, but with no finishes higher than a PTQ top 16. I have a lot of experience playing Burn in my earlier years of Magic, but most of my recent experience comes from playing against it. I did win the TCG Florida Modern State Championship in 2014 with Mardu Burn

I haven’t had the opportunity to play in any large Modern events since Khans of Tarkir was released. I did some testing with friends for PT Fate Reforged, and where I tried Seige Rhino and the Abzan midrange deck. There were some things I didn’t care for in the deck, but I’d be willing to try playing it. That is, if I decide it’s better than Jund.

The Decision

Should I play:

– G/R Tron
– Midrange
– Jund
– Abzan

– Boros

G/R Tron

G/R Tron is going to suffer from its recent success at the SCG Invitational. Blood Moon will be strong this weekend, and another popular combo deck (Amulet Bloom) has a large amount of overlapping hate cards. Tron is also fairly weak to Twin, which won PT Fate Reforged and has been a popular combo deck since the creation of the Modern format. On a more biased note,  I may have some horrible memories of playing G/R Tron in my last Modern GP. At GP Columbus, I played against multiple Twin decks and a dedicated land destruction deck to miss day two.

 

Midrange

Choosing between Jund and Abzan will come down to a small subset of cards in each deck. The core of the deck is very similar, with most of the variation in the sideboard. Both red and white offer a collection of powerful sideboard cards.

Option for Jund

Lightning Bolt

This card does a lot to shore up the aggressive matchups. Abzan only has Path to Exile as a turn one removal spell, which can provide dangerous mana ramp if used early. Lightning Bolt is extremely versatile, powerful spell. It also happens to makes Jund slightly more aggressive than Abzan, as it can be pointed at the opponent to finish games.

Terminate

Another upgrade in the removal suite over Abzan, Terminate handles Tasigur, the Golden Fang and Gurmag Angler, two of the premier threats in Grixis Delver. Abzan leans on Abrupt Decay as a two mana removal spell, with Path to Exile (mediocre), Go for the Throat (horrific against Affinity), Liliana of the Veil (unreliable), and Maelstrom Pulse (clunky) to deal with the delve creatures from Fate Reforged.

Kolaghan’s Command

The last large addition to the main deck of Jund is Kolaghan’s Command. The versatility of this card is the main draw, as it will always get a two-for-one. All four modes on Kolaghan’s Command are relevant, and it provides card advantage that Abzan cannot play.

Chandra Pyromaster, Olivia Voldaren, Huntmaster of the Fells, Outpost Siege

These are all cards attempting to fill a void from Bloodbraid Elf. Siege Rhino fills this slot in Abzan, but these are the best options for Jund. Each of them have merit, but none of them are strong reasons to play the deck. I would happily play none of them

Ancient Grudge

This is a huge addition to the sideboard for the Affinity matchup. However, Abzan has the advantage here, since Stony Silence is the better Affinity hate card.

Sowing Salt

This is a strong sideboard card against Tron, as long as it can be cast early. G/R Tron can simply play a Karn or Wurmcoil Engine before Jund ever gets to four mana. If Tron doesn’t have a fast hand, this can cripple the deck. It also has merit against Amulet Bloom, but it isn’t really any better than Fulminator Mage.

Anger of the Gods/Jund Charm

Abzan has access to Infest or Damnation, but these are both upgrades depending on what the sideboard is weak against.

Options for Abzan

Path to Exile

Overall, Path to Exile is worse than Lightning Bolt for a one mana removal spells. However, it does answer Deceiver Exarch, Tarmogoyf, and the various delve creatures in Modern.

Siege Rhino

This is a solid top end for the Abzan deck. However, it only really shines in the fair matchups in Modern. Many of the combo decks will simply ignore it, or just win the turn it gets cast. I would probably play it if I play Abzan, but I’m certainly not married to the idea.

Lingering Souls

Probably the biggest draw to Abzan, this is probably the biggest trump in the midrange mirrors. This is a genuine four-for-one, and extremely difficult for other midrange decks to overcome. Some combo decks like Infect also struggle against it. That being said, it is very ineffective against almost any other combo deck.

Stony Silence

This is the crown jewel of white sideboard cards. Most Affinity decks will simply lose to this card if it resolves on turn two. This is a strong motivator to play Abzan if Affinity will be present in any large numbers.

Zealous Persecution

There are a lot of corner cases where this card is valuable. It shines against opposing Lingering Souls decks, the Token deck, and anything else that uses small creatures (like Affinity or Infect). It also has applications against Twin, either killing Pestermite or fogging a swarm of Deceiver Exarchs.

Celestial Purge

Not a particularly exciting option, but it is good against anything with large amounts of red or black permanents.

After examining the list of cards I came up with, Jund feels like the clear winner. The removal spells seem stronger overall against the current metagame, and I’m not a huge fan of the best cards that Abzan brings to the table (Seige Rhino and Lingering Souls).

I have a good idea of most of the cards I want in the deck, with minor tuning to the specific creatures and removal. However, I just realized that Gerry Thomson pretty much did all the work for me. I like 74 out of 75 cards from his most recent list (below), with a single Grim Lavamancer being changed for a third Scavenging Ooze. I even like the sideboard, particularly the Night of Souls’ Betrayal. I’d want to spend a little time making sure that I understand what to board in and out with the sideboard, but I would be happy battling with this list.

Burn

Burn is an option that has a lot of appeal to me right now. A rise in the popularity of Jund is a good thing for Burn, though the adaptation of Feed the Clans mitigates that somewhat. Burn also did not have a high profile finish at the SCG Invitational. That is a good thing, as it will encourage players to skimp on sideboard cards for the matchup.

There are a few reasonable colors to splash in Burn, and that will be where most of the deckbuilding decisions come from. I don’t have any plans to mess with this core shell

Filling in the holes will depend on the color(s) added to the deck. Blue doesn’t really add anything of interest at the moment, so I’m left with white, green, and black.

White provides

Boros Charm

Lightning Helix

Kor Firewalker

Green provides

Atarka’s Command

Destructive Revelry

Black provides

Bump in the Night

Self-Inflicted Wound

I’m inclined to believe that white is mandatory at this point. Boros Charm is absolutely fantastic, and both Lightning Helix and Kor Firewalker are a huge boost in the mirror.

After multiple discussions and watching online content, I’m hesitant to run Atarka’s Command. It is fairly detrimental to the manabase for very little gain. Likely, it won’t be more than Skullcracks five through eight. I like Destructive Revelry enough that I will likely splash for just that out of the sideboard.

Bump in the Night is a card that I really like having access to. Burn really wants as many copies of Lava Spike as it can get, and this one has multiple upsides (Flashback, dodges Leyline of Sanctity, doesn’t trigger Kor Firewalker/Dragon’s Claw). Self-Inflicted Wound would be the main reason to run Black though, mostly to neutralize Tarmogoyf.  It would be a nice option to have, but most of the Tarmogoyf decks are slower than Burn.

I have a history of being greedy with the mana in Burn already. The list I posted above had both white and black cards in the maindeck, along with green in the sideboard. However, the mirror was not particularly popular at the time. Burn’s increased popularity means that the third color would be a dangerous addition.

Running the full four copies of Lightning Helix seems a little excessive, but I don’t really don’t like the other options. I dislike Grim Lavamancer in this shell, along with all of the other available creatures. Running more than one Shard Volley feels sketchy, as multiples can ruin an otherwise decent draw. The fourth copy of Searing Blaze seems reasonable, but drawing multiples against creatureless decks can lose games.

If I decide to play with green in the deck, I would remove three Lightning Helix and one land for four copies of Atarka’s Command.

Final Thoughts

Right now, I’m leaning fairly heavily towards Burn, but I’ve still got Jund ready to go. The Naya Burn list seems a little better, as there are several decks in the format that I would like to have additional Skullcracks. Lightning Helix also feels like a filler card, because I can’t find something better.

Jund seems like a decent choice, and I’m still considering it. If you have any thoughts, feel free to share them with me.  If you disagree with any of my assessments, I’d love to hear why.  If you like what I’m doing, wish me luck at Charlotte!
