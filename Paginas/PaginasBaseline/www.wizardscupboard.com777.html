An Overview and My Experiences with 1v1 Commander

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath

 

Magic the Gathering Online (MTGO) might be the worst program I’ve ever used. My collection has grown there simply because I tend to forget that fact about once a year. I’ll log on, sink another $50 into upgrading my only deck (Modern Burn) and start playing games. That lasts for about three hours before I can’t stand it anymore. I’ll log off for another year and then repeat. I’ve been playing that way for years.

I certainly don’t consider this a good strategy for improving at Magic. MTGO has got to be the most efficient way to test, but I just can’t bring myself to do it. If nothing else, I don’t think I could justify the expense to maintain a MTGO and paper collection simultaneously.

That might be changing now. 1v1 Commander leagues have been going on for a few weeks now, and I seem to have caught the bug. My preferred formats are Cube, Legacy, and Modern, and 1v1 Commander pretty much scratches all those itches at once. I get to see all the cards I enjoy in Magic, including the cards too old for Modern, too inefficient for Legacy, and restricted to one deck in eight in Cube. Honestly, it feels like playing Cube where I can pick my deck ahead of time.

The single game nature of the format also appeals to me. Keeping some of these attrition battles limited to a single game keeps rounds under half an hour. Aggressive decks can reliably be finished with a round in under 10 minutes. I consider that a feature if I don’t feel like playing for long stretches of time.

There hasn’t been a ton written about 1v1 Commander on MTGO yet. If you are new to the format, I’m going to briefly cover the most popular decks in the format. I also recorded my initial foray into the format. My MTGO budget is fairly limited, so I stuck to Zurgo Bellstriker and Drana, Liberator of Malakir. I’ve picked up some tidbits on those decks if you’re looking to try them yourself.

The Popular Decks

Baral, Chief of Compliance

This deck is widely considered the best deck in the format, though I don’t think it deserves the title. This deck consists of Islands, counterspells, draw spells, and the “combo” of Polymorph for Emarukl, the Aeon’s Torn. There are four copies of Polymorph (Polymorph, Proteus Staff, Reweave, and Synthetic Destiny) for consistency. Baral and a suite of creature lands (Mutavault, Blinkmoth Nexus, Mishra’s Factory, etc…) provide ample fodder to get Emrakul onto the battlefield.

This deck really benefits from Baral, Chief of Compliance. Having access to him every game is a huge benefit over sporadically finding him in a regular 60 card deck. Almost every spell in the deck benefits from the cost reduction, and a solid chunk of them get a free loot effect stapled on.

Jace, Vryn’s Prodigy

Realistically, I could just copy 90% of the information about Baral, Chief of Compliance, and I could easily get away with it. There is an incredible amount of overlap between the two decks, but that shouldn’t come as a huge surprise. Brainstorm is still the best cantrip, Counterspell is still the best counterspell, and Force of Will is still the best free counterspell.

Jace decks usually exclude the Polymorph for Emrakul, the Aeon’s Torn plan in favor of blue’s various Time Walk cards. Jace gives a rebuy on these, allowing the deck to blast ahead of the opponent. More importantly, this deck gets to rebuy High Tide, supercharging the deck enough to essentially win on the spot.

Vial Smasher the Fierce / Kraum, Ludevick’s Opus

If you use MTGO 5-0 results to gauge a deck’s success, this duo seems to be the king of the hill. The stock lists looks similar to Baral, replacing all the second string counterspells and draw spells for other cheap interaction and powerful planeswalkers. The Polymorph package no longer makes the cut, allowing for the inclusion of utility creatures like Snapcaster Mage.

Beyond the upgrades to creatures and planeswalkers, black offers a suite of tutor effects. This is probably the single biggest advantage to this commander pair over Baral. This deck can run devastating singletons and find them with surprising ease. Suddenly, your deck has multiple copies of Damnation against creature swarms without handicapping you against creatureless decks.

Vial Smasher the Fierce is the more potent of this commander duo. With cards like Force of Will, Commandeer, and anything with Delve, this Goblin carves some big chunks out of the opponent’s life total. The cheap mana cost allows it to come down early or to slip in around turn five without dropping counterspell mana. Kraum, Ludevick’s Opus is a fine card, but its main purpose is to provide the deck with access to blue mana.

Tasigur, the Golden Fang

Tasigur, the Golden Fang is regularly cast for only a single mana, highlighting the strength of Delve in Commander. Delve can be used to pay the Commander tax, making it difficult to kill Tasigur enough to keep it gone. Tasigur provides an undercosted body with free card advantage, making it an ideal commander for the color combination.

Tasigur decks are essentially Dimir decks, utilizing blue’s redundancy in card draw, deck manipulation, and countermagic combined with black’s discard, removal, and tutor effects. Green essentially adds Deathrite Shaman, Leovold, Emissary of Trest, Sylvan Library, and a few replacement level cards.

Overall, Tasigur decks share a very similar card set as the Vial Smasher / Kraum pair. Red offers a selection of slightly better spells in Pyroblast, Kolaghan’s Command, and some premier planeswalkers. Green gets the cards I mentioned above along with a cheaper Commander. MTGO results have shown slightly better results for Vial Smasher / Kraum, but the differences are small enough that you can let personal preferences bias you without costing much.

Breya, Etherium Shaper

Need more colors in your Dimir deck? Breya, Etherium Shaper has you covered. Breya implies an artifact strategy, but that isn’t really true. Breya is an impressive 6/6 in stats spread out between three creatures, with some decent activated abilities tossed in for good measure. At the low cost of four mana, Breya makes for a strong card regardless of synergy.

With four different colors available, Breya decks have access to so many “auto-include” cards that there is only room for a handful of flex cards. Breya decks look similar to the Vial Smasher / Kraum decks, utilizing all of the same cheap interaction. These cards are rounded out with white’s efficient spot removal and a few additional planeswalkers. Breya’s synergy with artifacts usually stretches far enough to include a full complement of signets and Daretti, Ingenious Iconoclast.

NIssa, Vastwood Seer

We finally reached the end of the blue decks. Nissa, Vastwood Seer is a traditional example of a ramp deck, with tons of acceleration and a stack of expensive threats capable of dominating a game.

A Nissa decks in 1v1 Commander is knowingly venturing into a format filled with counterspells. Traditionally, ramp decks are an underdog to control, but there are some options available to counter this. First, green has a bunch of interchangeable ramp spells, preventing any one from being vital to resolve. The quality is high enough that you don’t really have to scrape the bottom of the barrel either, keeping stinkers like Ruin in Their Wake on the sidelines where they belong.

The Eldrazi are the real reason that Nissa can compete in a world filled with blue. Emrakul and Ulamog present some devastating cast triggers that should be able to lock away a game. Cavern of Souls, Eye of Ugin, and Expedition Map add some redundancy to this plan, and green has plenty of creature tutors. A few other threats like Ugin, the Spirit Dragon make appearances, but a lot of the threat slots are filled with tutors.

Lastly, several of the flex spots are filled with value cards. Sylvan Library, Lifecrafter’s Bestiary, and Tireless Tracker are all great cards against control, so they find their way into most Nissa decks.

Selvla, Heart of the Wild

Selvala, Heart of the Wild decks are very close to Nissa, Vastwood Seer decks. The only difference is that Selvala has a slight combo aspect to the deck. Phyrexian Dreadnought, Phyrexian Soulgorger, and Sheltering Ancient function as insane ramp spells with Selvala in play, allowing for some truly obscene turns.

Other than that, expect a lot of similarities to Nissa decks. Selvala will rely slightly more on creature ramp due to the nature of the commander, but the overall gameplay is similar.

Sidisi, Undead Vizier

1v1 Commander’s main combo deck, Sidisi, Undead Vizier is an expensive Demonic Tutor that sits in the command zone. The deck is filled with tutor effects, since the deck needs to find Buried Alive and a reanimate spell. Buried Alive puts Necrotic Ooze, Phyrexian Devourer, and Walking Ballista (Triskelion) in the graveyard, and a reanimate spell gets Necrotic Ooze into play. Phyrexian Devourer’s ability lets Necrotic Ooze load up with +1/+1 counters, and Walking Ballista’s ability converts those counters into lethal damage.

Almost every black deck plays Demonic Tutor and Vampiric Tutor, but Sidisi goes far deeper down the rabbit hole. Imperial Seal, Beseech the Queen, Grim Tutor, Dimir Machinations (transmute), and Dark Petition all make the cut. Buried Alive is irreplaceable and there are only about eight reanimate effects available, so any tutors help to buffer those counts.

My Adventures in Budget 1v1 Commander

After reading about the format and watching LSV’s Baral video, I decided I wanted in on the action. I don’t have a MTGO collection beyond a Modern Burn deck, so Zurgo seemed like the place to start. I scrounged up some sample lists. I dumped about $20 into the deck, only skipping out on Koth of the Hammer, Goblin Rabblemaster, and Umezawa’s Jitte for budget reasons.

My results with the deck were medium. There was a sense of helplessness if you ever got paired against a deck with a combo finish. Sidisi combo decks were unbeatable. Baral’s Polymorph plan was a pain, but a few Bolts could at least buy some time. Honestly, the “combo” of Delve out a fast Tasigur for a 4/5 body was miserable.

That said, I certainly got my fair share of wins too. The deck curved out quite well, and I could crush just about anyone who stumbled. I also just enjoyed playing the deck.

Something I haven’t really covered is the increased starting life total. Aggressive decks have the extra burden of opponents with thirty life. That increases the reliance on repeatable sources of damage (creatures) over burn spells.

What I learned from playing Zurgo

  • One drop creatures are not be a priority. Zurgo is almost always my turn one play, so any competing cards would need to be worth playing on a later turn. Cards like Goblin Guide are worth a slot, but Legion Loyalist can stay on the bench.
  • Two drop creatures are incredibly important. Not being able to follow up Zurgo with a creature on turn two virtually guarantees the opponent will have time to take over.
  • Recurring damage is vital. Cards like Lava Spike and Skullcrack deal damage in chunks small enough to question their inclusion. Traditional burn decks in 60 card formats should be able to assemble 20 damage after the opening hand and about three draw steps. Giving the opponent an additional 10 life means four or five more draw steps to even assemble lethal damage. In a format filled with countermagic and combo finishes, this is not the path to glory.
  • There are some great options for hating out combo in red, such as Eidolon of the Great Revel and Harsh Mentor. Unfortunately, there are no ways to ensure you find those cards in the correct matchup. You have to pray really hard to hit your one-outer in a 100 card deck.

After my mediocre results with Zurgo, I decided to move onto Drana, Liberator of Malakir. Somehow I convinced myself that red’s lack of options instead of the poor positioning of aggressive decks was my problem. Actually, I’ll be honest. I was blinded by the opportunity to Dark Ritual into Hypnotic Specter, my favorite Cube opening (don’t judge).

Drana’s strength over Zurgo is twofold. The first is the suite of discard spells available, giving your deck a vital way to interact. Second are the tutor effects, black’s biggest contribution to Commander. Silver bullets could be included to handle extreme problems

Drana decks have had some success already, and this deck still essentially builds itself. To maximize Drana, the deck needs a ton of one and two cost creatures. Black has tons of options for mediocre aggressive cards, so those fill out the bulk of the deck. Tutors, discard, and removal fill out most of the deck, with a few silver bullet cards to tutor for in the correct matchup.

What I learned from playing Drana

  • From a purely aggressive stance, Drana is miles worse than Zurgo. Zurgo nullifies the need for one drop creatures, while Drana does the same thing for three drop creatures. That leaves the Drana deck full of much worse top deck draws when the two are directly compared
  • Very few of black’s threats feel better than replacement level. When I needed to Demonic Tutor for a threat instead of an answer, anything I picked felt lackluster. I’m not sure how to fix this problem, but I imagine there is something I could throw tickets at to make it less of a problem. Maybe Batterskull would be a good start.
  • Hellfire is awesome. I didn’t know this card existed before I put the deck together, but it has overperformed. My deck should have a Damnation or Toxic Deluge to tutor for, but I didn’t pick one up for budget reasons. Hellfire comes with a higher mana cost, but sometimes wins the game on the spot.
  • Hatred is great. I definitely knew about this one before I started building, as it is my favorite Mental Magic card. The abundance of discard spells makes it trivial to know when it’s safe to go all in.

At this point, I’m ready to explore building a Sidisi combo deck. I like playing aggressive decks, but I’ve already gone into the problems that aggressive decks face. I would enjoy playing one of the multicolor decks, but my MTGO account isn’t flush enough to delve that deep.

tl;dr
Think constructed Cube. Blue or Blue/x is control/midrange, green is ramp, and black is combo. Tutors are great. Vial Smasher OP.
Zurgo and Drana are mediocre, but affordable. Don’t expect to 5-0 many leagues, but you can have a decent time for ~$20.
