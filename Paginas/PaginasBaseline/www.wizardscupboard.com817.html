The Modern Grind

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath

 

Modern is filled with decks that let you do whatever you like.  There are decks that bring the game to a grinding halt (anything with Sword of the Meek).  There are decks that aim to win on the second or third turn of the game (anything with Simian Spirit Guide or Become Immense).  There are decks that actually try to cast Emrakul, the Aeons Torn.  You can build a deck that actually draws all 60 cards by turn five.  if you don’t like that, you can flip your opponent’s library into their graveyard at about the same speed.  There are multiple decks that kill you with lands.

All that, and I couldn’t find a single deck I actually liked.

I’ve been a Jund player in Modern since the format’s inception.  I watched Deathrite Shaman’s printing supercharge the deck, and I survived the crash landing after Bloodbraid Elf and Deathrite Shaman got banned.  Countless hours of testing Modern always leads me back to Blackcleave Cliffs and Overgrown Tombs.  All that, and I’m convinced the deck just isn’t good.

According to MTGTop8, Modern is approximately 20% reactive decks and 80% proactive decks.  In a format with 30 viable decks, it is completely impossible to have a successful plan for all of them.  You just can’t make 15 sideboard slots cover that many different problems.

Beyond that, Jund isn’t the top dog for reactive decks anymore either.  Nahiri, the Harbringer poses some questions that Jund is ill-equipped to answer.  A four mana planeswalker is immune to everything Jund used to play beyond a pair of Thoughtseize and possibly a Maelstrom Pulse.  Dreadbore and Maelstrom Pulse are good against Nahiri, but they are less optimal against several other decks.

It gets worse.  Path to Exile, Lightning Bolt, and Snapcaster Mage are sufficient answers for Jund’s threats.  That wasn’t too bad originally, since Jund ran a bunch of hand disruption to strip answers.  Ancestral Vision is an effective answer to that plan now.  Jund wants an attrition battle that ends with both players Hellbent.  That just isn’t how the games play out anymore.  Liliana of the Veil is still great, but that’s really the only positive thing left for the Jund side.

I played in my first Modern PPTQ of the season two weeks ago.  I played Jund since I haven’t tested the format in a few months.  It did not go well.  Tron is an awful matchup, and I consciously chose to ignore it in my sideboard plan.  Making that matchup favorable would cost me a third of my 15 card sideboard, and I didn’t think it was worth it.  My round one opponent at the PPTQ lead with Urza’s Tower.

I ended the day at 3-3, beating Grixis Control, Affinity.  I lost to Blue Tron, Jund, and Naya Zoo.  My Lantern opponent in round six decided to head home early instead of playing.  I’ve certainly had better tournaments.

 

I knew that I wanted something different for the next weekend.  Traditional Jund wasn’t going to get the job done.  My first thought was the brew from my last article.

I started with this list because it just seemed sweet.  I’m still planning on working on it, but there are a few problems.  I’ve given up Blackcleave Cliffs for Lightning Bolt in order to have a better late game than traditional Jund.  Abrupt Decay is a good card, and Nameless Inversion isn’t completely embarrassing, but they both cost two mana.  Not being able to interact with the board is a great way to fall behind to Burn, Zoo, Affinity, and Infect.  The removal also can’t answer a Kalitas, Traitor of Ghet, Tasigur, the Golden Fang, or Gurmag Angler.

The list needs one of two different things.  It either needs to pull the focus away from hyper Delerium or find some cheaper payoff cards.  I expect the best plan is to pull out the Haakon package for Lightning Bolts and reduce the Transverse the Ulvenwald toolbox.  Either Emrakul, the Promised End or Whispers of Emrakul will have to pull their weight, or the deck shouldn’t touch Delerium cards.

 

My next thought came from an article Sam Black wrote a couple months ago.  He was pretty adamant that Suicide Zoo was the best deck in Modern.  That’s a bold claim, but he and Justin Cohen were saying the same thing about Amulet Bloom a while ago.  They were probably right at the time, which adds some credibility to Black’s statement.

The article came down to how mana efficient the deck is on an average draw.  It’s more than just how cheap the cards are, even though most of them only cost a single mana.  Several of the cards, Death’s Shadow, Wild Nacatl, and Become Immense, all generate threats that are way above the curve for a single mana.  That puts the Suicide Zoo player ahead if both players are casting the same number of spells in the first few turns.  Then there are the matches where the opposition isn’t doing much on the first turn or two.  Suicide Zoo will just run those over with mana efficiency.

This list has several things I really like.  I covered the mana efficiency above, but there is more than that.  The threats in this deck are so efficient that Lightning Bolt and Path to Exile are really the only effective answers.  Snapcaster Mage should help, but Suicide Zoo can just win before Snapcaster even comes online.

Sideboarding is another place where Suicide Zoo picks up an edge.  People have cards like Leyline of Sanctity, Kitchen Finks, and Feed the Clan as hate cards for Burn.  Those cards are just awful against Suicide Zoo.  Most hate for Burn is too slow, to minimal, or just completely ineffective against any form of Zoo.  Sideboard cards for Zoo include 2-3 mana removal, cards like Supreme Verdict, or other cards that Suicide Zoo just laughs at for their mana inefficiency.  That’s a huge edge for this kind of Zoo deck.

I didn’t want to explore this deck for a couple reasons.  The manabase is unforgiving, and I didn’t have a lot of time to test for the next PPTQ.  I needed to be sure it was the deck I wanted to play before investing time there.  Beyond that, I know the Burn matchup is complicated for Suicide Zoo.  I didn’t have time to learn the intricacies of that matchup, and I won’t play a deck if I don’t know how to play against Burn.  It’s just a common sight in Florida.

Time constraints won’t always be a problem, so I expect to return to Suicide Zoo sometime in the near future.

 

My next plan was Dredge.  That all came down to one card

This card seemed great.  For two mana, I got to load my graveyard with 1-2 cards and cast Duress, Syphon Soul, and possibly Disfigure.  That’s an insane deal, and I was pretty sure I had broken it.

I had dreams of crushing Burn with Collective Brutality, but that just wasn’t how the games played out.  I spent my first 2-3 turns setting up while Burn spent the first few turns murdering my face.  There are some options to improve the matchup, but I didn’t have time to find the best configuration.

The mana was definitely wrong for this deck.  I’m pretty sure there should be some Blackcleave Cliffs somewhere, and I’m pretty sure that 3 basic Mountains is overkill.  Previous Dredge decks really only cast red spells and Life from the Loam.  This list needs to be able to cast Collective Brutality early and still be able to dredge Life from the Loam and cast it.

The third Life from the Loam was far more important than I thought it would be.  I needed to get back lands for Bloodghast in the midgame, and there were times that I just couldn’t make it happen.

Lastly, there are some cards that still need to be tested.  Burning Inquiry, Vengeful Pharaoh, and Rally the Peasants are all considerations.

I’ve never been a huge fan of the controlling Scapeshift decks.  I’m actively looking for a deck that is proactive, so this has a lot more appeal.  It clearly has a plan against aggressive decks, and Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle is a trump against decks looking to go long.

Testing with Nahiri Scapeshift never really got off the ground.  I haven’t played a Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle deck since it was Standard legal, and I’m missing a few of the cards.  It didn’t seem worth testing a deck that I couldn’t guarantee that I had access too.  I shelfed it for another time

 

That brought me to Bant Eldrazi.  I only got to play in one Modern event when the Eldrazi menace was in full force, and it was great.  SCG Regionals was the same weekend as PT Eldrazi, and I had cobbled together an approximation of the deck that was running rampant through the Pro Tour.

The Bant version is definitely worse than the original colorless version, but I still liked a lot of what the deck was doing.  Early Thought-Knot Seers are great, and Reality Smasher can bail you out of some pretty tough situations.

It wasn’t quite what I was looking for, but it wasn’t too far off.  It wasn’t a popular deck, so plenty of people didn’t have a plan for it.  It also had a proactive plan, my default requirement for a Modern deck.

Long story short, I came in second place.  I beat Burn in the Top 8, and then lost to it in the finals.  I also beat 1 Jeskai Control deck, 1 R/G Tron deck, 1 Sultai? Tron deck, 1 Temur Scapeshift, and 1 R/G Primeval Titan deck.  I also lost to a Jeskai Control deck in the swiss.

Most of the results were about what you would expect.  My results against Remand decks were directly related to the number of Cavern of Souls that I found.  My results against Tron were fairly lucky sprinkled with a copious number of Thought-Knot Seers.

Burn was one of the few matchups I had reasonable experience with both before and after the banning of Eye of Ugin.  The colorless version had Chalice of the Void and a lack of Searing Blaze targets.  The new Bant version has draws reliant of Noble Hierarch surviving and has to forgo the Chalice of the Void plan.  Thougt-Knot Seer and Reality Smasher are still great, but the matchup is very close.

Bant Eldrazi is certainly on my list for further testing.  It certainly does some powerful things, but I’m itching to explore most of the decks I discussed today.  I certainly think there is something to the Dredge deck with Collective Brutality.  Suicide Zoo just seems like a great deck, provided I can put the reps in.  I haven’t cast Primeval Titan in a long time, and I’m looking forward to doing it again.  Whenever I have a long time, I can’t wait to tinker with Traverse the Ulvenwald.
