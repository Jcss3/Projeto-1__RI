Modern Musings: Burn Primer Part 2


by Justin Alderman

Welcome back to my Modern Musings at The Wizard’s Cupboard! Last time, I introduced you to the first part of my Modern Burn Primer and promised to follow up with how to beat the Big Four. Today I keep that promise, as I am a man of my word, and I’ll do my best to provide you with the necessary tools to beat the bogeymen of the Modern format. However, due to the length of this section, I must break it up into two parts. Today, I’ll be focusing on Affinity and Splinter Twin.

THE BIG FOUR TO THE STAGE, PLEASE!

The Big Four are what I’ve dubbed the current giants of the Modern metagame and encompass the full spectrum of the Modern pillars: Aggro, Midrange, Control, and Combo (granted, there is some overlap in these definitions). These decks are known as Affinity, G/B/x (Rock, Abzan, and Jund), Splinter Twin, and Burn. And while I’ve stated previously that Modern is a wide open format with numerous viable strategies, the Big Four undoubtedly make up the largest portion of the meta, hence the name. Learning how they operate is essential to beating them. Sun Tzu once said in the Art of War that “if you know your enemies and know yourself, you will not be imperiled in a hundred battles; if you do not know your enemies but do know yourself, you will win one and lose one; if you do not know your enemies nor yourself, you will be imperiled in every single battle.” After these articles, you will know your enemy. The rest will be up to you.

BATTLE PLAN, GO!

While I won’t be going giving you a card-by-card breakdown on how to sideboard against the Big Four, I will be giving you a brief description of each deck’s Game 1 and postboard attack plan strategies. The key will be using this knowledge to not just have the cards to fight them, but the “know how” to implement a counterattack.

AFFINITY

Affinity is the premiere aggro strategy of the Modern format and has one of the best Game 1’s against the majority of the field. The goal of the deck is to reach a critical mass of cheap artifacts and then deal huge chunks of damage with the help of Cranial Plating, Arcbound Ravager, Master of Etherium and Ensoul Artifact. Nothing exemplifies the concept of deck synergy more than Affinity, and if left unchecked, the deck will beat you down very quickly. Here is a typical Affinity deck:

Creature 26
4 Ornithopter
3 Memnite
4 Vault Skirge
4 Signal Pest
3 Steel Overseer
4 Arcbound Ravager
2 Etched Champion
2 Master of Etherium

Spells 17
4 Mox Opal
4 Springleaf Drum
4 Cranial Plating
1 Welding Jar
1 Ensoul Artifact
1 Galvanic Blast
2 Thoughtcast

Land 17
1 Island
4 Glimmervoid
4 Blinkmoth Nexus
4 Inkmoth Nexus
4 Darksteel Citadel

GAME 1

Affinity will typically start off by dumping most of their hand onto the table and going wide, swarming the board with little creatures that get stronger with the help of Signal Pest, Steel Overseer, and Master of Etherium. However, if the offense is stymied, the followup plan is to create a large, evasive monster with the aid of Cranial Plating and Arcbound Ravager. You must be cautious during attack steps, as Arcbound Ravager will eventually sacrifice a bunch of useless artifacts to power up an unblocked or evasive critter, and Cranial Plating can also be equipped at instant-speed if they have access to just two Black mana. When exhausted, the Affinity player has the opportunity to refill their hand with Thoughtcast or by turning their Inkmoth Nexus into a lethal Infect threat with a Cranial Plating. Even letting a Vault Skirge live long enough to be the target of an Ensoul Artifact can decide the outcome of the game, as the life gain can just be too much for you to handle. As said before, the deck is essentially a vicious snowball that, if not stopped early, will overrun you or force you to race – a race that you’re hard pressed to compete with.

As a Burn player, you walk a delicate line of deciding when to use your burn spells to control Affinity’s board presence and preserving your life total, or throwing everything you have at their face. Each decision you make in the first few turns are crucial, as Affinity has numerous ways to catch up from being behind. If you can stick a Grim Lavamancer early, then you’re in great shape since you can use him to snipe off Vault Skirges and Plating targets. In fact, Lavamancer is so crucial to this fight that it is oftentimes correct to use your Boros Charm defensively to save your Lavamancer from Galvanic Blasts. You will also be leaning heavily on Searing Blaze, Lightning Bolt, and Lightning Helix to stop threats, but you must be able to correctly identify which threats are a “must kill” as to not waste your gas. Your haste creatures are great early on but quickly become outclassed or easily blocked, rendering them solely as blockers while allowing you to send burn damage to the dome.

POSTBOARD

Affinity’s postboard plan is to bring in cards like Spellskite, extra Etched Champions, counterspells such as Spell Pierce or Dispel, and additional removal such as Galvanic Blast and Whipflare in order to cripple your offense, forcing you to empty your hand trying to control the board. Fortunately, your game against Affinity improves tremendously postboard. Having access to extra Grim Lavamancers, Destructive Revelry, Smash to Smithereens, Pyrite Spellbomb, Searing Blaze, Searing Blood, Forked Bolt, Volcanic Fallout, Path to Exile, and even Stony Silence gives you the means to fight Affinity as the Control player and still stick to your game plan of attacking that life total. However, the key to proper sideboarding against Affinity all comes down to identifying the core weaknesses of your maindeck, along with your position, and quickly adjusting that.

For starters, Eidolon of the Great Revel is actually pretty terrible in this matchup. Because of Affinity’s blazing fast starts, you’re usually beginning on the backfoot and fighting hard to get ahead on board presence. In most cases, playing an Eidolon just means that you’ll be damaging yourself more than Affinity will, as well as getting stonewalled by a Spellskite. As a result, Eidolon should be the first four cards out of your deck when sideboarding and replaced with artifact hate.

Other weak cards in the matchup are your burn spells that can’t hit creatures, such as Lava Spike, Boros Charm, Skullcrack. Granted, you can’t afford to take out all of these spells, as you’ll need them to deal damage, but any number of these can be taken out if you have more than 4 cards dedicated to your Affinity hate. It’s also important to remember your position in each game, as it is sometimes correct to board out other creatures like Monastery Swiftspear instead of the burn spells so that you can keep your damage output consistently high.

One thing to keep in mind is how much damage your own manabase will deal to you in postboard games. Destructive Revelry is the best card to bring in for Games 2 and 3, since it can nail Ensoul Artifact, but trying to fix your colors to play Lightning Helix and Revelry could leave you in a very hard place. Avoid taking fetchland plus shockland damage in a single turn if you can help it.

TIPS AND TRICKS

-An Arcbound Ravager on the field changes everything and fighting over one can be tricky. As a rule, it’s best sometimes to wait until the Ravager has sacrificed as many artifacts as it can, including itself, and then killing the recipient of the modular counters (if possible). One of the biggest mistakes you can make is starting a fight over this creature, wasting multiple burn spells, and losing the fight anyway.

-Be smart with your Destructive Revelry, Lightning Helix (when targeting a creature), and Smash to Smithereens, as having the target lost before the spell resolves will cause you to lose out on the damage or life gain.

-Affinity will usually win or lose by just a couple points of damage. The Affinity player is always thinking and doing math in order to maximize damage with their creatures. You need to do the same. It will help you decide which target to kill.

-If Affinity has a slow start, you should absolutely be focused on killing their mana production. In Game 1, you can do this by killing creatures that can be used to activate Springleaf Drum or Mox Opal.

-Remember that Skullcrack can turn off damage prevention should one of your creatures be blocked by Etched Champion.

-Keep an eye on Welding Jar since it most likely be used to regenerate a creature you’re trying to kill, effectively countering your spell.

WRAP UP

Affinity is a ruthless, beast of a deck held together by powerful synergies and a painless manabase, which is something that Burn cannot capitalize on. As such, you’ll need to keep a very strong hand in order to compete. If you expect a lot of Affinity in your metagame, you can gain an edge by keeping your maindeck at an incredibly low curve by using Forked Bolts and additional Grim Lavamancers, thereby ensuring that you mulligan as little as possible. Just remember to play your role as the Control player, fix your mana as painlessly as possible, carefully evaluate each threat before acting, and you should do fine.

SPLINTER TWIN

Bridging the gap between Control and Combo in Modern is Splinter Twin. For the uninitiated, Twin is a deck that uses counterspells, cantrips, and premium removal to gain a tempo advantage on its opponent before going on the offensive with small value creatures and burn spells. It also has the ability to outright steal games with its signature card: Splinter Twin. As early as turn 3, Twin can simply flash in Deceiver Exarch or Pestermite during the opponent’s end step, untap on Turn 4, and then cast Splinter Twin to make an infinite army of hasty tokens. Splinter Twin’s versatility and multiple angles of attack make it one of the best decks in the format.

At its core, Splinter Twin is based in Blue and Red, but is also known to splash into other colors for additional removal options or utility creatures. The most famous splash variety is known as “TarmoTwin,” which dips into Green to play Tarmogoyf and utilizes a more aggressive approach when it can’t combo. However, the usual run-of-the-mill Twin deck is still Blue/Red and will look something like this:

Creature 13
4 Deceiver Exarch
2 Pestermite
4 Snapcaster Mage
2 Grim Lavamancer
1 Vendilion Clique

Spells 24
4 Splinter Twin
4 Serum Visions
4 Remand
4 Lightning Bolt
2 Cryptic Command
1 Dispel
1 Spell Snare
1 Peek
1 Electrolyze
1 Flame Slash
1 Vedalken Shackles

Land 23
5 Island
3 Steam Vents
1 Mountain
1 Stomping Ground

GAME 1

Splinter Twin will usually start off conservatively, either opting to play a fetchland into a tapped Steam Vents or by playing a Serum Visions, as it wants to see what the opponent is on in order to decide what route to take. The minute you drop a creature and begin the assault, the tempo battle begins. The Splinter Twin player will do everything it can to stave off your initial attack – killing your creatures, countering your spells, or blocking with Snapcasters. Anything. Ultimately, the deck will try to end the game with the combo as early as turn 4, but that’s okay since you’re in the business to do the same thing. Sometimes they have it and you just die, and sometimes they don’t. If they don’t have it, they’ll attempt to prolong the game long enough to either assemble the combo pieces, or exhaust you and kill you with Snapcaster Mages and Lightning Bolts.

What does this mean for you? Well, once you’ve correctly identified the deck as Splinter Twin (and not a Delver deck), you have a few lines to take. Lightning Bolt in conjunction with Lightning Helix, Searing Blaze, or Grim Lavamancer will be your outs to stopping the Splinter Twin combo. If your hand is super aggressive, you’ll probably want to try to end it quickly before the threat of the combo becomes real. Eidolon of the Great Revel is a great tool to have since it will punish them for every spell they cast, allowing you to maintain pressure while keeping a Lightning Bolt in hand to kill their Pestermite. However, the real problem is the Deceiver Exarch and its 4 toughness. Oftentimes, the Twin player will flash in the Exarch, tap down a creature, block another creature, and then combo off. If you don’t have the double Lightning Bolt (or Lightning Bolt + Helix/Blaze), you have no chance.

Should you have the removal spell(s), and if the Twin player suspects that, they will absolutely tap down any of your lands to preserve their Exarch or Pestermite from dying. If you need that mana, you have to tap your land for mana right then and there with the tap trigger on the stack. This especially applies in postboard games when you have access to Combust, Path to Exile, or Rending Volley, but in Game 1 it is still just as important.

If your hand isn’t as aggressive, then you’ll want to feel it out. Non-removal spells and creatures can be spent to chop away at their life total, but you’ll want to dance around the counterspells by using the Bait and Wait method from Part 1. Long games against Splinter Twin involve a lot of thinking, finagling, and outmaneuvering. Use your instants wisely and at the end of their turn, forcing them to use up their mana at inopportune times.

The best case scenario for Game 1 involves a very fast start and Twin not having the combo. The worst case involves a slow start and you dying on turn 4. However, the majority of the games will involve fighting a tempo war where you must squeeze out every point of damage you can muster. If you’re playing against TarmoTwin, then you have an advantage in that your opponent will be damaging themselves a lot to fix their manabase. However, they can also be very aggressive with their Tarmogoyfs, even going so far as to play a Splinter Twin on one to really put the pressure on.

POSTBOARD

Aside from a few hard counters and the addition of Spellskite and Batterskull, there aren’t too many changes that a Splinter Twin player will make postboard. Certain TarmoTwin decks have been known to side in Obstinate Baloth for lifegain, and Grixis Twin (splashing Black for hand disruption) will absolutely bring in some extra kill spells (usually Disfigure) for your ground forces. However, Twin’s manner of play can change drastically depending on their plan and position, ranging from aggressive plays to outright control.

Your best weapons against Splinter Twin will be Combust and Rending Volley to potentially stop any Exarch/Twin shenanigans. Path to Exile is also a flexible option from your sideboard, as it can be applied in other matchups, but note that it can still be countered. And of course, Destructive Revelry or Wear//Tear can be incredibly valuable, as it will destroy the Splinter Twin enchantment and other artifacts such as Spellskite and Batterskull. Keep in mind that preventing your death from the combo should be your main priority, and this is one of the rare times where it is absolutely acceptable to bring in cards that cannot damage the opponent.

Now, depending on your opponent’s postboard position, it may even be correct for them to sideboard out the Splinter Twin enchantment in order to render some of your enchantment removal dead. This isn’t uncommon, especially in the TarmoTwin and Grixis Twin matches since they have a stronger, aggressive angle of attack. When in doubt, it’s always best to remain proactive. Playing Molten Rain when you’re on the play to keep them off of double Red mana is a preemptive strike that can keep your guessing to a minimum. You may also decide to cut back on your Wear//Tear or Revelry, but under no circumstances should you skimp on Rending Volley, Path to Exile, or Combust. You cannot take that risk.

Your weak cards will be the spells that don’t target creatures, such as Skullcrack and Lava Spike, and Rift Bolt due to the fact that it’s pretty slow and an easy Remand target. With that said, you do need to consider keeping your curve low enough to play multiple spells a turn and also have access to spells that just don’t care about Spellskite. It can be a delicate balance.

TIPS AND TRICKS

-If you suspect that their open mana represents a counterspell, then you’re probably right. Don’t just run out an Eidolon into a Spell Snare just to see “if they have it.”

-Managing the stack is very important and cannot be stressed enough. Besides tapping down your attackers, the second most common target for a Pestermite or Exarch’s enter the battlefield trigger is your lands. If you need that mana, use it while the trigger is still on the stack.

-Snapcaster Mages will often be used defensively against you. Use the stack wisely and kill it before the graveyard trigger resolves and before it can be declared as a blocker.

-Don’t underestimate a Twin player’s resourcefulness. A Splinter Twin attached to a Snapcaster Mage or a Tarmogoyf can still win games.

-Keep an eye on your life total as the game draws out. Sometimes you’re just a couple of Lightning Bolts and a Snapcaster Mage away from death and you don’t even realize it.

-Spellskite can change the nature of this game in an instant, keeping you from stopping the combo. If you can risk killing it early, the you should. Otherwise, you’ll be trying to outmaneuver it all game.

WRAP UP

Splinter Twin is one of Modern’s top dogs for a reason. It’s flexibility and versatile game plan allow it to play the long game with the threat of a turn 4 win, which Burn can be soft to. With that said, Burn is still able to capitalize on Twin’s somewhat slightly painful manabase and its occasional slow starts. Stay aggressive while holding up spells to disrupt the combo, be mindful of the countermagic, utilize the stack to your advantage, and you’ll stand a solid chance.

BUT WAIT, THERE’S MORE!

Dragons of Tarkir has come out since my last article was written and it has definitely brought something to the table by giving us Atarka’s Command. This card is quite the powerhouse, enabling Burn to rush on the offensive harder with an anthem effect and giving the deck additional “Skullcrack” abilities. And while there is no doubt that this card is worthy of inclusion in the deck’s 75, a few questions remain on where exactly in the deck Atarka’s Command is most effective. Recent States results vary, with some decks running the full set in the main and moving Skullcracks to the sideboard, and some decks jamming a couple copies of Atarka’s Command in the sideboard to supplement the maindeck Skullcracks. Some decks even ran a full set of Skullcrack and Atarka’s Command in the maindeck! It’s been a mixed bag, to say the least.

And while there seems to be no wrong way to run the card, it is worth noting that maindecking Atarka’s Command will inevitably result in a slightly more painful manabase to the traditional RW Burn build. In the right metagame, this could be of no consequence, but in a meta chock full of Burn, Affinity, and even Zoo, this could be problematic. However, in an Abzan or Twin heavy metagame, this could be exactly what the doctor ordered. The real questions is probably this: how comfortable are you taking more damage from your manabase? If you just don’t care, or have a plan to cut certain colors postboard, then it’s certainly feasible to run Command in the main.
