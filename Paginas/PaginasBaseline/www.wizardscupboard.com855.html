The Modern Compendium – Part 2

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath
 
 
There have been some minor shifts in the popular decks since last month. Merfolk won GP Copenhagen, and an additional copy made the top 4. That caused a pretty large spike in popularity in the following weeks. Other than that, Grisix looks to be finding its place in Modern, with the control variant moving up a few percentage points. Twin has gotten even more popular, but I can’t seem to find a specific reason. It’s just a good deck and more people appear to be picking it up.

R/G Tron

Metagame Percentage: 6% (with Blue Tron)
Deck Category: Ramp Combo
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview
This has been the premier ramp strategy in Modern between the banning of Cloudpost and the appearance of Amulet Bloom. The cards Urza’s Mine, Urza’s Powerplant, and Urza’s Tower can generate seven mana as early as turn three. Karn Liberated, Wurmcoil Engine, Ugin, the Spirit Dragon, and Oblivion Stone are the major payoff cards, with a single copy of Emrakul, the Aeons Torn at the top.
 
Game Plan
R/G Tron is designed to assemble the three Urza lands as fast as possible to generate obscene amounts of mana. Sylvan Scrying, Expedition Map, and Ancient Stirrings are all used to find the nonbasic lands. An early Karn Liberated, Ugin, the Spirit Dragon, or Wurmcoil Engine will often be enough to win the game unaided. Ancient Stirrings pulls double duty, searching additional threats in addition to lands. An Eye of Ugin can be found with Sylvan Scrying and Expedition Map, and will produce a stream of threats. If the game goes long enough, Eye of Ugin can search for a copy of Emrakul, the Aeons Torn.  The rest of the deck is filled with utility cards and cheap spells that can replace themselves.  Chromatic Star and Chromatic Sphere are present to help fix colored mana requirements.
 
Strengths
This deck is ridiculously powerful against fair decks. Wurmcoil Engine can an absolute nightmare for some decks, and a turn three Karn Liberated can start annihilating lands, threats, or the opponent’s hand . R/G Tron preys on the midrange and control decks of the format, due to the inevitable trump of Eye of Ugin into Emrakul, the Aeons Torn. This is one of the few decks in the format that has a noticeably positive matchup against Junk and Jund. The deck usually has room to play Pyroclasm in the maindeck, which can greatly improve its matchup against some of the aggressive decks (Affinity, Elves, etc…).
 
Weaknesses
Combo decks are a huge problem for R/G Tron. Twin is a particularly bad matchup, as R/G Tron cannot start casting expensive spells until Twin is ready to execute its combo. Also, the tap effect from Deceiver Exarch and Pestermite acts as a Time Walk against R/G Tron, as it will lock up two or three mana during the upkeep. Other fast combo decks can simply race R/G Tron, as it has very few ways to interact. Extensive sideboarding dilutes the R/G Tron deck, which slows it down substantially. Several hate cards exist and are difficult for R/G Tron to overcome. Blood Moon, Sowing Salt, and Stony Silence are all between difficult and impossible to beat.
 
Variations
There is a blue version of the Tron deck, but it is covered in its own section. There are a lot of fundamental differences between the two decks. Smaller variations can occur with G/R Tron, but it is mostly the pilots customizing their suite of threats. All is Dust, Sundering Titan, Mindslaver, Platinum Angel, Ulamog, the Infinite Gyre, and Kozilek, Butcher of Truth have all seen play, but they are not common.

Blue Tron

Metagame Percentage: 6% (with R/G Tron)
Deck Category: Ramp Control
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview
Blue Tron trades the raw speed in R/G Tron for additional resiliency and the deck space to play Gifts Ungiven. The deck can control the game with counterspells like Condescend and sweepers like Wrath of God until it can cast or cheat out a giant threat.  The deck is sometimes referred to as Gifts Tron or U/W Tron
 
Game Plan
The Blue Tron deck can still generate large amounts of mana quickly, but it is not built to rely on that. There is usually a more diverse suite of threats, which is encouraged by the inclusion of Gifts Ungiven. The deck is usually capable of generating a value Gifts Ungiven Pile for card advantage, threats, or sweepers. However, if only two cards are chosen with Gifts Ungiven, they will both go to the graveyard. This means that a Gifts Ungiven for a giant creature and Unburial Rites can put that giant creature into play on the following turn.
 
Strengths
Blue Tron is slightly weaker than R/G Tron in its best matchups. In exchange for that, it improves against the decks that R/G struggles against. The counterspell suite somewhat improves the combo matchup. The ability to cheat out an Iona, Shield of Imeria, Elesh Norn, Grand Cenobite, or Sundering Titan can quickly lock out certain decks, and is one of the biggest reasons to play the deck. Blue Tron is capable of casting huge Sphinx’s Revelations too, which will attract some players. More importantly, Blue Tron is capable of playing a fair game of magic through hate cards or without assembling Tron. Cards like Blood Moon, while obnoxious, are not impossible to beat.
 
Weaknesses
The Gifts Ungiven package adds some inconsistency to the deck, both in cards and in mana. R/G Tron is filled with colorless spells and single colors of mana. Blue Tron has demanding color restrictions, with spells requiring double blue, double white, and a small black splash. The mix of spells necessary for Gifts Ungiven means the deck runs a variety of silver bullet creatures that may not always be good against a given opponent.
 
Variations
This deck is kind of a variation of R/G Tron, but there are some key differences. Blue Tron will occasionally give up the Unburial Rites package for green cards. Sylvan Scrying, Explore, and Life from the Loam are some of the most common additions.

Amulet Bloom

Metagame Percentage: 4%
Deck Category: Combo
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview
Amulet Bloom has been a fringe player in Modern for years. Mattias Hunt made an impressive run at Pro Tour Born of the Gods before a string of losses kept him out of the Top 8. However, the deck started to truly gain popularity at Pro Tour Fate Reforged, when Justin Cohen piloted it to a second place finish. Cohen explained that the deck resembles a puzzle, presenting several decisions each turn. It eschews the opportunity to react to an opponent, focusing exclusively on executing its own plan.
 
Game Plan
The primary goal of Amulet Bloom is to quickly kill the opponent with a fast Primeval Titan or Hive Mind. Amulet Bloom uses the synergy between the Ravnica bounce lands and Amulet of Bloom to generate mana, and the cards Summer Bloom and Azusa, Lost but Seeking to deploy additional lands. A Primeval Titan can find Boros Garrison and Slayer’s Stronghold, and an Amulet of Vigor allows the Primeval Titan to attack immediately. Beyond that, Hive Mind provides an additional threat. Amulet Bloom incorporates both Summoner’s Pact and Pact of Negation, and Hive Mind converts these into kill conditions if the opponent is unable to pay for them.
 
Strengths
Blistering speed is one of the greatest strengths of Amulet Bloom. The deck is capable of winning the game on the second turn with Hive Mind or a specific Primeval Titan draw. Even with the speed, the deck is capable of winning long games, simply by deploying lands regularly until the expensive threats can be cast. The Transmute ability on Tolaria West adds resiliency, tutoring for Summoner’s Pact for additional threats. Primeval Titan can even facilitate this by fetching for Tolaria West and a Ravnica bounce land to return the Tolaria West to the hand. Overall, Amulet Bloom is quite resilient due to the difficulty in disrupting land drops. Wizards of the Coast has moved away from printing land destruction, so new sets offer little in ways to fight Amulet Bloom.
 
Weaknesses
There are few newer cards capable of dismantling Amulet Bloom, but Blood Moon is game ending. The deck is capable of recovering from land destruction like Fulminator Mage, but a resolved Blood Moon is basically impossible to beat. Amulet Bloom has minimal interaction, with Pact of Negation used mostly to protect instead of disrupt. Extremely fast decks can simply race Amulet Bloom, and there is little it can do to stop that.
 
Variations
Amulet Bloom does not have much room for extensive modifications without disrupting the core strategy. There are a few cards that can be changed, such as the number of Azusa, Lost but Seeking, but variations are limited. Dragonlord Dromoka has seen a small amount of play, as it can be difficult for delver decks to beat it.

Elves

Metagame Percentage: 4%
Deck Category: Aggro
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview
Elves has been a popular tribe for far longer than the Modern format has existed. However, it has lacked many of the powerhouse cards available to the Legacy Elves deck. Glimpse of Nature is banned in the format, and Natural Order falls outside the Modern card pool. Wirewood Symbiote is also unavailable in Modern, removing the deck’s fallback plan. With that said, Elves in Modern has finally started to gain some traction, thanks in part to Collected Company. Combined with Chord of Calling, Elves can quickly assemble a swarm and overpower opponents.
 
Game Plan
Activating the Overrun ability of Ezuri, Renegade Leader is the preferred route to victory. The Elf deck floods the board with as many creatures as possible to take advantage of this ability. Elvish Archdruid, along with the combination of Heritage Druid and Nettle Sentinel, provide all the mana anyone could ever want. Collected Company and Elvish Visionary provide a steady stream of threats. Chord of Calling and Fauna Shaman give the deck multiple copies of powerful, narrow effects, such as Reclamation Sage, Spellskite, and Scavenging Ooze.
 
Strengths
The deck produces a quick clock, rapidly filling the board with creatures. Elves is similar to Affinity in its method of attack, deploying several threats and winning before the opponent has time to remove all of them. Ezuri, Renegade Leader, Elvish Archdruid, and Elvish Champion make the individual creatures into sizable threats.  Elves also has access to a toolbox of specific hate, fueled by Chord of Calling and Fauna Shaman. This leaves the deck’s strengths partly in the hands of the pilot. A quick Scavenging Ooze against Ghrishoalbrand or Reclamation Sage against Affinity can delay an opponent by several turns, and the Elf deck will capitalize on that delay.
 
Weaknesses
Board sweepers, while relatively uncommon, are devastating for the Elf deck. Pyroclasm, Anger of the Gods, Supreme Verdict, Night of Soul’s Betrayal, and Golgari Charm can reduce the elf deck to an irrecoverable mess. Ezuri’s regenerate ability can mitigate some of these, but it will be the prime target for removal. Snapcaster and Lightning Bolt will also be a problem for this deck, as cheap, reusable removal can be problematic. Overall, the deck has most of the same weaknesses as Affinity, without some of the backbreaking hate cards. The tradeoff is a lack of comparable marquee cards from Affinity, such as Cranial Plating.
 
Variations
There are very few variations to the Elf deck, beyond the suite of toolbox creatures. The most likely splash color would be black for options like Thoughtseize and Shaman of the Pack. White could provide Gaddock Teeg, Ethersworn Cannonist, Eidolon of Rhetoric, and Path to Exile, but most of those could hinder the Elf deck as well. One of the draws of the Elf deck is the relative cheapness in constructing it (mostly inexpensive creatures and a single color manabase), and this will discourage splashing any colors.

Junk (Abzan Midrange)

Metagame Percentage: 4%
Deck Category: Midrange
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview
Junk is the definition of a midrange deck. It is filled with cheap, versatile, and powerful cards designed to grind an opponent’s resources down until there is nothing left. Once each player is reduced to almost nothing, Junk relies on its individually powerful cards to finish a game before the opponent can rebuild.
 
Game Plan
This deck relies on the raw power level of its cards, eschewing synergy almost completely. The black discard spells Thoughtseize and Inquisition of Kozilek provide early protection from fast combo decks. Abrupt Decay is one of the most versatile removal spells available, capable of removing anything from an opposing Tarmogoyf to Cranial Plating to Pyromancer’s Ascension. Tarmogoyf, Scavanging Ooze, and Siege Rhino provide ample power to stabilize a board and finish a game. (Yes, this got copied almost word for word from Jund. They are almost the same deck)
 
Strengths
Junk’s worst matchups are still completely winnable. The focus of the deck is to bring both players down to minimal resources, then rely on individual cards to win the game. Thoughseize, Inquisition of Kozilek, and Liliana of the Veil are versatile enough that this strategy works against almost any opponent. Junk’s removal spells, Abrupt Decay and Path to Exile, can handle almost any threat. Seige Rhino, Tarmogoyf, and Lingering Souls can all win games without support. White also provides the strongest selection of sideboard cards. The power level of each single card in Junk is among the highest of any deck in Modern, and that is where it’s strength lies.  The strength of Junk over Jund comes mostly from a decision between Lingering Souls and Lightning Bolt.  Junk will often be the stronger deck if Lingering Souls lines up well against the format.
 
Weaknesses
Junk will not have any free wins or highly favorable matchups. Even when Junk is operating properly, the opponent can still string together a series of top-decks to win the game. There are also some strategies in Modern that Junk is ill-equipped to fight. Decks like Tron are full of cards that Junk cannot favorably interact with, and heavy sideboarding is necessary to improve these matchups. Junk also struggles with the size limitation for sideboards. There are plenty of options to drastically improve certain matchups, but it isn’t possible to play them all. Improving a matchup against a deck could take 5-6 cards in a sideboard, but those sideboard might be better used for something else. Often, a Junk player will have to make concessions against certain decks, and they will rely on avoiding them in a tournament.
 
Variations
Junk is mostly a variation of Jund at this point. The power of Kologath’s Command has pushed Junk solidly down into a tier two deck. Some versions of Junk don’t use Siege Rhino, instead playing more interactive spells and/or Dark Confidant. There was a variation of Junk that did well at Pro Tour Fate Reforged, which included Noble Hierarch/Birds of Paradise and focused on the strength of Gavony Township. However, that deck was more of the transition between the banning of Birthing Pod and the printing of Collected Company. It doesn’t see much play now, but both Loxodon Smiter and Wilt-Leaf Liege could be strong in a field full of Liliana of the Veil.
