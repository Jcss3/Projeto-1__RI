This month, I start to tell my personal Commander story by reflecting on how I became aware of, involved in, and singularly passionate about this particular MTG format, as well as what can be learned from this experience.

Chapter 1: Zendikar

On November 22nd, 2009, I decided to honor the anniversary of JFK’s assasination by scrounging around for the newspapers of that day handed down to me, the family history buff. As I reached for a particularly heavy, overstuffed box on the top shelf, a shiny cube spiraled down toward the floor, landing on the instep of my left foot.

I haven’t weighed a full, 400-count, hard-plastic card box recently, but the impact of that massive block had me jumping and howling in agony. I recalled liquidating my collection of tournament-worthy cards at the end of my college days (2004) at BGSU Ohio. I had kept many of the useless rares like Mahamoti Djinns, Sengir Vampires, Balduvian Hordes, and other junk from that time… like Lions Eye Diamonds. There was also a whole 1600 count shoebox of Uncommons, including about 7-8 Force of Will and a playset of foil Standstills. Â I googled MTG, wondering if the game still existed or anyone still played, and found that they had just reprinted a mechanic I was very familiar with: -Fetch Lands.-

By then, Zendikar had been out for several months, and decks such as Craig Wescoe’s White Weenie and Boros Bushwacker had gained popularity, and the Landfall mechanic had proven to be a resoundingly successful strategy. Â It was cheap to build compared to the Jund or Naya decks in the Alara-Zen Metagame. Â Seeing as how I was relatively uncommitted to playing at the tournament level, I decided to acquire the cards for those cheap decks, namely Goblin Guides, Arid Mesas, Marsh Flats, and Scalding Tarns, all of which retailed for $10 each at the time. Â I spent about $100 at the newly opened Wizard’s Cupboard, and went to another Tampa Bay area store to draft Zendikar and trade for lands with my binder full of Thran Dynamos and Hypnotic Specters.
