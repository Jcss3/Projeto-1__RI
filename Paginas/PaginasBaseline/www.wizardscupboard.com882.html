The Competitive Commander’s Guide to: Oloro Esper Control

 

 

By: Craig Sidorowicz

Ahh November!  Once a year commander players get a dedicated special treat in the form of five new decks packed with all sorts of goodies to enhance our builds or try entirely new commanders (Planeswalkers!!!).  I took a break from greedily unboxing and meticulously sorting my complete set of Commander ‘14 to bring you a guide to one of my decks with several distinctions that begin with: “The Most.”

Those titles include:

“The Most Competitive”

“The Most Expensive”

“The Most Difficult to Play”

“The Most Difficult to Play Against”

 

Some of you may be tempted to stop reading at the sight of “Most Competitive.”  I understand, that for some, the mentality and intensity of competition found at the FNMs and PTQ’s has no place at the MTG Commander table.  I wholeheartedly agree, but on occasion I like to pull out the stops and crank up the difficulty to “Hard Mode.”  To do this, you must intentionally make yourself the “Archenemy,” and this deck will elevate you to that status within the first several turns of the game.   This is not a deck to play at a table with newer players, or players with a vastly underpowered card pool.  This is a deck ideally meant to be played against similarly powered decks, or in a tournament setting where eliminating players and winning is the goal.  Keep in mind that Commander is designed to be a casual and social atmosphere, and it’s important for competitive players to evaluate each pod, (by that I mean consider the feelings of the unique human beings at the table) and select a deck that is best suited for the occasion.  In short, a good social player must give up the desire to “crush newbies in order to inflate his ego.”

 

Some may balk at “Most Expensive.”  Yes, this deck’s price tag is measured in the “thousands of dollars” and may seem inaccessible to those with limited MTG budgets, I’ll comment that acquiring the cards for this deck on a public school teacher’s salary did not come easily or quickly, but took many months of trading, selling, saving, and sacrificing other decks/deck ideas in order to make it happen.  It is my hope that many of you with a similar goal might find inspiration here.   “Most difficult to play and play against” is largely based on my experience and feedback obtained from other players.  When the plan of this deck is executed correctly, all of your opponents will concede, or “Tap Out” as the MMA-Fan in me likes to imagine.  The problem is, it is often very difficult to put that plan into action while you are the archenemy.  This guide seeks to provide a strategy for a successful execution of the plan.

The Plan:

  1. Obtain resources through “Artifact Ramp”
  2. Use enchantments and board clearing sorceries to establish board control and prevent/make it unprofitable to attack you.  Best case scenario: They attack each other.
  3. Lock out your opponents by destroying all lands and preventing any sort of comeback.
  4. Opponents concede or die slowly to incremental damage from Luminarch Ascension, Oloro Ageless Ascetic or Planeswalker effects.

Part One: The Early Game and Obtaining Mana

Here is your Ramp Package:

 

The goal with these twelve slots was to find the cheapest color-fixing and colorless mana ramping artifacts, AKA “Mana Rocks.”  Most need no explanation, but I will say that Land Tax is by far the best card you can have in your opening hand. Period.  Mana Vault and Grim Monolith have drawbacks, but they interact well with the new Planeswalker Teferi.  Commander’s Sphere and Lotus Bloom are welcome additions that pair well with Sun Titan.  Dimir Keyrune is included over Dimir Signet for its utility against opposing Planeswalkers.   Land Choices:

 

First, our color-fixing lands.  I chose the three appropriate Original Duals, Shocklands, and Filter Lands, as well as the three on-color only fetchlands.  In the past I was a fan of including all nine possible fetches that would fit, but this inevitably led to too few basic lands to search for.  Falling victim to Blood Moon or Back to Basics is always embarrassing and awkward.

 

 

So I use 11 Basics.  According to Deckstats, the deck has:

  • 37 White Mana Symbols
  • 19 Black Mana Symbols
  • 19 Blue Mana Symbols

This information led me to apply the ratio of roughly 2:1:1 (Plains:Island:Swamp) for calculating how many basic lands I needed of each type.  The color-fixing lands package is equalized, so I didn’t need to do much more figuring than this.  By taking a look at the utility nonbasic lands package, we also have colored mana coming from Command Tower, Urborg, Tomb of Yawgmoth, Creeping Tar Pit (which also beats up opposing planeswalkers!), Bojuka Bog, Flagstones of Trokair, and the all-powerful Serra’s Sanctum.

 

 

Most of these lands explain their purpose with a quick read of their text.  Kor Haven and Maze of Ith help us out with our second goal, which is controlling the enemy attacks as well as saving our lives.  I don’t see players using Glacial Chasm very often, but it can be really amazing with Crucible of Worlds or Sun Titan recursion.  The life loss is not too bad either, considering we are playing a Commander that passively gains us a reasonably high amount of life.  Since this deck only plays four creatures, The Tabernacle at Pendrell Vale is a disgustingly one-sided card.  The price tag on this one is exorbitant, so if you would rather use Magus of the Tabernacle, I understand!

Part Two:  Building the Fortress of Fear.

The immediate goal of this deck after achieving a reasonable amount of mana is to start deploying the disruptive and defensive artifacts and enchantments to protect us from attack, and to encourage our opponents to take their bloodthirst elsewhere.  I generally want to see one or two of these in my opening hand, preferably in the 2-3 converted mana cost bracket.  Aura of Silence, Torpor Orb, and Arcane Laboratory are the most disruptive to enemy players looking to ramp or build a mana-rock collection to match our own.  I only use Aura of SIlence’s removal ability when it’s an absolute emergency, or I can bring it back immediately by attacking with Sun Titan.

Next we have the “Pay X to attack” enchantments: Collective Restraint and Sphere of Safety.  An opponent looking to do some damage may look elsewhere.  These two are generally better than Ghostly Prison and/or Propaganda, which don’t do as much, but cost a bit less.  I’d much rather topdeck a Sphere of Safety or Collective restraint later in the game, because they make the Gaea’s Cradle-toting elf overrun player have to calculate that much harder.   Forcefield can also be an MVP in the defensive realm, especially against those looking to finish you with 21 Commander Damage.

Humility is the most powerful card in this deck because it helps shuts down creature decks (or can completely lock them out if you choose to play Night of Souls Betrayal alongside it), and requires an instant or sorcery-based solution to remove it.  If you study up on this card’s rulings, it basically has a built-in Torpor Orb effect which prevents a solution like Harmonic Sliver or Indrik Stomphowler.  Aura Shards does get around Humility (but not Torpor Orb due to its wording).  The other important fact to know about Humility is how the “layers” of overlapping effects work, as well as “timestamps.”  These aspects of MTG rules can take quite some explanation, but long story short, If an effect pumps a creature’s power, such as Overrun, that creature gets to keep that power boost if that effect happens after Humility is already in play.  An animated artifact or creature gains the power and toughness that it is supposed to.  Creeping Tar Pit will be a 3/2 when animated, for example, as well as unblockable, because unblockable is not a keyword ability, but it will not be able to tap for man).  Additionally, equipping a Sword or equipment that boosts power will be effective in pumping that creature’s power if it is equipped after the initial Humility time-stamp is in play.   Here are a few helpful links to provide more insight on how humility works:

Magiccards.info’s Humility Page

MTGsalvation’s Humility Page

Part 3: Lock the Door, and Throw Away the Key

Now for the Mana lockout.  To perform it perfectly you will need a combination of 12 mana, including two to three White, two blue, and one black.  Tap all of the mana, cast Armageddon or Cataclysm. Play Land Equilibrium, then play Nether Void.  If you do not play another land, and your opponent has no way to remove Land Equilibrium on the board already,  your opponents can not play land for the rest of the game.  If you think your opponent has a Krosan Grip or another instant spell that could remove the Land Equilibrium, You can play Nether Void first, but this will require 15 mana to resolve the lock.  Players may continue to play, but they will be limited to using their existing mana rocks, or elves (unless Humility is in play!) for mana.  If The Tabernacle is in play, your opponents say goodbye to their armies during their upkeep.  If you have a win condition (a Planeswalker or Luminarch Ascension) you should be able to force concessions from most opponents.  It’s also worth noting that an early Land Equilibrium powered out by mana rocks can put you in the driver’s seat for the entire game against decks that want to ramp with extra lands via Cultivate or Oracle of Mul-Daya.

The WMD’s

This deck contains a formidable arsenal of Mass removal as well as a few pieces of spot removal to take care of threats on a permanent basis.  The secret here is that all of these cards either exile, “bottom-deck,” return to owner’s hand, Give -1/-1’s, or Destroy without the possibility of Regeneration.  Vanilla “destroy” effects like Day of Judgment just don’t make the cut because there are so many indestructible and regenerative creatures out there.  We also would rather exile, Bottom-deck, or Leyline of the Void the creatures of reanimator-style decks.  Cataclysm is particularly gross if you set it up in your favor, but keep in mind that it also says: “Destroy all Planeswalkers.” A solid play is to have mana floating, and then cast a Planeswalker post Cataclysm.

It’s worth explaining that we don’t play counterspells in this deck because they are just too narrow and singular in their application.  Counterspells are good in Commander decks that are trying to protect a large horde of creatures from Wrath effects.  Enchantments and artifacts are less susceptible to mass-removal simply because cards like Shatterstorm and Tranquility are not played as much as they should be.   For us, the question becomes: “Why devote a slot to a card that can only answer one problem?”  Mass removal is much more effective against permanents, and it’s more efficient to have and play an additional threat or recur our threats rather than save something with a counter.  Additionally, this Commander deck does not have room to accommodate enough counterspells to control the outcome of all of our opponents plays, and given that we usually have three opponents,  the deck would  rather cast or deploy blanket effects to contain as many threats as possible.  Our instant-speed single-target removal spells seem to break this rule, but the word “exile” is present on all three, making them too powerful to pass up at their cost.  Return to Dust often over-performs when cast as Sorcery speed, and is the preferred way to cast this spell.

The Shoulders of Giants: Creatures and Planeswalkers

The only “creatures” that made the cut were our Gods, Giants, and Planeswalkers. Oloro Ageless Ascetic provides us with a nice life-buffer and the potential for card-draw if we end up playing him.  It’s not uncommon to be at 50+ life before my opponents get around to attacking.  His “in play” ability can be a bit hard to take advantage of in this deck, especially since untapping with him is our only source of consistent life-gain, but It’s always worth casting him if you have nothing better to do.   The Gods Erebos and Thassa are two of the most versatile creatures in Magic, are undercosted and “splashable,” (meaning they only have one colored mana symbol, making them easy to cast).   and synergize well with our “enchantments matter” subtheme.  Getting them the devotion they need can be difficult,  but I’m more interested in having those two around for their card draw and card selection abilities.  It is worth noting that if Erebos gets stolen he can shut off your life gain ability, so be aware if your opponent is playing blue and black. Elspeth Knight Errant gives us both offensive and defensive abilities, and her “ultimate” generally reads: “Game Over” for our opponents.  I like her over any of her other incarnations because she is the most offensive, and effectively boosts your life total because your opponents must deal with her before you untap with 8 loyalty counters on her. Jace the Mind Sculptor is quite often the finisher against those who are too proud to concede once you apply a board lock.  We also need to draw cards and bounce threats.  Jace more than earns a spot in this list. Liliana of the Veil is great against an opposing “Voltron/Hexproof” general because of her “edict” ability, and the 3-for-one discard is very disruptive.  She will die quickly if she does not have proper protection, but I generally play her as soon as I can, and if she dies I can recur her with Sun Titan later.  Her Ultimate is probably one of the weakest in this group of Planeswalkers. Sorin Lord of Innistrad is most useful for his -2 Emblem ability because of how it interacts with Humility.  The emblem effect(s) are applied in the layer above Humility’s effect, so your creatures still get the +1’s while humility is in play.  I’ve made some pretty mean Creeping Tar-Pits and Dimir Keyrunes (which also keep their abilities when you activate them while Humility is already in play).  His Ultimate can swing games heavily in your favor  as well. Fresh out of the box, Teferi, Temporal Archmage might be a bit cost-prohibitive, and may not have the immediate impact I want a 6-mana card to have, but I’m interested to try him out mainly because of his +1 Scry/Draw ability and -1 ability.  If I -1 after I cast him, and untap a Grim Monolith, Mana Vault, Sol Ring, and/or two lands, he is essentially a free spell.  I’m not sure that his -10 ability will ever get activated, but I’ll be happy if I can gain that achievement someday!  I have not played with him enough to know whether or not he is worthy of this spot.  I could see several other cards competing for this spot, especially Tamiyo the Moon Sage, Night of Soul’s Betrayal, or even Treachery. Finally Tezerret the Seeker is the best choice (over Agent of Bolas) because of how he can grab our defensive artifacts like Torpor Orb and Cursed Totem.  He can also help pay for his cost by untapping mana rocks, and has a really cool interaction with Sensei’s Divining Top, which tends to be the first artifact I search for with him.  My usual second search is Crucible of Worlds.   I’ve hinted at the power of Sun Titan, and I hope that you can begin to put together the interactions that showcase the raw power of this card.  He is the only Enter-The-Battlefield creature to make the cut, and despite the negative interaction with Torpor Orb, he still stands out as the best attacking creature for this deck.   The Glue:  Draw, Tutors, and Recursion Pulling all of the strands of our plan together are the dedicated draw engines and tutors.  In every case, considerations were taken to get the most return from our investment.

 

For tutors: Demonic Tutor, Grim Tutor, Vampiric Tutor, Enlightened Tutor, and Lim Dul’s Vault. they had:

  1. Lowest mana Cost
  2. Smallest drawback: Those drawbacks include ability to search for the widest amount of spells in the deck, which is why Mystical Tutor, though amazing, did not make the cut in this list.  It can only search for 7 cards, none of which win the game immediately like Insurrection, Overwhelming Stampede, or Rite of Replication.

For draw spells, we also took into consideration points 1 and 2, but also added:

  1. Passive/low activation/upkeep cost.  Mystic Remora’s upkeep can get pricey, but remember it can be brought back regularly with Sun Titan.

Much has already been said about Sun Titan’s gravitas in this list, and Replenish’s application in a deck with 20 enchantments is fairly obvious, so the focus here will be dedicated to Ill-Gotten Gains.   Many players favor the other Urza’s Saga Recursion spell, Yawgmoth’s Will, but think of the devastated looks on your opponents faces when you cast this with a Leyline of the Void in play, or after you’ve Nihil Spell-bombed a graveyard or two with your pal “Big Sunny.”

Why is “Card X” Not in the Deck, and how can I build this list on a budget?

To answer the first question would take thousands of words and require mind-reading abilities, so to be candid:  I’ve tried many different cards in this deck, and over the course of a year playing this list I’ve found what works best for:

  1. Executing the plan without great cost to me:  You don’t see Stasis in this list because It requires a dedicated set of off-plan cards such as Capsize or Time Elemental to be most effective.  It’s also about drawing a line in the sand as far as efficiently shutting down the opponents, vs. delaying the game for no good reason.
  2. Mana Considerations:  Wrath of God is superior to Damnation in this deck because I play more white mana producing lands and fewer that produce black mana.
  3. Avoiding Infinite Combo:  As mentioned in my previous article, Infinite combo is frowned upon in casual play and is banned in competitive play in most of the metagames I play in.  Yes, Helm of Obedience can easily kill people with Leyline of the Void in play and end the game faster, as could Power Artifact on my Grim Monolith paired with Stroke of Genius.  The problem I’ve found in the past is that once you execute these combos once, it loses it’s fun, and in future games these combos become a crutch to allow an “undeserved win,” simply by tutoring up a combo piece and slamming it on the table if the original plan fails.

Budgetary concerns   There are many expensive cards that can be replaced in this deck with cheaper, less effective versions:  For example:

  • The Tabernacle at Pendrell Vale — Magus of the Tabernacle (Though the cost and vulnearability increase dramatically.  You also have to pay for his upkeep.)
  • Grim Tutor — Diabolic Tutor
  • Nether Void — Sphere of Resistance
  • Original Dual Lands/Filter Lands: — Check Lands such as Drowned Catacomb or Glacial Fortress.  Ice Age “Painlands” such as Adarkar Wastes and Apocalypse’s Caves of Koilos have all been reprinted many times and are easy to find in trade binders.  You might also use Scry Lands from Theros block, or Lifegain taplands from Zendikar or Khans for their interaction with Oloro.
  • Fetch Lands — Were just reprinted!  Get them now while they are being opened in drafts and the supply is high/price is low.
  • Liliana and Jace can be replaced with Liliana Vess and Jace Architect of Thought.  Vess provides an additional tutor, and Architect of Thought helps to reduce the damage you take from attackers.  Tamiyo also has decent applications in this deck, and her Ultimate can be used in place of Elspeth’s.
  • Land Equilibrium can be replaced by Desolation or Mana Vortex, as well as Infernal Darkness/ Contamination (although Contamination will require other Higher-priced cards like Bitterblossom)
  • Forcefield can be replaced with Crawlspace, but it doesn’t help as much vs Voltron generals that want to kill you with Commander damage.
  • Maze of Ith — Mystifying Maze
  • Other cheap defensive alternatives to Forcefield include: Island Sanctuary, Propaganda, Ghostly Prison, No Mercy, Norn’s Annex, and Meishin the Mind Cage.

Until Next Time!   Thank you for hanging in there as this was a pretty long article!  Hopefully I’ve provided you with a working understanding of the deck’s plan, how it achieves it, as well as some of the more nuanced plays the deck has that I’ve learned the hard way through trial and error.  Please let me know if you have any questions, and I’d be happy to answer them in the comments below.   -Craig   Official EDH/Commander Rules About the Author: Craig Sidorowicz is a public school teacher who will never leave the 6th grade.  He has been playing Magic: The Gathering since Ice Age, and EDH/Commander for Five years.  He can regularly be found at playing and trading at The Wizards Cupboard in Seminole Florida.
