Four Scorn and Seven Years Ago

Wizards of the Coast wanted to be absolutely sure that a horde of Dragons made their way into the Standard format. The obvious way to manage that was to print a bunch of dragons with high power levels at competitive mana costs. This definitely happened.

The development team for Dragons of Tarkir went beyond simply printing some good dragons.  They produced a cycle of uncommons with a “dragons matter” theme. These cards that make players excited to have dragons in hand or on the battlefield, even when said dragons aren’t currently reducing the opponent into a smoldering ruin.

Instant

These cards are rather inelegant with their giant text boxes, but their power is more than sufficient for constructed play. Several of these cards have already found their way into winning decks.

There are a few factors that influence how often each card in this cycle will get played. These considerations are:
-How powerful the card is when the “Dragon clause” is not met
-How much of a downgrade, if any, is necessary to incorporate sufficient dragons into the decklist
-How early in a game does the card need to be cast to gain the full effect

 

Orator of Ojutai

Without the “Dragon clause”
Orator of Ojutai is a functional reprint of Angelic Wall when the clause is not met. I haven’t scoured every decklist in the history of Magic, but I’m pretty sure Angelic Wall has not had a successful Magic career.

Dragons in white:

Arashin Soverign
Dragonlord Dromoka
Dragonlord Ojutai
Dromoka, the Eternal
Ojutai, Soul of Winter
Pristine Skywise
Sunscorch Regent

Orator of Ojutai is significantly better on turn two than any future turn. Blocking damage is a cumulative effect, so it needs to be deployed as early as possible to get the maximum effect. Unfortunately, that means that either
-The deck has quite a lot of dragons
-You’ll be casting an Angelic Wall a noticeable percentage of the time.

Conveniently, Frank Karsten already covered the math for Orator of Ojutai. To trigger the “Dragon clause” on turn two on the draw, you’ll need 10 dragons to break 80%.  14 dragons is the minimum to exceed 90%.
Putting 10+ dragons in a pure control deck is not going to work, since that is far too many finishers. It might be possible to supplement finishers with a large number of Icefall Regents (instead of some removal). If you want to work with this card, I’d recommend a R/W, Jeskai, or Mardu list filled with cheap burn and dragons

Silumgar’s Scorn

Without the “Dragon clause”
Silumgar’s Scorn is a 2 mana Force Spike when the “Dragon clause” is not met. This is a powerful effect in the early turns of a game, when players are still developing their mana. This is a reasonable, if unexciting, card to play when the “Dragon clause” is not met.

Dragons in blue:

Dragonlord Ojutai
Dragonlord Silumgar
Icefall Regent
Necromaster Dragon
Ojutai, Soul of Winter
Pristine Skywise
Silumgar, the Drifting Death

Silumgar’s Scorn is good in both the early and late turns of the game. There will be times when your opponent has extra mana available and you can’t trigger the “Dragon clause,” but it won’t be that common. When the game reaches a point where they can pay the extra mana, you will likely have found a dragon. Anticipate and Dig Through Time can help artificially inflate the number of dragons you have, while keeping the actual number closer to 5 or 6.

Silumgar’s Scorn is the best card in this cycle by a good margin, and the results have already started arriving to prove it. Force Spike is a simple card to nullify by simply waiting a turn to cast your spells. That does not work with Silumgar’s Scorn, since the card turns into a functional reprint of Counterspell if the “Dragon clause” is met. Owen Turtenwald discussed the card at length in a recent article (link below), which pretty much covers everything I wanted to say.

One concern about Silumgar’s Scorn is the double blue mana cost. One of the only utility lands in Standard is Haven of the Spirit Dragon, which also incentivizes playing dragons. The decks that play Silumgar’s Scorn will sometimes be unable to generate the correct mana in the beginning turns of the game. This is somewhat mitigated by the continued strength of SIlumgar’s Scorn in the late game.

As an added bonus, a few people have tried to brew Modern decks using Silumgar’s Scorn. Most of these are based on Mutavault and/or Nameless Inversion, since there are very few decks that can reasonably run actual dragons. These haven’t had any real tournament success yet, but it’s an interesting concept.

Several successful decks already exist with Silumgar’s Scorn. Alexander Hayne won GP Krakow with Esper Dragons on 4-19-15 (the decklist is above)

Foul-Tongue Invocation

Without the “Dragon clause”
Unlike the Orator of Ojutai and Silumgar’s Scorn, most of the strength of Foul-Tongue Invocation does not come from the “Dragon clause.” Three mana is a little expensive just to sacrifice a creature, but it would see some amount play even if that was all the card did. Foul-Tongue Invocation answers both Dragonlord Ojutai and SIlumgar, the Drifting Death, and it provides another tool against the Heroic deck.

Dragons in black:

Boltwing Marauder
Deathbringer Regent
Dragonlord Kolaghan
Dragonlord Silumgar
Kolaghan, the Storm’s Fury
Necromaster Dragon
Silumgar, the Drifting Death

The sacrifice effect of Foul-Tongue Invocation get better with fewer creatures in play. Any time there is a single creature in play, Foul-Tongue Invocation will be strong. In the games where the “Dragon clause” matters, it will be important to trigger it early. Situations will arise where it is necessary to cast Foul-Tongue Invocation on an empty board just to gain life.

The weakness of sacrifice effects is that your opponent gets to lose the worst creature they have. There are a lot of Goblin tokens, Satyr Wayfinders, and Sylvan Caryatids in Standard that can make a sacrifice effect look pretty bad. The “Dragon clause” does an acceptable job mitigating this with four additional life. That life gives you additional time to develop your resources and stabilize the board.

Foul-Tongue Invocation has shown up in many of the UB Dragon Control and Esper Dragon decklists. Shota Yasooka’s UB Dragon Control deck from PT Dragons of Tarkir (4/12/15) and Alexander Hayne’s Esper Dragons deck from GP Krakow (4-19-15) both have two copies

 

Draconic Roar

Without the “Dragon clause”
Draconic Roar is closer to Foul-Tongue Invocation than the other two cards. The “Dragon clause” increases the strength of the card, but Draconic Roar’s main purpose is to kill creatures. However, Lightning Strike is a more versatile card that does roughly the same thing. Draconic Roar is acceptable without meeting the “Dragon clause,” but it is clearly worse than Lightning strike if you don’t have sufficient dragons.

There are a large number of decks to look at that include red dragons, because Stormbreath Dragon has been having tournament success since it was printed. Lightning Strike was in most of these decks, since it serves the dual purpose of removing creatures and reducing your opponent’s life total. Draconic Roar helps mitigate the lost tempo of killing an opponent’s creature by dealing additional damage to the opponent. This is a highly desirable effect for decks filled with dragons, as the usual game plan for those decks is to resolve a dragon and attack with it.

This card has is noticeably worse than Lightning Strike against decks with few or no creatures. Draconic Roar cannot deal damage to the opponent unless there is a creature to target. Draconic Roar’s popularity will likely rise and fall with the number of popular decks relying of creatures to win.

So far, Draconic Roar has only been successfully incorporated into the GR Dragons deck (formerly GR Monsters). Chris VanMeter won with the deck at the SCG Open in Syracuse (4/14/15), and that list is above.

Scaleguard Sentinels

Without the “Dragon clause”
Scaleguard Sentinels is a functional reprint of Elvish Warriors (ignoring the creature type) without the “Dragon clause.” That is not strong enough for tournament play.

Dragons in green:
Arashin Soverign
Atarka, World Render
Dragonlord Atarka
Dragonlord Dromoka
Dromoka, the Eternal
Foe-Razer Regent
Harbinger of the Hunt

With the “Dragon clause,” Scaleguard Sentinels is one of the best things you can do in Standard at that rate. It easily outclasses every other creature that costs two mana or less. A 3/4 creature for two mana is close to the average for a Tarmogoyf.

However, Tarmogoyf resides in formats where few creatures are realistically larger than it. Standard is not one of those formats, and Scaleguard Sentinals does not have the luxury. A 3/4 Scaleguard Sentinals only gets a few turns of damage before it might have to contend with creatures like Seige Rhino or Dragonlord Ojutai.

This is overshadowed by the bigger problem with Scaleguard Sentinels. The double green mana cost makes it very difficult to cast on turn two without making significant concessions to the manabase. This is compounded by the need to add colors for additional dragons. Scaleguard Sentinels is only a fantastic rate if it comes down on turn two, so it will need the support of at least 10 dragons (from the math on Orator of Ojutai). To reach a sufficient dragon count, dragons with double off-color mana will be needed (likely Thunderbreak Regent, Thundermaw Hellkite, or Sunscorcher Regent). That is a tough hurtle to overcome

Making the mana issues worse is Haven of the Spirit Dragon. Haven is one of the best tools available to dragon decks to prevent mana flooding (it converts useless excess lands into dragons). However, it only taps for colorless mana. It can be used to supplement mana for non-green dragons, but it is an additional strain on an already taxed manabase.

There have been a few decklists that use Scaleguard Sentinels to help increase Devotion. None of those decks have had much success though, since Scaleguard Sentinels is quite underpowered without the counter.

So you’ve read everything I’ve written and decided I’ve wasted your time. It’s real easy to sound smart while reviewing cards after they have had tournament success. However, those decks don’t show the full picture. The Esper Dragons deck didn’t start with dragons and decide that Silumgar’s Scorn was probably worth testing. Decks started with 4 copies of Silumgar’s Scorn, and then they started looking for enough playable dragons to make it work. Ancestral Recall was a sweet reprint for Standard, and we apparently get a Counterspell too.
