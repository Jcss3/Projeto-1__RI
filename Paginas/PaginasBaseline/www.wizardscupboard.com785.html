Modern Musings: Attacking the Modern Classic with Burn!

By Justin Alderman

Last weekend I set people on fire in the SCG Modern Classic, blazing my way into 13th place. Like most people, I had arrived in Orlando to play in the Standard Open with high hopes of winning it all, but I encountered an unexpected Akoum Firebird in Round 5 to ensure that I would not see Day 2. Fortunately for me, I had my trusty Burn deck at the ready and was stoked to play my favorite format come Sunday morning.

BURN OR ZOO?

Now a lot has changed in the Modern world since I last wrote about Burn. Birthing Pod, Amulet Bloom, and Splinter Twin have all exited the format. Zoo and Burn decks of numerous flavors all became major contenders for a hot minute, but were eventually quelled by the Eldrazi winter. With Eye of Ugin now gone, it’s safe to say that anything goes as you traverse the Modern landscape. The format is alive and diverse, which has some people asking an important question: is Burn still good enough?

Absolutely.

But the real question is this: which version of Burn is the optimal one?

Burn decks these days are still blurring the line between Zoo and the traditional haste creatures and damage spells simply because of the existence of Wild Nacatl and Atarka’s Command. As a result, many camps are divided on what exactly counts as a Burn deck, and which build is the right one to battle with, as each version will have several advantages and weaknesses.

Purists argue that fetching to fix your colors for Atarka’s Command is just too painful in the mirror matches and that Wild Nacatl may just as well be a dead draw past Turn 1. Since the deck is now playing a higher creature count than what Burn decks used to play in the past, it’s actually closer to a Zoo variant and should be treated as such. Simply pick Green or White and just stick to that (excluding sideboard splashes, which are treated as “free”).

Those in the hard Naya camp insist that the damage you deal to yourself allows you to increase your damage output more than by just sticking to two colors. Wild Nacatl and Atarka’s Command are just too powerful to pass up, no matter the cost. Heck, Kird Ape can join the party too if it means swinging for lethal on turn 3 or 4. The cost of paying so much life is outweighed by a much more explosive draw in the early game, even if it means you draw less burn spells as the game drags on. Yes, the higher creature count means that your opponent’s removal spells are always live, but that’s just the price you gotta pay.

However, as the rest of the world’s Burn mages squabble over who’s right or wrong, I took it upon myself to narrow it all down to a few key points:

Be proactive and aggressive. Most decks aren’t prepared for the early rush that Burn is capable of producing and any amount of burn spells in your deck still serve as reach when your offense has diminished.

Play the most powerful cards that you can. If this means damaging yourself to use these powerful cards, then so be it. It doesn’t matter how much life you have left when you win as long as your opponent is dead.

Burn has always been a mix of cheap, aggressive creatures and efficient damage spells. The faces may change, but the overall strategy is still the same: get on the ground fast, selectively remove key blockers or threats if needed, and then finish with direct damage spells. Even by just playing a handful of creatures means that your opponent can still interact with you if need be and as of right now, Burn isn’t strong enough (yet?) to forego repeatable sources of damage, so think of your creatures as a necessary evil if you must.

Wild Nacatl is extremely good against decks that do not want to interact with you and gives you one of the strongest Game 1 plans. However, if you’re facing decks that want to interact with you, remove Wild Nacatl. It’s that simple.

Atarka’s Command gives Burn a greater chance at achieving a Turn 3 or 4 nutdraw. The floor on this card is usually very good, but the ceiling on it goes pretty darn high. If you’re not playing this card, you need a very good reason not to be.

Know how much damage you’ll be dealing yourself in specific matchups and have a plan for that, especially in the Burn mirror.

MY TOURNAMENT

Knowing all of this, I chose to run a build utilizing my favorite Ninja Kitty and Atarka’s Command in the Modern Classic. I just wanted a strong, proactive deck. My only plan for the mirror was to run 2 Lightning Helix in the maindeck to recoup the 3-6 life loss I may take when mana-fixing. I eschewed sideboard cards like Kor Firewalker and Dragon’s Claw in favor of some good, old-fashioned know-how and more Burn spells. This allowed me to have a much more diverse plan for an unknown meta.

Besides, winning the mirror sometimes just comes down to gaining 2-3 life anyway. Sad, but true.

Fortunately for me, I didn’t face a single mirror match in the tournament. (Not that I wasn’t prepared, but hey, it is what it is)

These were my matchups:

R1 2-1 vs Black White Tokens

R2 2-1 vs Jund

R3 2-0 vs Abzan Little Kid

R4 2-1 vs Abzan Collected Company

R5 1-2 vs Abzan Midrange

R6 2-1 vs Merfolk

R7 0-2 vs Esper Zur

R8 2-0 vs Tron

As you can see, I didn’t play any of the same deck twice in 8 rounds- proof that the format is pretty healthy and diverse.

Of my two losses, the closest one was against Abzan Midrange. I managed to bring my opponent down to 7 life and just needed to survive an attack from his two Kitchen Finks so I could untap and deliver lethal damage. A well-times Sorin, Solemn Visitor would make sure I never got that chance.

The second loss was to Lance Austin’s Esper Zur, a deck that I had never encountered before. If you haven’t seen it, I highly recommend checking it out. After facing a plethora of hand disruption, I was dispatched by Zur the Enchanter, who grabbed Steel of the Godhead and put the game far out of reach for me. It’s possible that I could have won the first game, as I had a couple of opportunities to topdeck a lethal burn spell, but I failed. In Game 2, I was forced to mulligan to 5, and then Zur did to me what Zur does best.

SIDEBOARDING

Even though that second loss put me out of Top 8 contention, I was still very happy to achieve a Top 16 finish playing a deck that I love. I was very satisfied with the configuration of my 75; it was very focused and had a lot of flexibility. Because of this, I felt prepared for anything, and soon found my sideboarding to be very fluid and tight.

Part of my sideboard preparation comes from understanding how sideboarding packages in Burn work and how they overlap in different matchups. A good example of this is with the multiple Skullcracks in the sideboard. This card is most often a counterspell for life gain in the Abzan CoCo matchup, and is brought in as a package with Grafdigger’s Cage and some removal spells. However, most of the same package is also sided in for the Merfolk matchup (minus the Cages), but for different reasons. Since Merfolk likes to attack your manabase, Skullcrack is actually much easier to cast than some of the multicolored spells in the deck. It also has the bonus of turning off damage prevention in case your opponent makes the mistake of blocking with their Master of Waves. Note that you aren’t siding out the exact same cards in both matchups, but you’re simply removing the cards that are the weakest in that particular match to make room for a specific package.

The other part is knowing how to play your position when sideboarding, such as knowing to keep Eidolon of the Great Revel in while on the play and taking him out when on the draw, depending on the matchup.

And finally, the hardest part I think, is keeping yourself from going overboard and diluting your game plan. The easiest way to explain this is with Path to Exile and Deflecting Palm. Both cards are entirely dependent on what your opponent plays or what action they decide to make. Sure, they can be lifesavers or blowouts when timed right, but ultimately they weaken your plan when drawn in multiples when it becomes time to close out a game. It takes a very specific metagame to justify running multiple copies of these cards and when you do, it generally makes your deck more reactive, not proactive. Keep your eye on the ball, so to speak, and remember that you still need to win with damage on mostly your terms – not the opponent’s.

Now I typically don’t like to give out specific sideboarding guides, but I do want to give a loose outline on what to do for some of the most common matchups you’ll face when playing Burn. After all, the worst plan you can have is no plan at all.

Abzan Coco Package:

3 Skullcrack
2 Grafdigger’s Cage
1 Path to Exile
1 Grim Lavamancer
2 Searing Blaze

This deck is very good at gumming up the ground and presents some very formidable blockers. As such, you want to take out your Wild Nacatls and Eidolons and 1 other burn spell. I prefer Rift Bolt since it’s sometimes embarrassed by a Spellskite. Boros Charm is actually very in this match since it protects your Grafdigger’s Cage, so don’t blow them early if you can help it. Speaking of Cage, it is definitely the strongest card in your deck postboard here.

Merfolk Package:

3 Skullcrack
1 Grim Lavamancer
2 Searing Blaze

Optional:
3 Destructive Revelry

I really like to cut white out altogether in this matchup because Spreading Seas can be pretty devastating if you’re land-light and trying to hit all 3 colors. Try to protect your Green sources by defensively fetching in response to a Spreading Seas if you opt to run Revelry (I usually side out Eidolons here). Applying early pressure with creatures is also critical here.

Burn Package:

3 Skullcrack
1 Deflecting Palm
1 Path to Exile
1 Grim Lavamancer
2 Searing Blaze

Remove your Green cards (Nacatl and Command) and focus on playing defensively. Counter life gain with Skullcrack (I cannot stress how important this is) and be selective when playing Eidolon of the Great Revel so as not to get locked out.

Jund and Abzan Midrange Package:

3 Skullcrack
1 Deflecting Palm
1 Path to Exile
1 Grim Lavamancer
2 Searing Blaze

Remove Wild Nacatl and your Eidolons since the opponent can easily brickwall or interact with them. You want to focus on a heavy Burn strategy and counter any and all life gain. Sandbag haste creatures late in the game if need be so you can get in a few chips of damage in after your opponent attacks. Grim Lavamancer is still okay if he sticks since he can also shrink Tarmogoyfs or deny Scavenging Ooze with his ability. Don’t forget that Boros Charm can also be used to take out Liliana in a pinch!

Affinity Package:
3 Destructive Revelry
2 Ancient Grudge
1 Grim Lavamancer
2 Searing Blaze

Remove Eidolon of the Great Revel and Boros Charm. Apply pressure with your creatures while also controlling your opponent’s field. Don’t forget that Atarka’s Command provides reach to your creatures if you’re forced to block a flying alpha strike.

Tron Package:

3 Destructive Revelry
3 Skullcrack
1 Path to Exile

(Optional)
1 Deflecting Palm
2 Ancient Grudge

You’re pretty favored in this matchup. Searing Blaze, Grim Lavamancer, and Eidolon (on the draw) are your weakest cards here. Apply aggressive pressure with your creatures and burn them out as quickly as possible. Atarka’s Command can save your team from a sweeper or allow a Nacatl to punch into a Spellskite. I’m normally not a fan of Ancient Grudge since it doesn’t deal damage, but it is an option.

Lantern Control Package:

3 Destructive Revelry
2 Ancient Grudge
3 Skullcrack

Searing Blaze is a dead card, so that can easily go. Ensnaring Bridge can be a big problem for Burn, so your non-haste creatures can be a liability. I usually sideboard out Nacatl and Grim Lavamancer (since they usually name him with Pithing Needle) and try to go for a burn oriented plan.

Infect Package:

3 Destructive Revelry
1 Deflecting Palm
1 Path to Exile
1 Grim Lavamancer
2 Searing Blaze

When played correctly, this can actually be a very good matchup. Just control the board and save your Revelries for Wild Defiance and Inkmoth Nexus. Boros Charm and Lava Spike are your usual cards to sideboard out since they can’t target creatures.

Suicide Zoo:

1 Deflecting Palm
1 Path to Exile
2 Searing Blaze

This deck has the ability to win out of nowhere, especially from being at a low life total. Eidolon of the Great Revel, Deflecting Palm, and Path to Exile are your biggest weapons here. It may be tempting to bring in Destructive Revelry to fight Phyrexian Unlife, but it’s usually not worth it since those cards could potentially be dead in hand while they combo out on you. Since this deck usually goes crazy and burns its life total as it attempts to go for the win, it is usually ideal to sandbag some instant speed burn until just the right time.

Jeskai Package:

2 Grafdigger’s Cage

This is generally one of your harder matchups since they’re packing countermagic in addition to having a lot of the same weapons you do: Lightning Bolt, Lightning Helix, and Path to Exile. Since there’s so many flavors of Jeskai (Nahiri, Kiki-Jiki, pure Control, Flash, etc), it’s very hard to pinpoint an exact plan. Sometimes you encounter Leyline of Sanctity and can’t win if you side out Boros Charms, Atarka’s Command, or creatures, and sometimes you just die so multiple instances of Bolt-Snap-Bolt into Colonnade. I definitely like Grafdigger’s Cage no matter what since it at least stop Snapcaster shenanigans (and Jeskai mages do love a good shenanigan). Skullcrack is also good is you don’t think you’ll see a Leyline of Sanctity.

Like I said, this is just a lot of general guidelines to follow; nothing is set in stone. With Modern being so wide open as it is, you could play in any tournament and never encounter any of these decks. As long as you remain proactive and come out swinging, Burn will always stand a chance.

Onward…

That’s it for today. I was certainly thrilled to bring Burn to a Top 16 finish at a large tournament. Hopefully next time I’ll win the whole thing. In the meantime, keep slinging some bolts and lighting them up!

Stay aggressive, my friends.
