Amonkhet Rares and Mythics

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath
 

Not much special to cover today, let’s get right into the review.

This card doesn’t mess around. A 3/4 flier for 5 mana that handles the biggest planeswalker, creature, vehicle, etc that your opponent controls should put you firmly in control of the game. There are several cards that Angel of Sanctions is weak to (Archangel Avacyn, Heart of Kiran, Glorybringer, Chandra, Torch of Defiance, Grasp of Darkness), but you can just exile any of those threats that came down before Angel of Sanctions. The Embalm ability adds insurance against removal and makes an opponent think twice about killing it. Just remember the last time you had to point a Shock or Fatal Push at Scrapheep Scrounger. Angel of Sanctions is great.

Casual all star and future $5 rare. Currently gargabe.

Enough people have talked about this card that I don’t want to immediately dismiss it. Aetherworks Marvel is good at casting Approach of the Second Sun the first time, and it does the requisite amount of digging to find it again. Unsubstantiate and Failure//Comply also bounce the first copy so you don’t need to wait or dig for it. This still seems more cute than good, but I could be convinced.

Archfiend of Ifnir will probably slot into a black cycling deck if one exists. It doesn’t look powerful enough to force a deck into black, but it can serve as a roleplayer if a deck already exists. The triggered ability is fine but not spectacular, and only having four toughness is a liability. Shadow of the Grave is probably the most likely card to make Archfiend of Ifnir playable.

If As Foretold makes it in Standard, some bannings are going to be necessary. Taking off the third turn of the game for some value starting around turn 5-6 is a great way to get run over by Heart of Kiran or endless Felidar Guardians. Some control decks might be in the market for it, but only as a sideboard option.
I’m incredibly excited to play with As Foretold in Modern. I picked up a set of Restore Balance to go with Ancestral Visions, and I’m hoping to brew something up soon.

A Modern roleplayer in Hatebears and the Creature Toolbox decks, Aven Mindcensor doesn’t look to make that big of an impact in Standard. The single toughness makes it fairly weak to Walking Ballista. More importantly, there aren’t that many library searching effects in Standard right now. Attune with Aether is the most common, and Aven Mindcensor is too slow to stop it (and it doesn’t completely stop it). Traverse the Ulvenwald is the best hit for Aven Mindcensor, but it has taken a dip in popularity after the banning of Emrakul, the Promised End.


I’m pretty sure this card is just awful. The sacrifice ability is expensive for a fairly minimal bonus, and Standard is lacking in both good sacrifice outlets and good sacrifice fodder. There are some serviceable cards for both, but they seem like far too much effort to turn on a 4/6 Menace creature.

Thematically, this card is great. The comparison to the Nile River flooding to increase trade and receding to produce farmland is captured well by this card. Even if it took a lot of words to get there. Unfortuantley, Bounty of the Luxa probably isn’t good enough to stand on its own. Like As Foretold, taking off a turn for a future effect is dubious in the current Standard format. The new Nissa is a strong pull to green-blue, so there might be a home for Bounty of the Luxa in a sideboard somewhere.

These lands are great, just buy a set if you are ever going to play this color combination. The cycle lands are, on the surface, just a little worse than the scry lands in Theros. However, Cycling is a supported theme in Amonkhet, giving increased utility to these lands.
They also have the basic land types, so they can be searched out with fetch lands. Modern Life from the Loam decks might be interested. I’ve got some Seismic Assaults ready, hoping these are good enough.

I’ve got no idea what deck wants this. I suppose an Indestructible land is nice if Darksteel Citadel wasn’t good enough already. Any deck that wants multiple colors probably can’t wait until turn 6 to start generating colored mana with this card. Pretty sure this is a bulk rare.

Aetherworks Marvel has a plan B now. This makes it a little less dubious to just jam four Ulamog, the Ceaseless Hunger into the deck, since drawing them isn’t a complete blowout now. I could see this not being good enough, since it is small enough that it probably won’t get to attack more than once.

For any deck not utilizing Energy, Channeler Initiate is going to usurp Servant of the Conduit’s position. It simply has a larger body and does more as a topdeck in the late game. If you expect to play green ramp decks that don’t involve Aetherworks Marvel, this is a good pickup.

The single toughness of Combat Celebrant has me extremely nervous. Walking Ballista and Liliana, the Last Hope just laugh at this card. It is certainly a combo with Always Watching, but I still expect this to trade off in combat the first time it attacks. I expect the format is simply too hostile for this kind of card to have a chance.
As far as Modern is concerned, Combat Celebrant is yet another combo with Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker. It might see a small amount of play for that reason alone.

Commit is a little mana intensive, but it is a solid bounce effect for a control deck. Memory doesn’t play particularly well in those kind of control decks, but any of the Aftermath abilities might make it into a deck that simply wants value from cards discarded. Overall, I can see this as a singleton in blue decks looking for an answer to early planeswalkers. Not sure what deck wants the Memory side of the card.

For that one guy you really hate when you play Commander.

If a Cycling deck shows up in Standard, I expect Curator of Mysteries to have a home in it. It is a solid finisher with an extremely cheap Cycling cost. Any of the Drake Haven decks should be looking at this card.
I don’t think Modern Living End wants to move into blue, but this is a good addition there if the change happens. As Foretold makes that more likely.

Heart of Kiran laughs at this card. Otherwise, the removal is fine but disappointing at rare. The Ribbons side is a nice bonus for decks that can cast it, but it wills see play based on the need for the Cut side of the card. The exception being a deck that mills a lot of cards and makes a ton of mana. That might be a deck in Modern or Legacy, but I highly doubt it in Standard.

This is a good sideboard option for black decks against Aetherworks Marvel. If a deck isn’t 100% dedicated to getting a specific artifact in play, this is not a good sideboard plan. Heart of Kiran is the prime example of a terrible target for Dispossess. It comes down early enough that copies in the hand will dodge Dispossess. Future copies are mediocre at best. Most importantly, the player casting Dispossess will be down a card for an opponent who only lost some potential draws in the future. Aetherworks Marvel and possibly something like Paradox Engine or Aetherflux Reservoir are the only reasonable targets in Standard right now.

Amonkhet’s version of Astral Slide or Lightning Rift. Drake Haven is a great finisher for a dedicated Cycling deck. Once again, taking off the third turn for no value is rough, but making a Drake token or two for the next several turns should mitigate that lost tempo. I can easily imagine a deck similar to Dynavolt Tower that uses Drake Haven as a payoff card.

Favorite card in the set, and it’s not close. I’ve got a long history of happily playing with small black creatures. Vampire Lacerator is one of my favorite cards (I’m weird like that). It’s like a reverse Craig Wescoe thing. Oh, you wanted to know if the card was good? Mardu Vehicles is going to beat Zombies as the best aggressive deck without a doubt. Dread Wanderer is only going to see play if that deck stays W/B/r and wants a compliment to Toolcraft Exemplar.

This misses just enough cards in Mardu Vehicles and 4 Color Saheeli to be pretty awful right now. If the format shakes up enough for Verdurous Gearhulk to make a return, Dusk // Dawn should have a strong home in sideboards. Dawn might also be good enough for some kind of Aristocrats deck, offering white a fairly strong draw spell.

Plummet is still legal, so Heaven is only going to be a better option if Whirler Virtuoso’s hoard of Thopter tokens is dominating the format. A Temur deck (Dynavolt Tower, or otherwise) that is interested in going long could run a copy just for the versatility. It’s a little expensive to see mass adoption in the format though.

Reflector Mage is back, just without the body. Unsubstantiate hasn’t seen any play, which is bad news for Failure. Comply isn’t exactly a great effect either, as Silence never saw extensive use outside of specific combo decks. Overall, Failure // Comply looks like a pretty big failure. Approach of the Second Sun gives it a sliver of hope, but I’m not banking on that.

See Canyon Slough. Pick up your copies now. This is one of the best color combinations for synergy with Cycling lands, so they should be particularly valuable.

The era of Gideons is upon us. Players everywhere are overvaluing the Emblem ability on Gideon and gleefully looking to slot copies into their aggressive decks. In the land of reality, you really need a good reason to play this over Gideon, Ally of Zendikar for the next six months. After that, the price of the card should drop to something more reasonable, and the card is good enough to stand on its own. It’s just not the same game ending card against durdly decks that the four mana Gideon is.
The plus ability on Gideon of the Trials is a defensive ability. It goes up to prevent damage from any single permanent, including creatures, planeswalkers, enchantments, and vehicles. Not particularly strong against swarm decks, but it is good against some of the large monsters in G/B delirium, Gideon, Ally of Zendikar, Heart of Kiran, and Electrostatic Pummeler.
The first zero ability makes Gideon into his typical indestructible creature. You lose a little size for the reduced mana cost, but it is still a respectable attacker at three mana. The biggest loss is the fifth point of toughness against Grasp of Darkness.
The Emblem ability is better than flavor text, but likely worse than the plus ability in most games. If an opponent is on the offensive, you likely don’t have the resources to protect Gideon. If you can protect Gideon, you likely don’t need the Emblem in the first place.
Overall, I expect Gideon to make his way into white decks after Gideon, Ally of Zendikar rotates. Until then, I only expect to see him in small numbers.
Eternal formats are a different story, where I expect the Emblem to be a lot closer to a three mana Platinum Angel. I’m looking forward to exploring Gideon against decks like Ad Nauseam, which can’t beat a Gideon Emblem in their current form. There is plenty of room for innovation there. Just be sure to watch out for Path to Exile as an answer to Gideon’s creature ability.

Runed Halo and Nevermore spliced together probably cost a little too much to be effective. Four mana is a hard bar to clear for Gideon’s Intervention, and either half of the card would probably be good enough if the card was cheaper. It does theoretically stop the Copycat combo, but Authority of the Council does that at a much cheaper cost already.

Final Fortune effects are cute, but they aren’t particularly powerful. The extra mana to counter a spell or mess up a combat step is cute, but I don’t know if it is a good trade in the end. I’m leaning towards this being better, but it is close. Gideon’s Emblem makes this a fun card, but I don’t expect Gideon of the Trials to overtake Gideon, Ally of Zendikar for another six months.

This card looks pretty strong. The stats are good. The ability is strong. It has synergy with both Humans and Heart of Kiran. It fits well into a deck that would already want Always Watching. The only downside is the weakness to Walking Ballista and Liliana, the Last Hope. Most decks that would want Glory-Bound Initiate already have weaknesses to those cards, so I expect that to only be a minor consideration. If the popularity of those cards is low, this card should do great work.

We have the next iteration of Stormbreath Dragon. The lack of mythic rarity has me a little skeptical that this card is as good as its predecessors, but I expect it to stack up pretty well. There are plenty of great targets for Glorybringer, like Kalitas, Traitor of Ghet, Gideon, Ally of Zendikar, Angel of Sanctions, Felidar Guardian, and more. Always Watching is a great combo, but decks that want to run Glorybringer are unlikely to use Always Watching to particularly good effect. The card should stand up well on its own though.

This seems like a sideboard card for control mirrors. The ability doesn’t prevent creature damage, so Archangel Avacyn is a nighmare for Glyph Keeper. Most of the creatures in Mardu Vehicles trade with Glyph Keeper. Control decks relying on removal to clear creatures will have a harder time removing it, but Dynavolt Tower can break the protection bubble too. Harnessed Lightning can even break the bubble and save all three energy (dealing zero damage). If Glyph Keeper makes an appearance at all, it will be minimal.

I’m pretty sure Hapatra is a nifty card that just isn’t good enough in Standard. There are only a few cards that make -1/-1 counters that are good enough to stand on their own, and a two mana 2/2 that generates a little value from those probably isn’t worth the slot. G/B mirror matches are probably the best place to try to generate deathtouch snakes, so I would look for this as a sideboard card in those mirrors.

Harsh Mentor looks like it will be a roleplayer in Standard if mono red is a deck. The stats alone are fairly unexciting, and it stacks up poorly when compared to Kari Zev, Skyship Rader. Unlike Eidolon of the Great Revel, Harsh Mentor can easily die to a removal spell before it ever triggers.
Older formats are a different story entirely, with fetch lands easily triggering Harsh Mentor enough to warrant consideration. I would be surprised if Harsh Mentor doesn’t make a splash in Modern and Legacy.

It’s easy to imagine getting absurd amounts of lands from this card, but I expect it to play out poorly. The easiest way to get a bunch of cheap creatures is with mana dorks, but the switch to two cost creatures makes that less effective. Harvest Season might play well as a backup to Cryptolith Rites, but redundancy there seems unnecessary. I expect this to be a dud.

There look to be just enough synergies for Hazoret to see a little bit of play. This god will probably show up as 2-3 copies for the top end of a very aggressive deck. Possibly a full playset, if there aren’t any other desirable cards at that mana cost, since the activated ability makes some use of additional copies drawn.

Giving creatures haste is a nice ability, but only a specific deck can make use of targeted haste with upside at the cost of loosing the creature at the end of the turn. A swarm deck might be interested as a sideboard option against a slow control deck, but I still have doubts there.

I suppose four copies of Fling might not be enough for some people. Don’t expect this card to see much play.

The cheap embalm cost screams value with Noose Constrictor. B/G Delerium decks could run Honored Hydra as a Traverse the Ulvenwald target, but I’m not sure what it does better than most other cards. The Embalm cost’s weakness to Fatal Push is unfortunate.

Damage prevention occasionally comes up as relevant, so I won’t write off Insult // Injury as a complete dud. Injury isn’t too far off on rate, though Hungry Flames is a little better for the same cost. The combination together might make Insult // Injury show up occasionally, but the odds are fairly low.

See Canyon Slough. Pick up your copies now.

It’s hard not to draw comparisons to Thassa, God of the Sea, but Kefnet the Mindful just seems pretty bad. Seven cards is a tough bar to reach, and the payoff isn’t exactly worth the effort. Cast Out is also a decent answer to the cycle of gods, so Indestructibility isn’t quite as valuable as it could be. I expect this one to warm the bench for it’s time in Standard.

I suppose this could be a sideboard card for Zombie decks, but the rate is a little high for Standard. Anthems usually need to be cheaper or stronger to make much of an impact.

Liliana, Death’s Majesty has Ob Nixilis, Reignited to contend with in Standard right now. They are both decent at generating value, so let’s cover Liliana’s abilities individually.
The plus ability to make a token and mill two cards is fairly strong. Gideon, Ally of Zendikar has already proven that a 2/2 token is a good way to defend a planeswalker, and Zombie tokens are much more likely to matter than ally tokens. The mill effect feeds Delirium, Liliana’s minus ability, any Aftermath cards, several Zombie abilities, and a bunch of corner case cards. All in all, it should be pretty comparable to the value from Ob Nixilis’s card draw.
Liliana’s minus ability can range from mediocre to insane, depending on deck construction. It might warrant a dedicated reanimator deck, but it should be fine without dedicated support. G/B delirium recurring an Ishkana, Grafwidow should be pretty game breaking against several aggressive decks.
Liliana’s ultimate is probably the least exciting part of the card. Somewhere between Damnation and Plague Wind, it is fairly easy to play around when a player is working towards it.
All in all, I expect Liliana, Death’s Majesty to be a roleplayer in any midrange black deck. I expect it to be a centerpiece of any reanimatior deck in Standard, but I expect those decks to be more fun than good.

Mouth is an awful rate for the mana cost, and Feed is going to range from decent to awful depending on the board. Green can draw cards more reliably than Mouth // Feed, so this card won’t be seeing play.

Amonkhet looks to be another Theros block, providing a handful of MInotaurs that aren’t strong enough to warrant a deck. The bonuses here are mediocre, since you can’t rely on them at all times. I don’t expect this to make an impact in Standard (or any other format).

This will compete with Ruinous Path for the next six months. If your deck needs additional threats, Ruinous Path is the better card. If your deck is weak to graveyard effects, Never // Return is the better choice. If you aren’t specifically concerned with either, Never // Return is the better card, since you can always get the value later if you need it. Either way, this effect at Sorcery speed has proven to be a little too slow in Standard over the last year or so.

All I really want is to flicker this with Felidar Guardian. Realistically, a cycling deck might be interested in New Perspectives, but it is expensive enough that it might not be worth it. Some combo deck with Shadow of the Grave is interesting, but I don’t know what the payoff would be. I’m not writing this off as a bulk rare, but I don’t really know where to start to work with it.

This is exploring some new territory for planeswalkers with the X cost allowing Nissa to scale in power. She doesn’t protect herself very well in the early game, relying on the Scry ability to build up a decent number of loyalty counters. The zero ability can defend her very well, but it will depend on how the deck is constructed. The scry ability helps set up the zero ability, but that would leave her undefended for a full turn. The ultimate is decent, and particularly powerful as you can use it immediately if you draw it late in the game. That seems like the most exciting part of the card honestly.
I really like Nissa, but I don’t really know what to do with the card or what deck to put it in.

This is one of the easier gods to turn on, as white often wants to deploy a ton of creatures. The token producing ability is decent, but a little on the expensive side for 1/1 tokens. The big problem is the competition with Gideon, Ally of Zendikar, who beats this card out 100% of the time. In six months, Oketra might see a little play, but not much more than Heliod ever saw.

This looks like a commander card with extremely fringe possibilities with Paradox Engine. I don’t expect to see this card in Standard.

A fairly strong reward for pushing a Zombie theme, Plague Belcher comes with a decent body and a strong trigger. I expect decks that utilize Dread Wanderer to play a set of these as well.


Combat tricks rarely make a dent in Constructed, and this card costs way too much mana.

The text on this card looks appealing, but it doesn’t tend to play out particularly well. Prowling Serpopard is easy to remove once it hits the battlefield, allowing for bigger threats to be countered in the future. If the format shifts dramatically, Prowling Serpopard might be a consideration in the sideboard. It should be avoided otherwise.

A decent draw spell for blue control decks. The discard part isn’t a huge drawback, with Aftermath, both Lilianas, Delirium, and Torrential Gearhulk all making use of the graveyard. The lack of synergy between Torrential Gearhulk and X spells is the biggest drawback to Pull from Tomorrow, but I still expect it to show up in small numbers of whatever control deck arises in the new Standard format.

The investment on this card is huge, but the payoff could be worth it for a deck that needs a mana rock. Pyramid of the Pantheon is a truly horrendous draw later in the game, and that is a big enough strike to keep this from seeing much play. I expect Dissenter’s Deliverance is also going to be popular enough to make this an even worse mana investment.

Rags compares poorly to Flaying Tendrils, both in mana cost and the exile clause. Riches is a little too expensive and rarely good against the same decks as Rags. This looks like a dud to me, stay away.

This card certainly puts a lot of power into play. Ishkana, Grafwidow creates a similar board state, but it requires a lot more work. Unfortuately, the Copycat combo makes Regal Caracal a difficult card to cast, when you can just die before you untap with it. The synergy with Felidar Guardian might allow a copy or two of Regal Caracal to slip in somewhere.

Rhonas looks to be the clear winner of the god cycle in Amonkhet. Turning it on looks trivial in a green deck, the ability is useful and not too overpriced, and the mana cost is cheap enough to come down early. I expect most green decks to play a few copies of Rhonas.

This card certainly does a lot of stuff. I’m not sure how well it stacks up against something like Verdurous Gearhulk, but they certainly play well together. I don’t expect this card is powerful enough to warrant a deck built around it, but a couple copies could slide into a deck if a Naya (or Gruul) shell already exists.

This is a fun win condition for a control deck, but I expect a green control deck can do better than an eight mana enchantment. This card is super cool, but it doesn’t look very good.

See Canyon Slough. Pick up your copies now.

I don’t think you ever want to play this card in a fair way. Using this as protection from discard spells is mana intensive and not particularly reliable. Using it to draw a stack of cycled cards from something like New Perspectives looks to be the best use in standard.
Using this with Fluctuator in Legacy looks super fun.

See Canyon Slough. Pick up your copies now. I really hope this card makes it in Modern with Life from the Loam and Seismic Assault

The comparison to Monastery Swiftspear is unavoidable, but Soul-Scar Mage comes up short there. Haste is a big deal, and Soul-Scar Mage has a nifty but relatively useless second ability. I expect this to be a roleplayer in Standard if red aggressive decks arise, but it isn’t on the same level as Thraben Inspector or Toolcraft Exemplar.

This will compete with Kozilek’s Return and Radiant Flames, and the best card will depend on how the format shakes out. We already have this effect in Standard, and the cycling ability doesn’t add much.

This looks like a centerpiece of an Embalm deck. Unfortunately, a dedicated Embalm deck doesn’t look to be worth the effort. The best Embalm creatures will see play anyway, and a creature that gives them bonuses probably isn’t worth the effort.

This is a pretty strong effect for a swarm effect. The Legendary card type is unfortunate, but I expect a copy or two to show up in several places. Cryptolith Rites is both the cutest and the least likely place, but red aggressive decks should consider Throne of the God-Pharaoh.
Modern swarm decks should also consider this card, at least as a singleton.

Yet another clone effect, Vizier of Many Faces certainly gives a lot of value with Embalm. It is probably still too slow for Standard.

This card is sweet. The stats alone are a little low, but it is a fantastic way to generate value in a game that goes long. Finding lands on the top of the library is a little rough, since your following turn will be pretty lackluster if Vizier doesn’t survive long enough. If a deck with a ton of creatures exists, I expect this to see some play there. If it doesn’t, Gonti, Lord of Luxury will probably see more play in the same slot.
In Modern, Vizier of the Menangerie plays well with Courser of Kruphix and Oracle of Mul Daya. Those cards clear lands off the top of the deck to make it easier to hit with Vizier.
