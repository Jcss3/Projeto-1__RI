MTG Commander Advanced Class #1: Sapling of Colfenor — Welcome to the Machine: Deckbuilding Using an Engine

by Craig Sidorowicz

To Build a deck using an engine:

Step 1: Go to Google.com and search for your Commander’s name…

Step 2: Find a deck list, copy it, gather the cards, sleeve it, throw it in a fancy Mana-Flip-Box…

Step 3: Find three people to compliment you on your deck-building skills after you CRUSH THEM!!!

-fin-

…Well, that was a short article…

Really though, the concept I want to share with you in this installment goes a bit beyond the basic tenets of Commander I aim to include in my “101” series.  (You can find the first article in that series here).  For that reason, I’ve decided to start a parallel series called: “Advanced Class,” where I can get more technical and delve into the intermediate and advanced concepts of Commander deck building.

So what is this “Engine” I speak of?  Certainly it’s not just a search engine like Google.  It’s also not going to be found on a database forum like mtgsalvation.com, DeckedHD (The app I used to produce the deck lists found in this article), or (my new favorite) EDHrec.com.  It’s a method of deck construction using cards that employ a unique synergy with one another to create an advantage.  That advantage might be a favorable hand, board position, or even a win condition.   It can also create unfavorable situations of a similar nature for your opponents.  So the goal of today’s class will be to demonstrate how to build a Sapling of Colfenor Commander deck using the Aluren/Recycle engine.  Then we will explore three different tribal deck lists that incorporate the engine to show it’s versatility and interchangeability.

So, my fellow engineers: “Full speed ahead!”

The Sapling of Colfenor Aluren/Recycle Engine. 

These six cards represent the core of the engine: [Aluren, Null Profusion, Phyrexian Reclamation, Recycle, Survival of the Fittest, and our Commander, Sapling of Colefenor.] Note that it’s actually five, because Recycle and Null Profusion are functionally the same.

How it Works

The goal is to build and profit from the assumption that Sapling of Colfenor will likely draw a creature card when it attacks.  Unless our opponent prevents us from attacking, the trigger will always happen regardless of the outcome of the combat.  Since Sapling is indestructible, he should usually survive to be reused again.  (provided we don’t attack into wither, and that Condemn is not on your opponent’s “must play” list.)

Once we draw our creature card, we can use it in several ways, depending on which engine parts are currently in-play.

  • Aluren allows us to play any creature with Converted Mana Cost 3 or less for free.
  • Null Profusion and Recycle allow us to draw additional cards when we play that creature, with a relatively negligible drawback considering the other cards we will have access to and include in the final product.
  • If both Aluren and Recycle/Null Profusion are in play we repeat the process until we fail to draw a <3 CMC Creature card, or a land if we have a land drop (or drops) available.  Yes, Recycle and Null Profusion trigger when you “play” any card, including a land.
  • Survival of the fittest and Phyrexian Reclamation allow us to spend our life and mana to move creature cards fluidly through our hand, deck, and graveyard in order to set up the situation that we desire– an army of creatures.

Of course, this “perfect world” scenario usually doesn’t exist.  Savvy opponents will do everything they can to disrupt us, so we will need to build around this engine in order to amplify, insulate, and add consistency to the finished product.  Below, I’ve expanded the core of the deck to include these 23 cards: (Click to enlarge)

I categorize the additional cards as follows: (Some may fit in multiple categories)

  1. Tutors that effectively add additional copies of the engine cards to our hand: [Demonic Tutor, Vampiric Tutor].  As a self-imposed rule, I *ONLY* tutor for the engine cards.  This is what makes the deck interesting for me, as opposed to just playing another “toolbox” or pile of “good stuff.”
  2. Cards that manipulate the top card of our library: [Sensei’s Divining Top, *Sylvan Library, Viscera Seer, Oracle of Mul Daya,] Not Pictured but under consideration for certain builds: [Scroll Rack, Courser of Kruphix, Volrath’s Stronghold, Unholy Grotto]
  3. Permanents that draw card outside of the draw step: [Phyrexian Arena, Underworld Connections, Erebos, God of the Dead, Primordial Sage] Not Pictured but under consideration: **Necropotence.
  4. “Nitro Boosts:” Cards that I have decided to include in the deck to increase it’s ability to accelerate, come back from a losing board position, or end the game in a timely fashion: [***Genesis Wave, ***Living Death, Blood Artist, Jarad, Golgari Lich Lord].
  5. Crash Test Dummy: [Solemn Simulacrum] is now an auto include in every one of my Commander decks that use creatures.
  6. Other Possible Enhancements: (Sanguine Bond, Exquisite Blood)  These are worth mentioning because Blood works so well with Sapling, and when paired with Exquisite Blood, we have an infinite combo.  I currently don’t run this, because Wizards Cupboard players tend to frown upon infinite combos, thus they don’t really tailor their decks toward beating them). If I had to pick one to include, I’d pick Sanguine Bond because I can control when the effect triggers more easily.  These cards tend to make Commander players panic (and gang up on you), because of their power level and the fact that your commander says “gain life.”

*(WARNING: Sylvan Library “turns off” when Profusion/Recycle are in play, but the outstanding draw effect, especially in the early game, can really work toward our advantage.  Therefore it earns and keeps a spot based on its sheer power.  Pay that ‘tenth’ of your life!  Browse liberally!

**(WARNING: Necropotence it is an extremely broken, often “crutch,” card that places a huge target on your forehead!)

***(WARNING: These cards are also extremely broken, and may contribute to boredom when played excessively, or on turn four!)

Deck List #1: The Little Green Men Ride Again: Sapling Elves

One of the oldest tribes in magic is also arguably the strongest: The Elves.  Our first passengers on this engine are the best choice if we want to be as competitive as possible (I will also include two other deck lists that are much less competitive, but are still extremely fun to play with!)  Golgari Elves is most often represented by Nath of the Gilt-Leaf, but he -is- and requires an engine all his own: Mass discard and hand manipulation effects.  These effects are not absent from Sapling decks (I love to include Sadistic Hypnotist at every opportunity), but these are not the angles I want to use to control the board.  I’d just rather pressure my opponents life totals than pressure their hands when using a creature-based engine.  So on to the first deck list: (Click to Enlarge)

 The Breakdown:  This list is ridiculously creature heavy, with 50 of my 64 slots dedicated to cards that can swing.  There are numerous triggers to be aware of (Wirewood Hivemaster), actions you can take on other player’s turns: (Wirewood Symbiote, Quirion Ranger), and ways to interact with most enemy permanents: (Nullmage Shepherd, Viridian Zealot).  You may find yourself missing them at first, but after many games played with this deck (or any Commander deck) the opportunities and correct lines of play will be easier to recognize and remember.

Mana Producers/Ramp Enablers: [Arbor Elf, Birchlore Rangers, Deathrite Shaman, Elves of Deep Shadow, Elvish Mystic, Elvish Pioneer, Fyndhorn Elves, Joraga Treespeaker, Priest of Titania, Courser of Kruphix, Elvish Archdruid, Farhaven Elf, Wood elves, Oracle of Mul Daya, Solemn Simulacrum, Wirewood Channeler.]  

All of these help us get Sapling and our other engine cards online quicker.  Oracle and Courser are very important, as they manipulate the top of our library by shaving off the lands, and further increasing the chances to draw creatures.  The ramp creatures make great soldiers once the Elf Lords arrive to command them, and they can later be used as fodder for other pieces of the deck, such as Sadistic Hypnotist or Jarad.

Lords/Creature Enhancers: [Joraga Warcaller, Elvish Archdruid, Elvish Champion, Imperious Perfect] ([Adaptive Automaton] is also a viable alternative) [Ezuri Renegade Leader, Immaculate magistrate.]

Evasion: [Eladamri Lord of Leaves, Champion of Lambholdt]

Removal: [Deathrite Shaman (Graveyard), Campire hexmage (Planeswalkers), Viridian Zealot, Nullmage Shepherd (Artifact/Enchantment), Glissa Sunseeker (Mana Rocks), Acidic Slime (Art/Enc/Land), Sadistic Hypnotist (Hand)]

Protection: [Caller of the Claw (Board Wipe Insurance) Eladamri Lord of Leaves (Shroud), Ezuri Renegade Leader (Regeneration), Tajuru Preserver (Anti-Annihilator)]

Other Utility/Specialty: [Quirion Ranger (Untap) Wirewood Symbiote: (Enter the Battlefield Ability Recursion/Untap)  Blood Artist, Jarad, Golgari Lich Lord (Grind at opponent’s life totals) Yeva, Nature’s Herald (Flash when we don’t have Aluren), Erebos and Disciple of Bolas: (Draw), Fauna Shaman and Wirewood Herald (Tutor)].

Curve and Mana Base:  This deck features an aggressive curve, with nearly thirty cards at one-and-two Converted Mana Cost.  We’re a bit heavy on three CMC, which happens often with Tribal decks that have a lot of 3-cost lords.

From this chart, we can easily determine how many of each basic land to use by looking at the mana symbol statistics (Also a great feature of Decked HD).  We have a rough ratio of 2:1 Green:Black symbols, so when adding Basic lands, we will adhere to that ratio.You can also factor in specialty lands like Urborg and Bojuka Bog, but be wary of doing this when Blood Moon and Back to Basics are present in this format.  I always count dual lands as “neutral” when tailoring the mana base, since they produce both colors. (EDIT:  Cut a Forest, and put in a Command Tower…Oops!)  Also, as a hard and fast rule, I never use less than 36 lands in any of the Commander decks I build.  It is so tempting, and yet so dangerous to assume that you can count on your sol rings, mana rocks, and Llanowar Elves to be there when you need them.  Board wipes happen often in ‘these parts,’ and if you overplay into Austere Command and have no backup plan, you just died.

I have been, and have met many a poor soul who wasted an hour of their time being mana-screwed, sighing mournfully, watching others play 3-player Commander.  Furthermore, a deck that is looking to ramp using cards like Exploration, Horn of Greed, Oracle of Mul Daya and Courser of Kruphix needs to have enough lands available (sometimes as much as 50%) in the deck to consistently draw and play their first, and second land drops each turn.  There are numerous articles that predate Commander to back up this assertion, going as far back as 15 years to the Zvi Mowshowicz “Turboland” days.  For reference: “Zendikar Era TurboLand,” and “Eat your Vegetables”

 The Day the Woods Came Alive: Sapling Zombies (Click to enlarge)

 The Breakdown: The “Haunted Forest” motif that the Golgari cards seem to embody has always appealed to me.  In this deck, the Elf ramp cards are substituted for Defender-based ramp, and the rest of the creatures become zombies.  One very appealing change is that this deck is much more defensive.  The Defender creatures are “bottom heavy,” meaning that they generally have more toughness than power stats, so they will gain life from Sapling attack triggers, and absorb more damage as defenders.  This is a deck that could sit back and wait for the opponents to kill each other, then pounce while they are distracted (Picture the flesh-hungry zombies popping out of the hedges to surprise their already injured victims!

While there are no changes to the engine here, I have made every attempt to find parallel-themed cards between tribes.  For example, Withered Wretch comes in as graveyard hate instead of Deathrite Shaman.  Scavenging Ooze could also fit in this spot, but I decided to stick with the theme and synergy rather than a card that is likely “better” in a vacuum.

Mana Producers/Ramp Enablers:  [Overgrown Battlement, Sylvan Caryatid, Vine Trellis, Wall of Roots, Courser of Kruphix, Oracle of Mul Daya, Sol Ring, Cultivate, Kodama’s Reach].

There are only half as many ways to ways to accelerate when compared to the Elf list.  This is OK, because we want to play defense in the early game and stay off of the radar.  Then we can come out harder using the engine cards, or after the board has been cleared once or twice.

Lords/Creature Enhancers:

+1+1: [Adaptive Automaton, Cemetery Reaper, Death baron, Lord of the Undead, Zombie Master, Undead Warchief, Mikaeus the Unhallowed.]  Overall I’m quite pleased with the Zombie lords, and I think they are a bit more rewarding than the elf lords at times.  Mikaeus, especially, can be abused with a number of cards in the deck: most notably Puppeteer Clique, and Sadistic Hypnotist.

Evasion: [Zombie Master, Zombie Trailblazer, Champion of Lambholdt]

Removal: [Ulvenwald Tracker (Creatures) Vampire Hexmage (Planeswalkers), Withered Wretch (Graveyard), Coffin Queen (Graveyard), Fleshbag Marauder (Sacrifice-based Creature Removal),]

Protection: [Zombie Master, Mikaeus the Unhallowed]

Other Utility/Specialty: [Glissa the Traitor, Grey Merchant of Asphodel, Puppeteer Clique, Balthor the Defiled, Disciple of Bolas, Erebos God of the Dead.]

Curve and Mana Base:  This version of the deck has a much more balanced curve, which is one of my favorite things about it.  Mid range decks like this tend to peak in the mid to late game turns, which is where we often find ourselves when playing commander.  Once we hit turns 5-6 we can play either: 2x 1-2CMC drops, or 1x 5CMC+ drop.  Note that the mana symbol ratio is again 2:1, but this time it is in favor of the ‘skulls.’  Therefore we adjust our basic land count accordingly.

The Infested Machine:  Sapling Relentless Rats (Click to enlarge)

 

This is a deck I would play:

  • after a long day,
  • not looking for a mental exercise,
  • not wanting to make a lot of decisions or focus hard on triggers,
  • if I were looking for a challenge.

Rats are fully capable of winning, but the deck is far more narrow in utility than the Elf list, and far less defensive than the Zombie list.  There are some fancy tricks though, including Thrumming Stone (An engine in itself), Ink Eyes shenanigans, and Marrow Gnawer Evasion.  Jarad is also at his best in a deck with giant rats.  I won’t go much deeper into the synergies here.  This is, at best, a rolling ball of carnage, or a small rat problem that is easily squashed by well-placed board wipes.  In my experience though, everyone is always excited to see a Relentless Rat deck, and are always curious if the deck’s pilot will be able to pull off something amazing.

Curve and Mana Base:  Note that the mana curve for this deck looks, incidentally, like a hand offering up the ‘middle finger.’  I will leave it at that!

 

Enjoy the Ride

Hopefully the take-away from reading this article is an understanding of how this engine works, and possibly a few new deck ideas.  If I were to expand on this engine, I would consider adding a third color.  White is a great option, especially if we put Karador, Ghost Chieftain at the helm.  The enchantment tutors are available in white, and those would help to put the engine together even faster. This engine can also use the graveyard as a resource to recur creatures discarded with Recycle/Null Profusion very conveniently. Blue is also viable, especially with Commanders Damia Sage of Stone, The Mimeoplasm, or Sidisi, Blood Tyrant (Zombies).  I would gear this color combination toward Dredge, include much larger creatures (Lord of Extinction and the dreaded Consuming Aberration).  Blue’s access to counter magic is also very useful at protecting the engine, as well as your graveyard (Stifle and Trickbind those Relics of Progenitus!) Sapling could still be included in any of these tri-color combinations, but these decks would likely be less focused on his attack effect and more tailored to the strengths of the three color generals.

For homework: keep thinking, building, interacting, and having fun!

Class Dismissed!

-Craig
