EDH- Radha’s Primal Surge: One Card, 15,000 Damage.

 

 

 

 

 

 

What are the two scariest words in EDH/Commander?

“Braids, Go?”

“Craterhoof Behemoth?”

“Kiki-Jiki?”

While those are likely to trigger “red alerts” and send chills through even the most seasoned MTGCommander Veterans, I offer that the the most fearsome, loathsome, and controversial words one can say are:

“I’m going Infinite.”

Some Commander games can go especially long, and could use a dose of “Kiki-Jiki + Pestermite/Deceiver Exarch/Zealous Conscripts.  That said, many players feel let down when the game suddenly ends with the “cool guy” the leather jacket and indoor-sunglasses tutoring up his combo piece, windmill-slamming it onto the battlefield, and heading outside to smoke a Lucky Strike while the rest of the pod scoops up and reshuffles.

While some may defend infinite combos with the statement: “If I pay ten mana, I deserve to win the game,” much of the controversy surrounding infinite combos comes from the prevalence of tutors in this format, which allow quick and consistent access to all forms of degeneracy.  Some players I’ve talked to believe the tutors are the problem, but “banning all tutors” not practical because there are tutors in all colors, that can tutor for every card type.  While not as powerful as Demonic Tutor and Vampiric Tutor, cards like Crop Rotation and Fierce Empath are technically tutors.  Try banning Kodama’s Reach in your playgroup and see how many confused looks you get!

The simple solution?  House-rule ban infinite combo.  Two of the semi-competitive playgroups in my area, (including Wizard’s Cupboard) ban infinite combo in tournament play, and it seems to work well.

By most definitions, infinite combo is:

A) Infinite mana: A combination of cards that produce infinite colored or colorless mana, often involving cards such as Ashnod’s Altar or Phyrexian Altar and recurring sacrifice engines like Nim Deathmantle + Siege-Gang-Commander

B) Infinite damage: A combination of cards that produces infinite creatures or sacrifices such as Mikaeus the Unhallowed + Murderous Redcap, or the Kiki-Jiki combos from the beginning of this article.

C) Infinite draw/mill: add Altar of Dementia to the above sac engines, or use your infinite mana with Future Sight + Sensei’s Divining Top.

There are some “one card combos” that are really powerful, game-ending cards that are not technically infinite (and thus allowed in tournament play) such as Omniscience, Craterhoof Behemoth, and Insurrection.  These are also not traditionally banned.  The focus of today’s deck is built around one such card: Primal Surge.

So did you notice?  Primal Surge is the only non-permanent in the deck.  This means that when I cast it, I basically get to flip my deck onto the table and say “I win.”  Many of you may be thinking this is pretty “cheesy” or “unfair,” and are subliminally offering me a “Light for my Lucky,” but I offer that this is actually a challenging, high-synergy, multi-theme list capable of winning without playing the one-card combo.  Know that if you do cast the Surge, this list takes full advantage of it.

Now comes the point in the article where I summarize the destruction you will thrust down upon your enemies when you cast Primal Surge. (Get your calculators!)

How Primal Surge Works:

In addition to reading the card’s text, you should know the following official rulings:

5/1/2012: A permanent card is an artifact, creature, enchantment, land, or planeswalker card.

5/1/2012: Repeating the process includes the instruction to repeat the process. That is, you’ll keep exiling cards and putting them onto the battlefield until you fail to put a permanent card onto the battlefield.

5/1/2012: If you exile a permanent card that you can’t put onto the battlefield (such as an Aura with nothing legal to enchant) or one you don’t want to put onto the battlefield, Primal Surge finishes resolving.

5/1/2012: If putting any of those permanent cards onto the battlefield causes abilities to trigger, those abilities will wait to go on the stack until Primal Surge has finished resolving. Starting with the active player, each player puts his or her abilities on the stack in any order.

I chose to bold the last ruling because I feel this is the most important to know.  You do not resolve the Enter the battlefield abilities until after all the permanents are on the table.  Assuming the following cards are not stuck in your hand, this means you get the following board state:

~70 creatures + Craterhoof Behemoth.  (There are 32 creatures in the deck, and 40 lands, so at maximum you could have ~70 creatures each receiving +70/+70 =4900+ base creature power power ready to attack (with trample) via any of the haste enablers in the list.

Each creature deals damage to any target equal to its power via Pandemonium. That damage should be placed on the stack so that you may calculate it after you resolve the ability of Craterhoof Behemoth. This equals ~4900 dmg. This ruling explains why:

10/4/2004: The triggered ability (of Pandemonium) does not check the creature’s power until the ability resolves. If the creature is not on the battlefield when the ability resolves, it does damage equal to the power of the creature right before it left the battlefield. This means that if a creature enters the battlefield which can destroy itself as a “enters the battlefield” ability, the order of resolution of the triggered abilities does not matter.

If Stalking Vengeance + a sacrifice outlet is in play you may sacrifice the creatures to deal an additional ~4900 damage.

Other incidental damage that will kill people:

Purphoros God of the Forge + creatures entering the battlefield.  Often this can provide lethal damage without any help from Primal Surge.

Valakut the Molten Pinnacle + Prismatic Omen.  While neither of these cards work without Primal Surge, it is one of my favorite interactions from Scapeshift combo decks.  Basically, if you cast Primal Surge and then  five or more lands + Valakut enter the battlefield, Prismatic Omen allows all 6+ lands to be “seen” as Mountains by Valakut and ALL 6+ will trigger for 3 damage.  You may get judge calls on this one, but you can trust that it works.  Since there will probably be 6-10 lands in play when you cast surge, you will have about 30 left in your deck to deal 90+ damage.

The Grand Total: ~15,000 damage.

The Rest of the Deck:

Building a Green/Red Commander deck composed entirely of permanents can be challenging.  A deck like this demands a high quantity of resources, so it needs to “ramp” without using cards like Kodama’s Reach, Cultivate, or Skyshroud Claim.  It needs ways to interact with your opponent’s permanents without Beast Within, Chaos Warp, or Starstorm.  It also needs ways to “dig” through the deck to provide card selection and options, which have always been an issue for these colors.  We have defined the problems.  Lets see how the deck deals with these problems.

Ramp:

I chose to use “extra land per turn” ramp for this deck in the form of:

Exploration, Burgeoning, Azusa Lost but Seeking, Oracle of Mul Daya, Solemn Simulacrum, Courser of Kruphix, Terrain Generator.

as well as the three best creature land-tutors in the format. (RIP Sylvan Primordial and Primeval Titan.)

Wood Elves, Sakura-Tribe Elder, Farhaven Elf.

Xenagos the Reveler: a very unique form of ramp, and a very useful one at that!

And finally, Radha Heir to Keld as my Commander, for a little more aggressive ramp.   She is the only “Mana Elf” in the deck by choice, because There are several other elf decks in my metagame, and they are more fragile than the “terra firma” that the extra land enablers provide.

A note about my lands and land count: I wanted the best lands I could get without utilizing too many “enters the battlefield tapped” lands.  When I play an extra land, I want to be able to use it immediately, when possible.  I play 40 lands, because a deck that ramps using extra lands per turn requires one to have lands in hand, as opposed to waiting in the deck.   Flooding is not a bad thing in this deck, and extra lands can be “put away” with Scroll Rack, and then shuffled away using a fetch land like Wooded Foothills (or Terramorphic Expanse if fetches are out of your budget.)

Interacting With Opponents:

As you comb through the decklist, you will see many ways that I use creatures, enchantments, and lands to interact with all possible situations.  I tried to balance between “best in slot” choices like Terastodon, (Did I mention RIP Sylvan Primordial?) and the Strip Mine package.  I also use a few more arcane choices like Viridian Zealot (to combat the deadly Torpor Orb), and Shivan Harvest (an old favorite that can utilize Radha’s attack mana).  I’ve thought of including Brutalizer Exarch in the Viridian Zealot slot if Torpor Orb becomes less prevalent in my metagame.

Preventing Interaction from opponents is also important.  You do not want to spend ten mana only to run headlong into a counterspell.  On our side in this battle: Boseiju, Who Shelters All, Dosan The Falling Leaf, and Vexing Shusher.  If you are playing against three mono-blue decks, you may want to use City of Solitude or Hall of Gemstone for additional opportunities for prevention.

Sarkhan Vol: Funny story, I once took control of an Avacyn and sacrificed it to Greater Good.

Homeward Path is pretty nice to have if your opponents play blue.  Reins of Power is scary, as is Insurrection, but to a lesser extent because it is not instant speed.

Dig:

This was the most expansive theme in my deck, as I really want to find that Primal Surge in competitive play.  Here is how I categorize the “dig” cards.

Asymmetrical Draw: These are some of the best available in Artifact/Green.  Red does not really have much to offer in this department.

Sensei’s Divining Top, Sylvan Library, Scroll Rack, Greater Good, Staff of Nin, Garruk Primal Hunter, Soul of the Harvest.

Symmetrical Draw:

Howling Mine, Font of Mythos, Rites of Flourishing, Temple Bell, Mikokoro Center of the Sea.

I don’t generally don’t use these because they are a “bad play.”  Think about it like this: if you play a Howling Mine, your opponents (x3) get to take full advantage of the draws before you do, which can often lead to your death.  However, if you have better card quality in your deck than they do, and more ways to get resources than they do, (Hint: This Deck) then the mines are an excellent choice.

Tutors:

Survival of the Fittest, Birthing Pod, Fierce Empath

These are the best Green has to offer in “permanent” tutors.  If Torpor Orb wasn’t such a thing I’d add Brutalizer Exarch.  Note the inclusion of Squee, which interacts exceptionally well with Survival and Pod.  (Note: I’ve left out the Artifact “tutor for anything” cards: Ring of Three Wishes and Planar Portal because it makes it too easy to snag Primal Surge.  This is a personal preference/challenge/fun factor choice for now).

The Opening Hand, and Closing Thoughts

When I take stock of my initial seven, here is what I’m looking for:

I generally want to see at least 3-4 land (hopefully in both colors so that I can cast turn two Radha, a ramp card, or Survival of the Fittest.  Dig and draw cards are very valuable early, and will really help you get the lands you need for your “big guns” at 6+ on your curve. You generally want to be proactive, and try to kill your opponents as fast as possible.  Be “the one who knocks!”  That said, don’t Primal Surge without protection.  If you like to use the “Partial Paris” mulligan rule, always throw back cards with CMC of 6+.  Always keep Primal Surge if it’s in your opening hand.

If you think the combo is too “infinite,” or too brutal, or you are playing against a less competitive playgroup, There are many ways to win besides Primal Surge, and I’d say that Surge is only responsible for 50% of my wins with this list. Occasionally, I experiment with the other Legendary Creatures in this deck as the Commander. and have had impressive results with Ruric Thar and God Xenagos. From experience, this list does benefit from Radha’s simplicity, non-threatening manner, and utility.

Thank you for reading this, my first article for The Wizard’s Cupboard!  please visit wizardscupboard.com for all of your Magic: The Gathering needs, and I’ll see you next time, for another EDH/Commander Primer!

-Craig
