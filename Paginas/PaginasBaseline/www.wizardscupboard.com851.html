The Modern Compendium – Part 3

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath
 

It’s time to get back to business here. It has been a little while, with my last two articles covering the BFZ set review and the possible implications of the battle (tango) lands in Modern. I didn’t go into Modern much with my BFZ set review, but I expect to see Bring to Light make a splash in the format. Retreat to Coralhelm is the hot tech with Knight of the Reliquary, generating a gigantic threat and removing blockers for a combo win. I expect some of the new lands to make an appearance, and Ulamog is a new addition to both Tron and Gifts Ungiven/Unburial Rites. Battle for Zendikar’s presence should make itself known in Modern

GP Oklahoma City was the last major Modern tournament. Lantern Control had been making its presence known with a few top 16 finishes, but it really stole the spotlight here by taking home the trophy. The deck is difficult to pilot and generates massive amounts of triggers on, so the results are mostly nonexistent on mtgtop8.com. My plan is to collect several decks that didn’t make this list for an additional Modern Compendium article, so I will get to Lantern Control eventually.

Delver has taken a large percentage hit, as Grixis Control continues to eat away at that place in the metagame. The increase in Affinity, Hexproof, and Living End are all likely due to their popularity at the World Championship. Those results are a little suspect, as the small field is highly incentivized to metagame against itself. The other changes look like nothing more than small fluctuations in the field. Let’s get to the decks!

 

Infect

Metagame Percentage: 4%
Deck Category: Combo
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview

Infect was one of the breakout decks of the first Modern Pro Tour, using Blazing Shoal to generate wins as early as the second turn. After the banning of Blazing Shoal, Infect became just another decent, but unremarkable, combo choice for Modern. The printing of Become Immense caused a resurgence for Infect, providing a powerful pump spell for the deck. With this addition, Infect has regained its place as one of the fastest decks in Modern, and it has become a much more popular deck.

Game Plan

Infect wants to resolve a creature and deal ten damage with it. Pump spells such as Vines of the Vastwood, Might of Old Krosa, and Become Immense increase the clock, while cards like Disrupting Strike and Apostle’s Blessing provide disruption and protection. Interaction is not the name of the game, Infect just wants to kill as fast as possible.

Strengths

Become Immense made Infect occasionally capable winning on turn two or three. This makes Infect one of the fastest combo decks in the format, flirting with the boundaries of what is considered acceptable in Modern. When operating correctly, the opponent will only have a few turns to execute their plan or mount a defense, or Infect will just win the game. Infect is another deck that gets to utilize its manabase with the addition of Inkmoth Nexus. There is also the fringe benefit of being able to win through high life totals.

Weaknesses

The interaction needed to beat Infect is creature removal, and almost no deck will be without that. A single Lightning Bolt can cause significant delays to Infect, either slowing development or killing a critical creature.

Infect shares some of the weaknesses of Delver with the low creature count. Infect only has 12-16 creatures to win the game and finding a replacement can be difficult if the first creature dies. None of the creatures in Infect have more than one toughness, so cards like Darkblast, Night of Soul’s Betrayal, and Ghirapur Aether Grid will singlehandedly win games.

Infect also fails to capitalize on the fetchland and shockland manabase in Modern. Opposing decks can freely damage themselves knowing that the repercussions will be minimal.

Variations

The standard version of Infect is Simic, but other versions exist. Mono green Infect is mostly a budget deck, as the second color significantly improves the deck. Sultai versions exist too, mostly for the addition of Plague Stinger/Phyrexian Crusader as additional evasive threats. The mana in Sultai is rather unstable, making the strategy even more inconsistent. The splash color is not popular.

Merfolk

Metagame Percentage: 5%
Deck Category: Aggro
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview

Merfolk is one of the few tribes in Modern that truly rewards sticking to a theme. There are several Merfolk lords available that provide +1/+1 to all other Merfolk, which quickly creates a dominating board position. The high density of creatures allows Aether Vial to shine, providing additional mana and adding a Flash aspect to the deck.

Game Plan

The main plan of Merfolk is to cast multiple lords to create an army of large creatures. Aether Vial allows for faster deployment and leaves mana available for disruption spells. Spreading Seas acts as both disruption and a Falter effect, as both Lord of Atlantis and Master of the Pearl Trident give other Merfolk Islandwalk. The deck will often leave Aether Vial set on two, allowing Silvergill Adept and multiple lords to be played from it.

Strengths

Aether Vial is a powerful card, and Merfolk is one of the few decks with sufficient creatures to utilize it. Combined with Cavern of Souls, Merfolk is extremely resilient to counterspells. Spreading Seas is also a powerful effect against multicolored decks, often causing extensive damage to an opponent’s early development. Decks reliant on red removal spells have difficulty killing Master of Waves, which can dominate the game if left unchecked.

Weaknesses

Lightning Bolt combined with Snapcaster can be a problem for this deck. If the opponent can overload on removal, the Merfolk deck will usually fall apart. Multiple creatures need to survive to generate a threatening board, as they are just 2/2 creatures without support. That creature density, while necessary, can cause the deck to lack sufficient permission for the combo decks. Merfolk, with only blue available, lacks some of the more powerful sideboarding options available in Modern.

Merfolk’s biggest weakness is to Affinity. That deck is significantly faster and blue doesn’t have good options to combat it. Hurkyl’s Recall is the strongest sideboard card available, but it isn’t always enough to alter the outcome.

Variations

The deck does not support splash colors well, considering the double blue in many of the casting costs, along with the presence of Mutavault. White is occasionally splashed for Path to Exile or Sygg, River Guide, and black is occasionally splashed for removal. Recently, green was tried Collected Company, but Master of Waves is the stronger four drop.

Collected Chord

Metagame Percentage: 3%
Deck Category: Midrange/Combo
Sample Decklist Links:
Abzan
Four Color Kiki
 
Overview

The Collected Chord decks are the remnants of the Birthing Pod decks that dominated the format. The shell of value creatures with multiple combos was powerful enough to survive without its engine card, and there are currently two distinctive decks. The Abzan Collected Chord deck is similar to a Junk deck in its disruptive abilities, but the creatures are more synergistic than intrinsically powerful. The four color deck usually relies on a Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker win or grinding the opponent out with value

Game Plan

Collected Chord usually attempts to grind out opponents looking to play fairly. Resilient creatures, such as Voice of Resurgance and Kitchen Finks, particularly backed by Gavony Township, can be difficult for decks like Jund and Grixis to beat. Beyond that, the deck usually contains one or more combos, capable of ending the game on the spot.

  • Viscera Seer/Cartel Aristocrat + Anafenza, Kin-Tree Spirit/Melira, Sylvok Outcast + Kitchen Finks = infinite life
  • Viscera Seer/Cartel Aristocrat + Anafenza, Kin-Tree Spirit/Melira, Sylvok Outcast + Murderous Redcap = infinite damage
  • Spike Feeder + Archangel of Thune = infinite life and giant creatures
  • Deceiver Exarch/Restoration Angel/Zealous Conscripts + Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker = infinite haste creatures

Chord of Calling in a deck with Eternal Witness, Restoration Angel, and Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker can tutor for the entire combo, given enough time.  Eternal Witness can return the Chord of Calling and Restoration Angel flickering Eternal Witness will return Chord of Calling again for Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker.

Strengths

The strength of the Collected Chord decks hinges on how versatile it can be. Kitchen Finks, Voice of Resurgance, Collected Company, and Gavony Township are all fantastic in long, drawn out slugfests. The combo elements of Collected Company give it a chance to race other combo decks. Chord of Calling artificially increases the number of powerful, situational creatures in the deck by providing more opportunities to access them.

Weaknesses

Cards like Chord of Calling and Fauna Shaman add virtual copies of hate cards, but they are not the fastest at finding them. Some of the faster combo decks can simply execute their combo before Collected Chord can find an answer. Generic disruption, like Thoughtseize and Path to Exile, is often left in the sideboard, so the deck relies on finding silver bullet creatures for the first game.

Collected Chord does not have access to much creature removal, relying on filling the board with creatures to stabilize. Creatures with flying can be somewhat problematic, particularly for the Abzan versions that often do not include Restoration Angel.

The combos that rely on the Persist mechanic are susceptible to graveyard hate cards. Grafdiggers Cage is an additional hate card that prevents both the Persist mechanic and Chord of Calling.

Variations

The two big versions are the Abzan deck and the Naya/four color deck. Beyond that, there are a lot of subtle variations depending on the pilot’s preferences and expectations for the metagame. None of these are big changes, but the deck’s strength is the ability to access these cards when it needs them.

The options are too numerous to list. Sufficient to say, if there is a hate creature that will devastate your deck, it would be better to play around it if possible. If you are looking to pilot the deck and are looking for ideas, I would search through old lists for inspiration.

UWx Midrange and UW Control

Metagame Percentage: 3% (Control), 4% (Midrange)
Deck Category: Midrange to Control
Sample Decklist Links:
Aggressive/Tempo
Tap-out Control
Heavy Control
 
Overview

The Azorious midrange and control decks are extremely difficult to separate, as there are three different decks and the distinctions between them are blurry. The most aggressive deck is usually Jeskai, featuring Geist of Saint Traft and burn spells. The other two versions are heavier control decks. One is filled with creatures that gain value from Restoration Angel or Sun Titan, and the other one is almost completely spell based, relying on Celestial Colonnade to win the game. The differences are pretty vague, and it can be difficult to differentiate between the decks with only the cards you see in a couple games. Some of the most distinctive cards for each deck are:

  • Aggressive/Tempo – Geist of Saint Traft
  • Tap-out – Wall of Omens
  • Heavy control – Think Twice
Game Plan

UWx Midrange looks to generate card advantage through cards like Snapcaster Mage and Electrolyze, while grinding the opponent’s resources down. The more aggressive decks can play a protect the queen strategy, resolving a Geist of Saint Traft and countering spells and removing creatures for the rest of the game. More controlling decks rely heavily on winning with Celestial Colonnade, one of the most powerful lands available in Modern. These decks use cards like Think Twice and Sphinx’s Revelation to generate an advantage until the opponent’s resources are depleted.

Strengths

The customization of the UWx Midrange deck is its biggest strength. There are cards available that run the full gambit from aggressive to midrange to control. Properly predicting a metagame will allow the UWx Midrange deck to have a substantial edge. Geist of Saint Traft, Restoration Angel, and Celestial Colonnade are all powerful cards with few other homes. The archetype exists due to the power of Celestial Colonnade and the finishing power it represents. However, knowing that the decks are moving away from Liliana of the Veil means you can show up with a bunch of Geist of Saint Traft and dominate the tournament. The different blue white decks offer a chance to use otherwise neglected powerful cards like Kitchen Finks, Vendillion Clique, Sun Titan, and more. Some of these individual cards can catch people off guard simply due to their ignorance of them. A deck full of Abrupt Decays will have a hard time beating a Sun Titan.

Weaknesses

All of the blue white decks rely on their sideboard to win matches. The cards the blue white decks use to beat midrange decks like Abzan, Jund, and Grixis are very different from the cards needed to beat Amulet Bloom, Boggles, and Affinity. Having the wrong maindeck configuration with any blue white deck means you will often be losing game one and reliant on both sideboard games to win a match. That can be an acceptable strategy occasionally, but it is a rough way to play through a big tournament. Bringing a bunch of Spell Pierces to a tournament full of Junk is a good way to ensure a quick exit from the tournament. Improper metagaming will be disastrous with any of these decks. While the customization is one of the deck’s greatest strengths, it can be one of its greatest weaknesses too. The low power level of the cantrips can be a problem too. Failing to find meaningful interaction in the early turns of a game can cause UWx Midrange to lose before it ever really gets started.

Variations

Most of the variations were mentioned already. Red adds a variety of burn spells, while black adds discard and additional removal spells. Black can also provide access to Lingering Souls.
