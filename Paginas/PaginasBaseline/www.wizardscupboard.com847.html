Welcome back to Zendikar! Â It’s been six years since we’ve trudged through the misty rain forests, cleansed our bloody daggers in the scalding tarns, taken refuge in its verdant catacombs, and felt the wind atÂ our backs as we scaled it’s arid mesas.   1. How good are those new dual lands?Â

  3. Do the Planeswalkers make the cut?
  4. Can we build around the new Legendary creatures?

That’s quite a tall order… but as you can see from the Landfall-inspired picture above, I have things well in hand! Lets begin!

The “Tango” Lands:

In a recent MtgSalvation poll, voters chose between several nicknames for these new lands that require two basic lands on your battlefield to come into play untapped. Â While “Battle Lands” seemed intuitive, the voters choice of “Tango Lands” rolls off of the tongue muchÂ better. Naming aside, we need to discuss function and decide where these lands fit into the noble hierarchy of dual lands.

Let’s remember our assumptions from the Mana 101 article:

  1. We are building a two or more color deck.
  2. We are evaluating the land in a vacuum.
  3. Untapped is best. Â And thus, we have to evaluate the drawback vs. its ability to produce mana untapped.

Pros:

  • They produce two colors of mana
  • They have basic land types.
  • They are fetch-able with Onslaught/Khans/Zendikar fetch-lands, as well as Krosan Verge.
  • They count for “domain,” meaning that they count for basic land types for cards like Collective Restraint, and will let you playÂ your Glacial Fortress and Drowned Catacombs untapped.

Cons:

  • You have to play basic lands. (If you do not play at least 2-4, I can’t think of a better reason to start!)
  • They will come into play tapped fairly often, but this is the case with most shock-lands in EDH as well.

To understand my thought process and thus my card evaluation of these lands will require a few words on my play style when it comes to opening hand, and the mana fixing process. Â Lets say my opener for my Mimeoplasm Reanimator deck is: Flooded Strand, Rite of Replication, Woodland Cemetery, Forest, Mystical Tutor, Consuming Abberation, Lord of Extinction, and It that Betrays (on the draw step).

When I see an opener like this, I automatically know that I want to get a Black/Blue dual land because I already have two green, and from experience with this deck I know I’m going to need BB and UU later. Â I also want to get UU as soon as possible, in case any great rite of Replication targets come up, or in case I draw Mana Drain or another Counterspell.

My possible fetch targets are: Basic Island, Underground Sea, Watery Grave, and now, Sunken Hollow.

Previous to BFZ, I’d almost always get Watery Grave and put it into play tapped, because there’s absolutely no reason to take two damage, and no reason to get Underground Sea, as I can get it later and untapped. Â If I need to, I can then untap and mystical tutor for Entomb, or Vampiric Tutor, Hermit druid, or most likely just get the Life from the Loam that will really get my deck moving by filling the graveyard. Â My second land will always be Woodland Cemetery, which is enabled into play untapped by Watery Grave.

Post BFZ, I can substitute Sunken Hollow for Watery Grave and make the exact… same… play. Â It might even be better to leave Sunken Hollow in my deck, and treat it as an additional Underground Sea when I have twoÂ basics online. Â I already have a forest in my hand.. it’s not a far cry to think that I will draw another one, or another fetch land in the next few turns to get one.

Because of the scenarios I just described, I must conclude that in EDH, Tangolands are Tier 4. (Slightly above basic lands, slighty below Shock Lands).

Worst Case Scenario: The only time when a Tangoland becomes slower than a shockland or checkland is when it is your second, third, or fourth land drawn and you didÂ not previously draw two basics or a fetchland. Â In this situation youÂ are struggling to fix your mana anyway. Â Be thankful to drawÂ your Tangoland.

Financially, They will likely stay at $6-10 short term, $10-20+ long term, as their usefulness in standard and modern formats will be decided by tournament players. Â To read more about that, check out Byron’s article on the subject here.
