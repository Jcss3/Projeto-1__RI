July 4, 2017

Hour of Devastation

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath
 
Set review!
 
At three mana, Abandoned Sarcophagus is too expensive to include for generic value.  This looks like a backup plan for something like Drake Haven, letting you actually cast all the Hieroglyphic Illuminations and Curator of Mysteries sitting in the graveyard.  Without a dedicated cycling deck, there will be too many times that this sits in play not doing anything.  If the format ever slows down enough for Drake Haven, this might be worth a good look. 
 
Since this is the Top 8 Game Day promo, I suppose it’s worth a closer look.  A 2 mana 1/1 with double strike is not too far from playable, but there aren’t a lot of decent enchantments to really push it over the edge.  The Eternalize ability is the real kicker here, as a 5 mana 4/4 double striker is a real threat.  Getting that bonus might be enough to push Abandoned Pouncer over the edge, but 4/4 creatures are surprisingly fragile in a world of Grasp of Darkness, Fatal Push, and Harnessed Lightning.  Not having any other forms of protection or evasion make me think that Abandoned Pouncer will ultimately wind up on the outside looking in.

It’s also a cat, for whatever that’s worth.

 

Ammit Eternal looks to be the pushed Afflict card in Hour of Devastation.  A three mana 5/5 is a strong threat.  Afflict makes it better and pushes it to be aggressive.  The drawback shrinks it over time and also pushes it to be aggressive.  Despite all of that there really isn’t a home for this in the current Zombie deck.  That deck is already glutted with three drops and is ultimately a synergy deck.  Plague Belcher seems like a better fit, and it already misses the cut.

I think the only home Ammit Eternal might have is in a midrange Rakdos or Jund deck.  I’m imagining something with Kalitas, Traitor of Ghet, Glorybringer, and a pile of disruption.  I expect Ammit Eternal to see some small amount of play in a deck like that.

 

Everything about this card seems to stack up worse than Eldrazi Displacer.  Costing more to get on the battlefield and adding a tap requirement are a couple big strikes against Angel of Condemnation, and some keywords and an Exert ability don’t come close to balancing things out.  The real benefit is not needing <> mana, but it still seems extremely safe to file this as unplayable for at least the next 3 months.  Probably a lot longer than that, but Angel of Condemnation might see a resurgence if something like Acidic Slime is reprinted.

 

Apocalypse Demon is overcosted and comes with a drawback to boot.  Bulk rare.

 

My suspicion is that this will occasionally be a roleplayer in some midrange or control deck, but it won’t see widespread play.  There isn’t enough artifact mana in this set to realistically cheat this out for less than six effective mana.  It’ll probably cost seven mana more often, as the single mana on the following turn is likely to go to waste.  Beyond all of the hidden costs to Bontu’s Last Reckoning, I just don’t imagine a format that really demands a three mana wrath effect.  Most boards won’t be developed enough by turn three to get respectable value from a sweeper in the first place.  Particularly with Heart of Kiran in the mix.

If this sees play, I expect the most likely homes are in sideboard G/B for other midrange decks.  It does pair well with Rhonas’s Last Stand, even if the mana gets a little tight.

 

If Champion of Wits makes it into Standard, it will be on the back of Elder Deep-Fiend.  This is great fodder for Emerge, and the discard works great with Kozilek’s Return.  Most of the Elder Deep-Fiend decks in recent history are Prized Amalgam decks that are always happy to have some additional loot effects.  Champion of Wits has a home for a few months, provided the metagame is ripe for a Kozilek’s Return deck.

 

There might be a ramp deck that happily includes a big creature with a sweeper attached.  I’m not sure this format is the right place for it though.  Maybe in a few months when Ulamog, the Ceaseless Hunger and Shrine of the Forsaken Gods rotates.

 

This card has some undeniable power, but it requires some important pieces.  Critically, there needs to be something that gains life for no mana cost that is already playable.  Something like Luxa River Shrine won’t cut it, as the card needs to be playable without Crested Sunmare.  Something like Ajani Goldmane would be perfect.  Even the common tapped lands from Khans of Tarkir that gained life would work well.  Ending the turn with two 5/5 creatures is a great payoff, so there is incentive to try Crested Sunmare.

 

Five mana for a 4/3 body that searches for a planeswalker is overcosted.  This might be an occasional sideboard card for decks that used to consider a Thalia’s Lancers plan.  You would need multiple good, different targets to justify Djeru as a tutor.  Otherwise, just play another copy of whatever planeswalker you wanted to find.

 

The front end of this card is just atrocious.  The idea is clearly to sideboard this against control decks, but they can just kill it before it starts eating away at their hand.  Eternalize is a nice option to rebuy, but this kind of effect is more important in the early game.  Plus, the token is still fairly vulnerable to removal spells.  Dreamstealer didn’t even have the decency to be a Zombie for tribal synergies.

 

This seems like a roleplayer in G/B decks for the mirror match.  Relying on a bunch of creatures connecting isn’t a solid plan against control decks.  Other midrange decks should get obliterated by this card, since you shouldn’t have a problem burying them with the cards you draw and strip out of their hand.  Casting Driven and Despair on the same turn should be the goal, and I wouldn’t treat this as a two cost card often.

 

Red aggressive decks will welcome this card.  A 2/1 creature with Haste is an underwhelming playable.  The blocking trigger is a nice touch.  Eternalize is a bigger bonus here, as it provides some good reach with the improved trigger.  Unfortunately, it doesn’t look like red will be getting the tools for a good aggressive deck this time around.

 

If this shows up, it will be in place of Westvale Abbey.  Endless Sands can slowly eat creatures that are dying to removal and spit them all out later for some extra damage.  That will play similarly to Westvale Abbey’s token generation.  Endless Sands lacks the impressive backside of Westvale Abbey, but it comes without Abbey’s risk of blowout.

Realistically, I don’t expect this card to see a ton of play.  Maybe a singleton in a deck without heavy mana requirements.

 

There isn’t anywhere close to enough support for this kind of card.  Bulk rare.

 

If God-Pharaoh’s Gift makes it into Standard, I wouldn’t be surprised to see Gate to the Afterlife tutoring for it.  Gate to the Afterlife drops the cost down to five and doesn’t require a full payment upfront.  It does need creatures in the graveyard, but God-Pharaoh’s Gift doesn’t do anything without those anyway.  Filling the graveyard requires some dedication, but the theme meshes well with Delirium.

I don’t expect this to be the next big deck in Standard, but I think there might be a place for it.  Delirium decks or Cryptolith Rites decks look like great places to start.

 

I originally thought Grind could put two counters on a single creature, but reality is much worse than that.  Your deck needs to value Dust to play Grind // Dust, and the deck that wants this effect the most doesn’t usually run white.  Maybe it is worth the splash, but I’m not a believer

Grind might wind up as a playable sideboard card if something like Dragon Fodder shows up.  That makes it a niche sideboard card at best.

 

This card would be unplayable even without the untap drawback.  You can’t even luck into chaining these together to get a payoff that might be worth six mana.  As Aetherworks Marvel has taught us, this kind of random effect can be worth the effort if the payoff is good enough.  Putting an upper bound on what cards this can hit pretty much kills the upside of this kind of random effect.

 

This card just screams Myr Enforcer.  There isn’t really a fair way to play this card, but I could imagine it as a roleplayer or sideboard option for Drake Haven or New Perspectives.  It also has some solid synergies with a lot of the cards in the U/R Emerge deck from forever ago, both as a payoff card and fuel for Elder Deep-Fiend.  Some Noose Constrictor brew might also be interested.  Hollow One will need some testing, but I’d be more surprised if it doesn’t find a home than if it does.

Hollow One might be good enough for Living End in Modern, since I imagine there are some matchups that you’d be happy to just jam a 1 mana 4/4 on turn two.

 

A colorless creature land is always worth a second look, as almost all of them have been playable over the years.  Creature lands usually function as a source of reach in aggressive decks.  In control decks, they are emergency blockers, backup planeswalker removal, and alternative win conditions.

Hostile Desert is going to play much better in a control or midrange deck than an aggressive deck.  A deck will need to play some of the cycling lands or the sacrifice deserts, and those are both more likely in midrange and control.  Hostile Desert also works much better as a blocker than an attacker.  The threat of activation will prevent some attacks, and any turn that it doesn’t need to be activated saves lands in the graveyard for future turns.  An aggressive deck would actually have to find fuel for multiple activations.  Lastly, slower decks shouldn’t mind the colorless land as much as an aggressive deck.

Modern is a somewhat different story.  Fetch lands are everywhere, giving Hostile Desert a ton of fuel.  However, the graveyard is a precious commodity to several decks and this might not be worth a slot.  I expect to see it tried, but I’m unsure if it will be good enough.  Dodging Lightning Bolt is excellent, but Fatal Push won’t have a problem with it.

 

I was originally pretty excited about this card.  It gives U/R control decks a great answer to everything besides Heart of Kiran out of Vehicles, Zombies, and Temur Energy.  I’ve cooled on it slightly, but it is still an effective sweeper for a color combination that doesn’t usually get one.  Several planeswalkers can naturally survive a 5 damage spell, and that will become more important as a deckbuilding consideration.  Verdurous Gearhulk is really the only creature that laughs at a five damage sweeper.  That will give G/B an advantage over control decks that most other midrange and aggressive decks wont’ have.

Overall, I would be very surprised if this card doesn’t see play.  If it doesn’t, it will be due to Hour of Revelation being playable in the same deck.

 

Mono blue gets a reanimation spell, though it is really expensive.  It does scale if plenty of mana is available.  However, you really need the Eternalize feature to be an improvement to play Hour of Eternity over Liliana, Death’s Majesty.  Likely a bulk rare.

 

Four mana removal spells are usually niche roleplayers instead of staples.  If exiling creatures is important, having access to a spell like this in your toolbox is good.  If exiling a creature isn’t important, I’d be surprised to see this over something like Unlicensed Disintegration.

 

I expect this to see some play in the coming Standard season, almost exclusively finding Shrine of the Forsaken Gods.  This puts Ulamog, the Ceaseless Hunger in range the following turn when this essentially ramps four mana.  Sanctum of Ugin and Sea Gate Wreckage provide some additional value if that much ramp isn’t needed.  Hour of Promise seems like a solid playable card.  I think the desert part isn’t important, but it might be worth including few deserts just in case.

Finding Valakuts in Modern also seems like an easy inclusion.  I expect this to see play there too.

 

Essentially a Planar Cleansing reprint, Hour of Revelation seems poised to make an appearance.  Control decks have needed a way to completely clear a board, and Hour of Revelations delivers.  Creatures, vehicles, and planeswalkers are all swept up by this.  I would be surprised if a U/W deck, possibly splashing red, came about solely due to this answer being available.

 

Cute, but it is way too hard to string together spells with the correct mana cost.  This is particularly awkward when it wants you casting one cost spells on turn four.  Beyond the difficulty in setting it up, it likely wrecks your mana curve.  Bulk rare.

 

This seems like a worse version of Confiscation Coup.  Over the course of two turns, it costs way more mana.  Kefnet’s Last Word can steal more expensive things (and enchantments), but I don’t think that is worth the untap downside.

 

If there is some combo with this card, I don’t know what it is.  Leave seems worse than something like Heroic Intervention as a protection spell, so you would need to play it to retrigger abilities on your creatures.

Chance might be something that modern dredge decks are interested in.  Casting a four mana spell might be a bit of a challenge though.

 

Maybe Sam Black’s Abzan token deck is looking for a finisher?  Creatures with the keyword sharing ability never seem to work out well, and I’d be surprised if this was any different.

 

This screams combo, but I’m not sure what to do with it beyond that.  At five mana to get the first activation, you need to do something broken to justify the cost.  Possibly something like Aetherflux Reservoir.

Mirage Mirror is one of multiple cards in this set that combo with Dark Depths.  I suppose that matters in Legacy and Commander.

 

This might be a good card to bridge the gap in a ramp deck.  If Nheb, the Eternal gets to attack, you should have at least eight mana on your second main phase.  A land drop and actually connecting for four puts you immediately in range of Ulamog, the Ceaseless Hunger.  All that said, it is a five cost card that doesn’t do very much on its own.  I don’t expect this to have much impact outside of Commander.

 

Plenty of people have written about Nicol Bolas already, so I’ll quickly go over the abilities.

The plus two ability is a way to gain value on the board.  You get to play the top spell off your opponent’s deck for free, which is a solid and variance ridden ability.  Hitting a Censor is pretty embarrassing, while hitting a Glorybringer is awesome.

The plus one ability is a great option for defending Nicol Bolas from removal like Never//Return.  Stripping two cards out of an opponent’s hand is an incredible option, and should help lock away any game that Nicol Bolas makes an appearance in.

The minus four ability should handle just about any creature or planeswalker in play.  If the opponent is low on life, it can hit them in the face too.

The ultimate is nice, but I can’t imagine a scenario where you need it if you have time to get Nicol Bolas up to twelve loyalty.  If you could protect your planeswalker for four turns, you probably don’t want to bother sweeping the board.

Overall, Nicol Bolas, God-Pharaoh is a sweet card.  I’m just not sure it’s worth the mana investment.  Torrential Gearhulk seems like a cheaper and more reliable win condition.  I wouldn’t be surprised if this card makes more appearances in a deck with Oath of Nissa than a dedicated Grixis control deck.  Some sort of a tapout midrange deck seems like a much better home for Nicol Bolas than a pure control deck.

 

Without the last line of text, Nimble Obstructionist would already be playable.  Cards like Spell Queller are better with additional support, and Nimble Obstructionist provides more options when you leave up mana.  Getting to Stifle a trigger when you cycle Nimble Obstructionist is a great bonus.

Overall, I don’t think this card is in the same league as Vendillion Clique.  Needing to pick between the body and the Stifle is unfortunate.  I still expect the card to see play.  U/W Tempo definitely wants it, and various blue control decks should at least consider a Stifle/creature split card.

 

This card gains a ton of life, so I don’t want to immediately discount it.  Usually, gaining life is at a small enough rate that it isn’t worth a card, but gaining 19 life for three mana is a different story.  Unfortunately, the untap drawback strikes again.  If your life total is being threatened mostly by burn spells (Modern burn), this should lock the game up for you.  If your life total is being threatened by creatures, your opponent can probably negate the life gain over two turns of inaction from you.  This seems like a possible sideboard card, but I don’t think it’ll ever be more than that.

 

If you can make it to eight mana, this does a great job of just thrashing an opponent.  Letting planeswalkers work is a bit of a bummer, but Overwhelming Splendor should shut down a lot of decks.

It is important to know that the G/B deck’s +1/+1 counter theme will still buff creatures through the power and toughness reduction.

 

Cat tribal?  I think I’d rather have Hooded Brawler if I was playing a green aggressive deck, and that’s not a high bar to pass.

 

Crucible World on a stick seems like a mediocre card in Standard.  It certainly works with the cycling lands from Amonkhet, along with several of the desert lands in Hour of Desvastation.  It’s just an underwhelming creature to gain value from.  Currently, I’d rather run Tireless Tracker for a similar effect.

Modern is a slightly different story, as Ramunap Excavator works well with fetch lands, Collected Company, Chord of Calling, Ghost Quarter, and Knight of the Reliquary.  I expect it to make appearances in several fair decks.  The G/W midrange deck that Todd Stevens played a while ago was the first home that I thought of for Ramunap Excavator.

 

I suppose there might be a deck that runs enough deserts to justify Ramunap Hydra.  Even if there are enough deserts that see play, this reminds me of Woodland Wanderer.  A nice, large body that just never finds a home.

 


Razaketh, the Foulblooded is definitely no Griselbrand.  That said, it might be a decent top end for a deck that uses Cryptolith Rites.  I’m not sure what you’re looking for that is better than an 8/8 flying trampler, but you can assemble all sorts of combos with enough fodder.  Since it doesn’t cost any mana to use, I certainly wouldn’t write this card off immediately.  However, I don’t plan to be the one trying to break the card either.

 

Reason is the real question, as the collective Magic community hasn’t had the chance to test a cantrip with decent Scry without a draw effect.  Theory articles like to write that a Scry is worth something like 0.6 of a card.  Reason will put that to the test.  If this ends up good enough, every blue deck is going to run it.  If it isn’t, Reason // Believe is never going to see play.

I suppose Believe works with Nissa, Steward of the Elements, but I still doubt that it is worth it.

 

This does some cute things, but it’ll be relegated to Commander.  Maybe I’ll get to hear a story about how this domes someone for eight when they cast Overwhelming Splendor.  Bulk rare.

 

I suppose this might have a hole to fill when Sylvan Advocate rotates, but I’m skeptical even then.  It is difficult to get value off the trigger on turn two, and I’m not sure if the trigger is even good by the time you reach turn six.  Seems like a bulk rare.

 

I suppose this is the best of the cycle of cards that doesn’t let you untap lands.  Giving you an giant creature makes it more likely than usual to actually end the game before the drawback really affects you.  I can’t really imagine skipping turn three in this format, as three cost cards are a critical point in almost every deck.  Even later in the game, I’m still skeptical.  The snake token dies to a ton of stuff in Standard, including Grasp of Darkness, Fatal Push, and most Harness Lightnings.

 

God, this card looks awful.  An Electrostatic Pummeler might be interested in Samut, the Tested for the Double Strike plus ability.  Minus two to cast forked bolt is a really poor defensive ability, but I suppose it exists.  The ultimate can do some cute things, but I have a hard time imagining the game isn’t over if you can double strike a creature for three turns.

Arlinn Kord seems like a better option in Electrostatic Pummeler, so I really expect Samut, the Tested to not see play.  It does some cute things in Modern with Doubling Season, but that is a tiny upside.

 

This looks like a great hate card for decks like G/B Delirium.  It isn’t particularly strong, but including it in a deck is a pretty low cost.  It also does some work against Prized Amalgam, Torrential Gearhulk, Hostile Desert, and the Zombie deck.  Scavenger Grounds looks like a well-designed piece of graveyard hate.

 

There are a few large interactions with Solemnity in Standard.  Energy is the biggest, as Solemnity completely prevents that.  Walking Ballista isn’t a card with Solemnity in play, and most of the Winding Constrictor deck takes a hit from this enchantment.

Modern has plenty of combos with Solemnity, with Phyrexian Unlife being one of the best.  There are also plenty of cards that use counters, like Chalice of the Void, Engineered Explosives, and Arcbound Ravager.  Legacy has even more combos with Dark Depths and a ton of Cumulative Upkeep cards like Glacial Chasm.

I expect to see Solemnity in all of those formats.  It will mostly be sideboard hate in standard, but modern and legacy are both likely to pick it up.

 


This looks like a bulk/Commander rare to me.

 


The new cycle of Gods are very different from anything else we have seen before.  These three cards play much more as recursive creatures, since they don’t have to be turned on, can die, and require a substantial mana investment to redeploy.  Needing to recast them is the biggest hit.  At this point, it is pretty ingrained into players that you can’t kill Gods.  These are different, just regular creatures that can come back.  Once that sinks in, I expect these cards will look worse.  Not requiring any setup to make them work might be a good exchange, but I’m uncertain without playing them

With all that out of the way, I don’t think The Locus God is particularly exciting.  It looks like a durdly six mana creature that is leagues worse than Torrential Gearhulk.  It can certainly generate an army if it survives a turn cycle, but I’d expect that from anything that I cast for six mana.

Not that this matters much, but The Locus God seems sick with Skullclamp.

 


All my generic thoughts on the new Gods from The Locus God apply here.

I’m not really sure what home The Scarab God will find.  It is a nice, recursive body with a decent triggered ability and a very solid activated ability, but U/B isn’t normally a creature deck.  It might work in some sort of control deck where you use the ability on the opponent’s graveyard.  Zombies could also consider this, but I’m not sure blue offers enough to justify the splash.

I wouldn’t rule out The Scarab God as a playable card, but I wouldn’t be surprised if it misses the mark.

 

All my generic thoughts on the new Gods from The Locus God apply here.

Several people have compared The Scorpion God to Olivia Voldaren, and the comparison makes sense.  This card will dominate a battlefield if it survives for a turn cycle or two, decimating an opponent’s board.  The activated ability is more expensive than Olivia Voldaren’s was, but it is permanent and The Scorpion God has a big enough body to control the battlefield in the meantime.  If a Rakdos or Jund midrange deck exists in the new format, I expect The Scorpion God to make the cut.  If the metagame doesn’t call for this sort of effect, it is still a fine sideboard card.

 

I’m well past the point where I get optimistic about cards that let your opponent make a choice.  I think any deck that wants this card will just be better served with regular Fireball (Burn from Within).  This is just a clunky finisher that theoretically scales well.  It isn’t particularly good until you get X up to 4+, and you can start casting Torrential Gearhulk, Nicol Bolas, God-Pharaoh, Sphinx of the Final Word, or whatever other finisher you want.  Bulk rare.

 

If you’re looking to assemble some infinite combo with creatures, I suppose this could be your card.  The way X works on here is awkward, since you can’t ever search for two one mana creatures or three two mana creatures.  With the weird working, sorcery speed, and putting the cards in your hand, this seems too clunky for Standard.  It probably does some crazy stuff in Commander.  Modern might play it to find Vizier of Remedies and Devoted Druid, but I think those decks are pretty full with four Collected Companies and four Chord of Calling already.

 


There are a lot of things that are just a little too bad about this card.  Only having four toughness is the biggest, as it dies to a lot of removal.  The cost reduction is pretty much impossible to leverage in Standard, and it is competing with Torrential Gearhulk for a finisher slot.  I doubt this makes the cut outside of some tribal Commander deck.

 


Why does a card with the name Wildfire unable to actually survive the card Wildfire?  Realistically, Wildfire Eternal is not going to do what you want it to do.  If you have a grip full of cards, the opponent will just aim a removal spell at it or take four a turn.  If you don’t have a lot of cards, they will often just take the point of damage.  This seems like a fragile way to cheat the mana on a card, and I’m not even sure what spell is exciting enough to cheat.  Maybe Approach of the Second Sun?

 

That covers all the rares and mythics in Hour of Devastation.  Overall, I’m pretty unimpressed with the new cards.  The Scorpion God, Scavenger Grounds, Nimble Obstructionist, Nicol Bolas, God-Pharaoh, Hour of Promise, Hour of Devastation, Hour of Revelation, Hostile Desert, Champion of Wits, and Ammit Eternal are cards I expect to break into Standard.  Overwhelming Splendor, Hollow One, and God Pharaoh’s Gift seem good if they find a home, but I’m not sure that they warrant a deck being built around them.  Solemnity seems like a lock for sideboards in Standard, but there aren’t any combos to give it a spot in the starting 60.  Ramunap Excavator, Solemnity, Hollow One and Hostile Desert seem like the cards that will break into Modern, with Hostile Desert and Hollow One being uncertain bets.  Some of the other cards will probably have their day in the sun, but I don’t expect them to become staples in any format.

 

Good luck at the prerelease!
