Commander 101: Mana in EDH Part One: The Basics (and Nonbasics)

 Commander 101: Mana in EDH Part One: The Basics (and Nonbasics)

by Craig Sidorowicz

1.  “I had a great hand, but…”

  • a. I didn’t draw enough lands.
  • b. I didn’t have the right colors.
  • c. My opponent strip mined my Gaea’s Cradle (how dare he!)
  • d. All of the above.

 Yep.  Mana screwed again.  Its always painful, and it’s always memorable.  But what if I told you it only happens to me in about one in twenty games?  In this two-part installment of Commander 101, I’m on a crusade to eradicate mana-screw complaints forever.  In part one, I’ll provide insights on how many lands to play, as well as my analysis and tiered rankings of the lands available in the format.  In part two, I’ll go deeper into the deckbuilding aspect of mana and explain how to choose, build, and ramp with deck-appropriate creature, mana rock, spell-based, and combination packages.

Quantity of lands

First off, How many lands should you play?  The short answer: Play 36-38 minimum, on average.  At the extremes (examples below) I’ve seen as high as sixty, and as low as twenty five.  Please remember that the primary goal is to never miss a land drop or resource development opportunity.  If you want to stay active and relevant in the pod (you may recall that this is how I define “fun” in EDH), you need resources.  If an opponent has 8-10 mana available on turn six, when you have 4-5, they are likely at an advantage.  Now multiply by three and consider how your resource base is dwarfed when compared to your “collective opponent,” that has 24-30 resources available.  To visualize this, think of the pod as a pie, and consider what percentage of the pie you currently control.  I like to have at least a quarter of the pie or more, or I perceive myself to be “behind.”

I still see players greedily undercutting the mana base of their decks to include more creatures and spells. I also see players counting cards like Sol Rings and Llanowar elves as land.  When you “goldfish” your deck (draw a hand and play out a few turns with no opponent,) you might find that your elves and sol rings always survive and never get disrupted.  But when a devious opponent hits you with turn-3 Toxic Deluge, or Vandalblasts your mana-rock collection, you are now in rebuilding mode.  The players who didn’t miss their land drops will have an easier time rebuilding, because they do not have to waste as much “tempo” (timely and efficient use of resources) casting new mana rocks or waiting for new elves to recover from summoning sickness.

 Extreme Example: #1: Sam Black’s Sixty Lands Azusa

Back in 2010, when EDH was still in its early stages of its popularity, pro-tour veteran Sam Black wrote this article that opened a mental door for me when it came to land quantity in EDH:

“More is usually better!”

This deck plays Azusa as the commander, -sixty- lands, Howling Mines, additional land enablers, all ramping into the endgame of eldrazis.  The reason the deck works is that it has actually has enough lands to utilize with extra land enablers like Azusa, Oracle of Mul Daya, Burgeoning, and Exploration.  Notice how few “threats” the deck runs (about 6 that could be considered finishers).  Many neophyte and even seasoned players make the mistake of thinking that they need an extreme amount of cards to finish multiple opponents, but this just isn’t true.  Just think about how often a single creature like Avenger of Zendikar, an Eldrazi, or a Vorinclex have taken over a game.  Below is an updated and street-legal version of this classic created by a Tappedout.net user named Gdubya. He explains that Its best opening hand is capable of playing a turn-2 Vorinclex off of Natural Order. (Click to enlarge picture)

 Extreme Example #2: Ezuri Elf Overrun

 

This is typically the deck I see taking advantage of a low “average converted mana cost,” to use a significantly lower quantity of lands.  A low cost commander in green paired with other low-cost mana creatures is typically how such a deck can get away with such a low land count.  Above is a typical list I found using google.  Unless you have a very low mana curve such a low land count is not recommended.

(Mana Curve is the breakdown of converted mana costs of the deck, usually represented in a bar graph that shows a curve)  For reference, 3.0 average mana cost makes for a pretty low curve for EDH. (Click to enlarge pictures)

 

Quality of Lands: Evaluating and and Ranking the Options.

Now, lets take a look at the tools available to help us.

 About the tier list:

When evaluating each cycle of lands, I graded them based on the following metrics:

  1. Does it produce colored mana?
  2. Does it produce multiple types of colored mana, if so, how many?
  3. Does it come into play untapped?
  4. Does it have another drawback? (Deals damage, requires/depends on having another colored mana in play?
  5. Does it have a basic land type to take advantage of the effects that have synergy with those land types?

 Assumptions:

  1. We are building for a two-or-more color deck.  If you are playing mono-color you have quite a bit less thinking to do because this tier list is removed from consideration, and you’d just play the best lands available that produce your color, and whatever utility lands fit well with the theme of your deck.
  2. We are evaluating these lands in a vacuum, absent the thought of the deck’s contents or plans, for now.  This is a “raw power” evaluation, because the “ifs” and “buts” can spiral out of control quickly if we don’t provide some focus.  This is a “101” article after all.
  3. Untapped is Best.  While “speed is king” in 20-life 1v1 formats, it’s not as much of a factor in a multi-player, 40-life format. That said, lands that come into play untapped and produce the color you want will always have greater utility than those that do not.  In the “building stage” (which I mentioned last article), you will likely want your lands to come into play untapped so that you can avoid waiting a turn to put your plan into place, or cast your low-cost commander.  Untapped lands are also really convenient if you need to board-wipe on any of those turns if a threatening situation develops.  Unless you compose your manabase entirely of “comes into play untapped” lands, you will be faced with decisions on which lands to play first.  Always analyze your hand to determine which spells you want to cast this turn, and on successive turns.  If you have nothing to do with the mana a land produces this turn (usually the first few turns) tapped lands are the better play.  Of course, the mana color requirements of the cards in your hand and the situation on the battlefield may force you to make adjustments to your plan.  Always try to anticipate your future needs, and have a backup plan if disrupted by opponents.

 The Top Three

#1: Original Dual Lands

Aside from Command Tower, you can’t do better than the original ten. (WOTC can’t print anything better than these outside of commander format).  They give you both land types with no drawback except for being nonbasic (yes, this is a drawback because of all of the nonbasic hate-cards that exist). They are expensive to own, but even the 3rd Edition Revised copies are well worth the investment.  I personally own two of each, and plan to acquire more as opportunity presents. They will never be reprinted (outside of Magic Online) so their value will only continue to rise.  If you have access to these, play them.

 #2: Fetchlands

These are second-best because they search for the basic land type (not just basics) and bring it into play untapped.  Their synergy with original duals and shocks make mana-fixing a breeze, at the very affordable cost of one life. These also have several subtle bonuses.

First, they “thin your deck” by removing lands from it, thus reducing the odds you’ll topdeck lands when you need spells.  Second, they can be used to shuffle your library to give you new options with your Sensei’s Divining Top or Scroll Rack.  Their biggest drawback is the time it takes to search and shuffle, so keep that in mind when playing in a full pod.  Know what land you want, or pass the turn and search if at all possible.

If you don’t own the allied color (onslaught/khans) versions to play in your decks, what are you waiting for?  They will never be this cheap again unless they get reprinted again in the long term.  As far as the enemy-color (zendikar) versions, they should be reprinted soon in Modern Masters 15 or in Battle for Zendikar block, so plan to acquire them after they hit peak supply after reprinting.

(Financial Tip: Wait until the hype dies down, and enough packs have been opened for supply to meet or exceed demand, thus driving prices to the lowest point.  Given the popularity and power of these lands, the window for action might not stay open for very long. A good place to track this is on mtgstocks.com)

#3: Shocklands

These are third best because they mimic the original duals (including basic land types) for the cost of two life.  Otherwise, they function like a guildgate.  Obviously, do not shock yourself unless you need the mana that turn, or need to bluff a Counterspell.  Financially speaking, Return to Ravnica block is not that far behind us, so the shocks are still available at reasonable prices, for now. They are also worth having in your collection, and will likely begin to rise in price the farther we get from their most recent printing.

#4: Basic Lands/ Snow Covered Basic Lands

This is probably where some of you may start to disagree with me, but basic lands are very important to include in each of your decks.  Nonbasic hate is real, and keeps getting worse.   Its painful to watch a player scoop up their cards after Blood Moon, “die” to Ruination, or wave goodbye to their battlefield when a Wave of Vitriol arrives.  If you don’t play basics they effectively read “game over” in the card text box.  Basic lands also allow you access to some of the most powerful ramp spells in the format (Solemn Simulacrum, cultivate, Kodama’s reach, Sakura-Tribe Elder, Burnished Hart, Myriad Landscape).

They also consistently do what you want them to do.  They produce the mana your deck needs, and are largely immune to hate strategies.  Understand that not playing with at least one basic land in each of your colors creates a serious liability for your manabase.

Snow covered basics have the additional utility of working with your Extraplanar Lens while shutting opponents out of the lens’ normally bilateral effect.

#5: Check Lands / other conditional untapped lands (torment swamplands, lorwyn creature type lands).

They produce two colors and it’s really easy to meet their “untapped” condition if you play lands from tiers 1-4. Even if they come into play tapped, they still untap and become dual lands without any drawback (aside from not having a basic land type).  All of these are very affordable to own because of multiple printings, but may increase in price as Wizards has decided not to print core sets anymore.

#6: Pain Lands

They always enter untapped, and can tap for colorless without taking a damage.  City of Brass and Mana Confluence tap for any color, but you always pay the life.  These are typically $3 when they are not in standard, so if you need to acquire these, it may be wise to wait a few months until they rotate from that format.

#7: Filter lands

 

These are good, and many players assume that because they are expensive to own ($10 and up) they are stronger than they really are. (The price is mainly due to their rarity and utility in Modern). They often get cut from my 3-color lists because they are awkward when drawn in multiples in early turns.

#8: Untapped Allied Futurelands

Wouldn’t this make a cool band name? Allied Futurelands provide decent utility but have odd checks or drawbacks that occasionally produce interesting effects.  Grove of the Burnwillows/ Punishing Fire is a classic combo (albeit underpowered in this format). And Horizon Canopy stands out in colors that have a difficult time drawing cards. It’s too bad that the R/B version is just a filter land, and the U/W version is a modified Checkland.

 #8.5: Other Untapped Mana Producing Lands

-Scars Turn 3 allied duals.  These make more sense to include in a tiny leaders deck, otherwise they are useful in fast decks that have allied colors (Bant and Jund and Esper stand out because they can use two of the five in this cycle)

-Charge untapped (gemstone) This is a command tower with three charges.  It’s pretty cool if you are playing a proliferate strategy or crucible of worlds, but unless I am really desperate or have very greedy mana requirements (10 or more cards with triple mana symbols in their cost) I leave this one in the box.

-Untapped single color-producing utility lands (urborg, kjeldoran Outpost, Nykthos, Cabal Coffers, Gaea’s Cradle).  You may be surprised to find the mighty cradle and coffers this far down the list.  They are very, very powerful indeed, however they are contingent on you having other cards in play.   Cradle is horrible if you have zero creatures, and if you have a coffers without urborg on the table in a non-mono-black deck, it loses much of its utility.

-Untapped conditional mana producing lands: meteor crater, etc

Tapped

#9: Ravnica Bounce Lands

These are played often, because they ramp us, and give us an opportunity to reuse a land like bojuka bog and khalni garden.  They get worse the more colors you are playing, because you never want to draw two in a row due to the loss in tempo they cause.

 #10: Man-Lands

The allied-color Worldwake lands are great to have.  i hope that Battle for Zendikar brings the opposing color manlands! They are outstanding when played alongside equipment.

Additionally Urza’s Legacy had single-color manlands, and Lorwyn and Antiquities brought us Mutavault and Mishra’s Factory.

My personal favorites include Creeping Tar Pit and Faerie Conclave for their ability to evasively hit Planeswalkers.

10.5 and beyond.

These have subtle effects, and by the time you get to this point you are only gaining marginal utility for the tapped mana fixing land.  Personally I’d rather play basics, unless budget constraints desperately push you into into these realms.

-Tricolored/Dragon lairs: Thanks to Khans of Tarkir., we now have access to all ten color combinations.   They are great to have in a three colored deck, however I dislike drawing them after turns 1-3

-Scrylands (Theros Block Temples)

-Lifelands (Zendikar and Khans)

-Tapped Charge lands/storage lands (Fallen Empires, Time Spiral)

-Fetch into tapped basics (Evolving Wilds, Terramorphic Expanse, Bant Panorama)

-Guildgates

Configuring Your Mana Base

-Two-color decks:  you’ll likely use tiers 1-10, and a few more basics (15-20) and utility lands 6+ than a 3-color deck.  Aside from mono-color decks, this is pretty easy to configure.

-Three-color decks: Use everything you have available from tiers 1-3, then delve into one or two of the lesser tiers before finishing with basics.  I like to run ten basics, then around 5 utility lands that have synergy with the deck.  You migt specialize in two of the three colors then add a splash of the third, which can allow you to go heavier on the two-color lands in the specialized colors, and include fewer of those that have the splash color.

-Five-color deck: This is often the most difficult to build correctly, but you can run all 30 lands from tier 1-3.  This is not recommended though, because you usually specialize in two or more colors.   For example, your Scion of the Ur Dragon deck might be mostly Red and Green with a splash of the other colors.  Therefore try to avoid over-doing it on the lands that don’t produce either of those: (u/w, u/b, w/b).  As for basics, play at least one of each non-mountain basic land (since Blood Moon will turn your nonbasics into mountains anyway).  From there, try to squeeze in 3-4 utility lands that have synergy with your deck’s plan. 

Now Lets use our knowledge to build a mana base for this Abzan Karador deck!

 So how many basics and non basics should we play?

As I’ve explained before, The quick and dirty way to figure out basics is to count your mana symbols, although there is occasionally more analysis required.

 My deck has:

44 black

28 green

31 white

 Without much analysis and a little rounding, I could get by with 4 swamps, 3 forests, 3 plains.  The problem is, I need green in the beginning stage for my early drops, so I want to make sure that I draw at least one untapped green mana in my first three turns.  My white cards are significantly higher mana cost, so I won’t need white until later turns. For these reasons I may add an additional plains and forest, making it an even 12 basics.

When deciding which Non Basics to play, I will likely make sure that they all produce green, and one or more additional colors.  This way, I am more likely to draw the green I need in the beginning stage.  That said, I’m still playing Scrubland and Godless Shrine, because I tend to always use the top three lands in all combinations that my deck will fit.

 So the first ten will be Command Tower and the 9 applicable lands from tiers 1-3.
