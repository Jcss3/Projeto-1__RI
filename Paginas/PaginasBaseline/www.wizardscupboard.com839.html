“Commanding Presents:” The Commander 2015 Set Review

By Craig Sidorowicz

On November 13th, Commander 2015 will bring us 55 new ‘gifts:’ including ten legendary creatures, five “Command-like” spells called confluences, and Myriad creatures that attack all of our opponents at once.  Since there are so many cards to review, I’ll cut right to the chase:

The Legendaries

 

Boros:

Anya, Merciless Angel: I could see her taking the helm of a “Voltron” equipment/enchantment deck, perhaps a R/W control deck.  I could envision a deck that seeks to hold opponents at bay with pillowfort effects like Ghostly Prison, or mass removal effects, lets opponents beat each other up, then plays a giant Anya to finish everyone off.  She could also work well with an Angel synergy deck.  Some possibilities here!  Rating: 6/10.

Kalemne, Disciple of Iroas:  My initial instinct would be to put him in a Naya deck with a bunch of big green creatures (such as Mayael), or even a R/W Giants and Dragons.  You could always ignore the experience counter thing all together and just throw a bunch of equipment on him to take advantage of Double Strike and Vigilance.  Then again, wouldn’t you rather just play Iroas himself? Rating: 4/10

Golgari

Mazirek, Kraul Death Priest:  So… I guess they didn’t care that this does really stupid, infinite things with Ghave, Guru of Spores and Ashnod’s Altar. This guy screams “Build around meeeee” in a high-pitched vibrato-laden insectoid voice.  Enjoy the broken interactions everyone!  This evasive commander is an easy candidate to lead a deck or play a role as part of a powerful engine in EDH’s two most powerful colors.  9/10.

Meren of Clan Nel Toth: Meren knows how to keep the party going with what I’m calling her: “Death Aether Vial” ability.  She is a deck engine on her own, and lends herself to an aggressive, attrition-based strategy.  Zombies, Elves, Relentless Rats, Solemn Simulacrums, and everything else can and will be abused with her ability.  Personally, I’m going to try her out in my Mimeoplasm Reanimator deck.  Rating: 8/10.

Izzet

Arjun of the Shifting Flame: I admire this creature simply because it has the ability to show you so many cards during a single game.  This is great for digging for answers, or for seeing more variety in your EDH games.  I wouldn’t say it’s ultra-competitive by any means though. Rating: 3/10.

Mizzix of the Izmagnus: The math of constructing a deck around Mizzix seems pretty daunting, but I bet it could be amazing if done correctly.  The first time I read it, I thought I got an experience counter for every instant or sorcery I cast, but then I re-read it and the card got considerably worse. That said, I’m sure that this deck could be constructed to outdraw many other decks.  What the end-game would be with this general… I’m unsure.  Perhaps Molten Psyche, Cerebral Vortex, and Psychosis Crawler with draw spells.  This is reminiscent of what I tried to do with Nin, the Pain Artist.  Rating: 5/10.

Orzhov

Daxos the Returned:  Given that Theros gave us so many interesting enchantments, Daxos will be quite easy to build around, and has some nice creature types included to further enable several viable deckbuilding directions. I have a feeling that Daxos will be quite popular. Rating: 7/10.

Karlov of the Ghost Council: Low-cost, aggressive legendaries are always popular.  Karlov will see play in EDH, and possibly in Legacy/Tiny Leaders.  Theres a little bit of extra price hype on him right now for that reason. His activated ability isn’t exactly cost effective, but it does kill everything, including gods and Eldrazis.  Rating: 7/10.

Simic

Kaesto, Orochi Archmage: Unblockable is not to be underestimated.  I have a feeling this guy will see play in a number of decks, even if he is not the general.  Vorel, Edric, Animar, Mimir Vig, and several others would do well to profit from his services.  Rating: 7/10.

Ezuri, Claw of Progress: A more complicated and less versatile version of Immaculate Magistrate, Ezuri has morphed from a thing of beauty (a 10/10 die-hard leader of renegades) into some sort of washed-up try-hard, has-been.  Not my idea of “progress.” Rating: 4/10.

 

Confluence Cycle:

Fiery Confluence: Red gets a little bit of versatility, but not too much power in its confluence.  This card is playable enough though.  I see the top and bottom mode being used the most.  If this were instant speed, I’d be much more excited about it.

Mystic Confluence: While this is missing the “tap all creatures” mode of Cryptic Command, This card is reminiscent of the aforementioned card.  Remember that you can choose the Mana Leak mode up to three times for the same spell.

Righteous Confluence: The Exile Enchantment mode is easily the most powerful part of this card.  I could see myself doing that once or twice then gaining five to ten most of the time.  I wish this card was an Instant, which would make the Knight mode much more relevant for producing surprise blockers.

Verdant Confluence:  Everything about this card seems fair as far as cost to effect ratio.  I wish it were an instant for the first mode, but that might make the second and third modes unfair.

Wretched Confluence: This card becomes more powerful once you realize that it’s an instant.  Often in Mono-Black or Golgari lists I’ll play various “Ambition’s Cost” cards: Pay 3 Draw 3, but most are sorcery speed.  The other effects are also things I want to do on opponent’s turns: removal, and Raise Dead.

Myriad Cycle

Banshee of the Dread Choir: I’m unsure why this isn’t a classic Hypnotic Specter (1BB 2/2 Flier).  As is, it seems fairly unplayable.

Broodbirth Viper: The Myriad Ophidian.  To be honest, it seems like a lot of hard work to draw a few cards with this snake, but I’m sure that players of Kaesto, Orochi Archmage will appreciate making it unblockable and drawing 1-4 cards each time it attacks.

Caller of the Pack:  Trample and Myriad seems to go well together.  This could see play in Mayael or another Beast-synergy deck.

Herald of the Host: Myraid Serra Angel is pretty interesting, if not incredibly threatening because of its vigilance ability.  It’s a true Planeswalker killer.

Warchief Giant: Haste and Myriad seem to be very compatible keywords, and I think this one could have some potential.  Red’s very good at inspiring violence and anger, which is what I’m sure will happen once you kill all of your opponents’ planeswalkers.

Blade of Selves:  Great News!  If you don’t like any of these Myriad creatures, make one of your own!

White

Bastion Protector:  The Tiny Leaders community is very excited about this card.  I’m hesitant to call it an auto-include in any non-human, non-soldier synergy EDH deck because of its narrow focus.  I’d much rather have a card like Ghostway, Rootborn Defenses, or Dauntless Escort that protects all of my creatures and is harder for opponents to play around.

Dawnbreak Reclaimer: Ahh politics!  The best case scenario: both players get what they want, worst case: both players get their worst (or only) creature, or somewhere in the middle, depending on player skill level and assessment of the board state.  Collusion could make this broken.  I’ll likely try and fit it in my already stacked Karador deck.

Grasp of Fate: Another wonderful Oblivion Ring effect to add to the collection!  A three-for-one should always be welcome in your arsenal.

Kalemne’s Captain: I can see why this giant was promoted to captain: an aggressive mana cost for a 5/5 body, vigilance, and an activated ability with a truly Luddite flair.  He may not fit in the traditional Voltron-style white equipment/enchantment decks, but my Marath token landfall deck may have a place for him, as might Mayael the Anima decks.

Oreskos Explorer: Land Tax Cat will see play, I’m sure of it!  The great news is that this will get your Dual, Shock, and Tango lands because it does not say “Basic” anywhere on the card.

Shielded by Faith: Uril the Miststalker players will be very interested in fancy new piece of armor.

Blue

Aethersnatch: Spelljacks, Misdirections, and Willbenders always make for an interesting turn of events.  Welcome to the party Aethersnatch!

Gigantoplasm: They don’t make Clones like they used to… They make them way better.

Illusory Ambusher:  What a great rattlesnake blocker! If you’ve been following along, you know Flash is good.

Mirror Match: I wish this only cost 1UU, as it seems to be reminiscent of sudden spoiling.  Despite its high cost, it certainly has the potential to be a blowout/comeback card for someone trying to end the game with an infinite attack.  It also has the added value of applying any Enter the Battlefield abilities your opponent’s attacking creatures have.  I can only imagine the horror if you have Purphuros in play and the opponent is attacking you with a swarm of tokens…

Synthetic Destiny: A new take on Mass Polymorph… Instant speed makes this card quite amazing, and its applications as a combo enabler with tokens, or its use as an anti-board wipe mean that this card will see play.  This is one of the more interesting cards in the set, to be sure.

Black

Corpse Augur: This is a great addition to Zombie decks looking for another Skeletal Scrying effect.  In fact, it might be worth cutting Skeletal Scrying for this card.

Daxos’s Torment: This could be a great addition to enchantment heavy Orzhov or Esper decks.  It’s initially worth the investment, and it has an upside if you can sustain it.  At worst, it’s +1 devotion if you fail to draw other enchantments.

Deadly Tempest:  Ah the black sweeper!  This will likely be an in-demand card from this set, as black sweepers are always popular.  As far as playability goes, it’s pretty high on the list if you are in a heavy elf-swarm/token meta, but it’s competing with Toxic Deluge, Decree of Pain, Overwhelming Forces, Damnation, Massacre Wurm, and many others.

Dread Summons: This is a very flavorful card, and may be quite functional if you can get your mono black or Golgari zombie mana going with Urborg/Cabal Coffers/Nykthos.

Scourge of Nel Toth: I’d be pretty happy to sac my Grave Titan’s zombie tokens or a couple of Thrulls to revive this guy.  A solid addition to the walking (flying) dead.

Thief of Blood: I was taken aback when I read this… At the cost of only three Vampire Hexmages I get a potentially giant flier that says “Destroy all Planeswalkers!”  This could be great in my meta, where “Super Friends” decks are quite common.  It’s also great vs. Marath, Animar, Ghave, Vish Kal, and those pesky Spike Weavers.  Imagine this with Doubling Season!

Red

Awaken the Sky Tyrant: I’d be a bit happier investing in this If it had two or three counters on it, and used those counters each time an opponent dealt damage.  Otherwise, it seems to be a very reactive card in a color that is usually known for being proactive.  It’s a flavorful design concept though.

Dream Pillager: Yet another dragon that fails to be 5/5.  Mayael decks cry out in sorrow.  High cost and a turn to set up put this dragon on the “B list” for me, but I’m sure die-hard dragon fans will enjoy the extra permanent or two he may deliver in the later turns.

Magus of the Wheel: This guy will see play in Tribal wizard decks, Mono-red decks, Nekusar decks, and many others.  Great art and a classic effect will make him one of the most sought-after cards in the set.

Meteor Blast: This is a semi-playable removal spell that will serve players of the precon well, but will likely get tossed aside when better options present themselves, such as Firestorm, Comet Storm or Starstorm.

Mizzix’s Mastery:  This is a great single-target spell, and when overloaded, potentially gamebreaking.  This will find a home in my Jeleva deck, and I’m sure that many other “spellslinger” decks will enjoy going crazy with this masterpiece.

Rite of the Raging Storm:  I really appreciate the politics and chaos of handing out ball lightning to everyone.  I doubt my opponents will.  This will fit nicely in decks like Norin the Wary that love to pit people against each other and force the issue.

Green

Arachnogenesis:  This might be called a green Sudden Spoiling, and is a very well-designed Fog variant.  The huge upside is that you get to keep the spiders, cast Craterhoof behemoth, and then swing back with them.  I can also imagine Ashnod’s Altar shenanigans ensuing as well.  This is a great piece of tech, and I have a feeling this card will be a sleeper hit.

Bloodspore Thrinax: This guy seems like an auto-include in decks like Ghave, Marath, or anything else that makes a bunch of tokens.

Centaur Vinecrasher: He’d fit well into last year’s Titania, Protector of Argoth Precon, as well as any deck that abuses Knight of the Reliquary.  His name implies that he should have Haste in addition to Trample, which would make this guy a bit more attractive.  I’d also like if the GG returned him directly to the battlefield.  I really don’t think either of those things would be too much to ask for in this format.

Ezuri’s Predation:  Green now has a Pesudo-Sweeper other than Hurricanes!  This is another potentially game-breaking card, considering that many of the beasts will survive, and potentially attack with a haste effect (Concordant Crossroads, Fires of Yavimaya)

Great Oak Guardian:  While target player will most likely always be yourself, this is another fun politics enabler, and a great Two-headed giant EDH variant card.

Pathbreaker Ibex: What a threatening card!  I’m sure that many Prophets of Kruphix will enable this evil goat to end games.  Otherwise the table will probably kill this with fire.

Skullwinder:  Another great political design.  This will see a ton of play, and it will test the skill of many players.  The best practice is to broker the deal that will work for you.  Use this as leverage to take down the most threatening player at the table, or to make an alliance with someone.  The later you play this, the more value it will have.

Artifacts

Sandstone Oracle: 7 Mana for a 4/4 with that ability is not what I want to do in any format.  I understand the concept, but this card is easily replaceable.

Scytheclaw: Quietus Spike, but on a living weapon.  I can dig it! So can Anya…

Seal of the Guildpact: This is a decent mana-rock, but not tier-one by any means.  It’s kind of sad to see that this is only in one of the five decklists.

Thought Vessel:  This IS a tier-one mana rock! I’d never play this over Mind Stone, but I’d always play this alongside Mind Stone if I have a slot.

Land:

Command Beacon:  This year’s Commander-Only land is great for high-cost commanders, but situationally poor for those like Marath, Derevi, Prosh, and Jeleva.  This is a simple and necessary effect that will hopefully encourage players to use some of the higher-cost commanders without fear of being priced out after a few board wipes.

Notable Reprints: Some older cards that usually retail from $4-10 are being reprinted in this set: Enjoy the value!  (Golgari seems to be the one to snatch up from Wal-Mart).

  • Phyrexian Arena (Orzhov)
  • Eternal Witness (Simic and Golgari)
