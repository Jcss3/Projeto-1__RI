It Takes 2 to Tango – Modern Remix

By Byron King
@DoneTheMath

 

This seems like a good time to take a break from the Modern Compendium.  PAX was last weekend, and we got our first big look at some of the new toys from Battle for Zendikar.  I want to talk about some of the immediate impact these will have for Modern.

Not this guy.  He’s a big dude, but he isn’t Emrakul big.  There might be some Modern applications, but we will set him aside for now.

 

Not these either, but they are absolutely stunning.  My desire to open booster packs has shot through the roof, but that isn’t the topic for today either.

 

Here we go.  This is something I’m ready to talk about.  The community seems to be fighting over the idea of calling them tango lands (since it takes two to tango).  It might not stick, but I’ll either refer to them as the new lands or the tango lands in this article.  My fallback was the CIPT duals (come into play tapped), but that doesn’t quite roll off the tongue.

In a vacuum, the tango lands are mediocre.  Needing two basics is an obnoxious constraint when designing a deck.  The buddy lands from previous core sets (the Glacial Fortress cycle) are quite a bit better in that regard, only needing one land and not specifying basic lands.  But that isn’t all these lands have to offer.  The strength of these lands comes from their land subtype, meaning the tango lands can be found with fetch lands.

That is huge

Before this, multicolor decks in Modern played with fetch lands and shock lands.  That was just the way of things.  The consistency they provided was phenomenal, and nothing could compete.  Until now.

The eternal formats have always had some kind of punishment in place for greedy mana.  Legacy and Vintage have Wasteland to police the lands.  Modern mana is held in check by the damage from Ravnica shock lands. If you wanted a deck with 4 colors, your mana would be coming into play tapped or dealing you a ton of damage.  Or it could be wildly inconsistent.

The real power of the tango lands is choice.  These give decks in Modern the option to not play with shock lands and still have great mana.  Even if they don’t completely change the format, the option exists.  That is a powerful choice, and I’m not sure I can repeat it enough.

The mana in Modern is already fantastic.  Honestly, it’s a little too good.  Players will often fetch for additional dual lands in the middle turns of a game, even if they don’t need the additional color.  Usually, this is looked at favorably, since it preemptively compensates for cards like Molten Rain, Spreading Seas, and the like.  Most decks could probably support a few more basics without a noticeable decrease in consistency.  To support the tango lands, the number of basic lands probably goes up by three or less.  Fetch lands serve double duty, artificially inflating the number of basic lands and providing ways to search for the tango lands.

The printing of the allied color fetch lands in Khans of Tarkir will prove to be a strong, but subtle boost for the tango lands.  Having dual lands that can fetch the correct basic lands will be necessary if the number of basic lands need to go up.  If Jund only had access to Verdant Catacomb for an on-color fetch land, playing enough basics would be impossible.  Between needing to cast Inquisition of Kozilek and Lightning Bolt on turn one, Bloodstained Mire would be critical.

 

The Influence on Existing Decks and Cards

Burn will noticeably suffer if these lands show up in a variety of decks. Zoo, Affinity, and other aggressive decks won’t appreciate them either.  Plenty of decks start at about 15 due to pain from the shock lands.  If those decks start closer to 18 life, that is another Lightning Bolt worth of damage aggressive decks will be required to produce.

The aggressive decks also can’t really utilize them.  The tango lands favor decks looking hit four or more lands.  Aggressive decks can usually function with about two lands, making them extremely poor for the new land cycle.  If the aggressive decks tried to run them, even in two or three color decks, things would go badly.  Wild Nacatl wouldn’t power up until turn three, Burn would struggle with casting Boros Charm and Eidolon of the Great Revel.  Affinity wants no part in this conversation whatsoever.  I had to dig pretty deep to find a semi aggressive deck that might be interested.  The Orzhov Token deck is around the threshold of how aggressive a deck can be while supporting the tango lands.  And there isn’t even an Orzhov tango land (yet?).

If the tango lands become popular, Blood Moon will become far worse against the average deck.  Simply having tango lands in your deck will encourage you to play around Blood Moon.  One of the reasons Blood Moon is so good against the average deck is that it requires a conscious effort to fetch basics.  If the common play pattern switches to finding basic lands for the first couple turns of the game, Blood Moon instantly becomes an awful card against a large swath of decks.

This one is a little less popular, but both Merfolk and Blue Moon could be poised to capitalize on an increase in basic lands.  Such an increase would make a single copy of Spreading Seas far more likely to deny a color of mana.  It is worth mentioning that the Island created by Spreading Seas will not count as a basic land for future tango lands.

Tectonic Edge has become dramatically less popularity since the rise of Amulet Bloom (in favor of Ghost Quarter).  That deck is quite capable of winning before Tectonic Edge can ever make a mark.  Still, the printing of tango lands is a mixed blessing for Tectonic Edge.  More basic lands means there are less dual lands for it to destroy.  However, those decks might have less stable mana, making a single activation more devastating.

I’m sure there are more implications to specific cards, but these were the first that came to mind.  Let me know what else you think of in the comments.

 

How Lands Could Change

There are three different options

  • Nothing changes
  • Decks incorporate a mixture of Ravnica shock lands and tango lands
  • Decks switch to exclusively tango lands

There isn’t really anything to discuss if nothing changes.  Life continues as normal.

Incorporating a mixture of shock lands and tango lands is more of a challenge.  My initial instinct is to play a single shock land of each color combination, then fill out the deck with tango lands.  Thinking about it a little more, that might not be a reasonable plan.  In Modern, every card needs to be squeezed for as much value as possible.  This includes the lands, which are often littered with Celestial Colonnades, Ghost Quarters, Gavony Townships, and more.  Deck designers have a pretty good idea of how many utility lands can fit in the various decks, and those slots really can’t be touched when replacing shock lands with tango lands.  If utility lands have to go to make room, the tango lands will be a significant amount worse than the shock lands, no matter the deck.  Fitting enough basic lands, fetch lands, and tango lands into a list will already be tight before even getting to shock lands.

The last possibility is simply removing all of the shock lands for tango lands.  This should avoid messing with utility lands, and the questions simplify to “can we support enough basics” and “is it worth the effort”.  Once again, this assumes there is enough room to fit in all the support without cutting utility lands.

 

Updating Decks

Working with a two color deck

Actually fitting the tango lands into current decks will be a little tricky.  I was several paragraphs into explaining how well they would fit into the Izzet Splinter Twin decks before I realized we didn’t have an Izzet tango land.  I really liked using that deck, since it is probably the best known deck in the format.  I’m going to have to try something a little different.

Blue White Control – Yuuya Watanabe’s Modern deck from Worlds 2015
To save you the click, the lands are
4 Ghost Quarter
4 Celestial Colonnade
4 Mystic Gate
4 Flooded Strand
3 Island
3 Plains
2 Hallowed Fountain
1 Minamo, School at Water’s Edge
1 Eiganjo Castle

 

The mana in these control decks has always left something to be desired.  Some versions eschew fetchlands altogether, using Seachrome Coast, Mystic Gate, and Glacial Fortress to fix colors.  This gives access to Suppression Field, but it certainly doesn’t inspire confidence in a deck looking to cast Kitchen Finks and Cryptic Command.  Other versions, like the one above, play a small set of fetch lands because they are  better than the alternative.  They fix colors just as reliably, along with giving the deck a fighting chance against Blood Moon.

This is the perfect kind of deck for the tango lands.  It is interested in playing a longer game without the damage from Ravnica shock lands, it already plays some undesirable dual lands, and it can support a high enough count of basic lands.

The easy switch here is to remove the Mystic Gates for Prairie Stream.  This is a minor upgrade, but every little bit counts.  Having a hand with just Mystic Gates is pretty much unplayable, as none of the lands can generate colored mana.  A hand with just Prairie Stream can be a little slow, but it can function without additional support from the top of the deck.

However, we can do better than just a small switch.
4 Ghost Quarter
4 Celestial Colonnade
3 Prairie Stream
4 Flooded Strand
2 Scalding Tarn
1 Arid Mesa
3 Island
2 Plains
1 Hallowed Fountain
1 Minamo, School at Water’s Edge
1 Eiganjo Castle

 

Once again, the changes are minor.  There are a few more fetch lands in this list, as they are important in setting up Prairie Stream.  Despite the lower number of basic lands, this list has a functional 12 basics instead of the original 10.  I haven’t gotten a chance to play with the new lands, but this seems like a more than enough to support the tango lands.  If this isn’t enough basics, the tango lands probably won’t make the cut in Modern.  The rest of the lands are utility lands, which help act as spells to avoid mana flooding.  If some of these have to start leaving to make room, any advantage provided by the tango lands will be overshadowed by the loss of the utility lands.

 

Working with a different two color deck

Another deck that could consider the tango lands would be the Gruul version of Scapeshift.  That deck is interested in playing basics, only runs two colors, and is highly interested in the number of mountains in the deck.  The most recent success this deck had was at GP Omaha

Gruul Scapeshift – Scott Lipp GP Omaha 2015
To save you the click, the lands are
7 Mountain
2 Forest
4 Stomping Ground
1 Blood Crypt
4 Wooded Foothill
4 Bloodstained Mire
3 Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle

 

Search for Tomorrow, Sakura-Tribe Elder, and Farseek all have synergy with the new tango lands.  The deck can easily add copies of Cinder Glade in place of some of the basic Mountains.  I’m going to shave one fetch land, because the deck occasionally runs out of Mountains to trigger Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle in an otherwise lethal Scapeshift.  The new manabase could look something like this

4 Mountain
2 Forest
4 Stomping Ground
1 Blood Crypt
4 Cinder Glade
4 Wooded Foothill
3 Bloodstained Mire
3 Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle

 

I’m not sure how consistent the Gruul Scapeshift deck was before Battle for Zendikar, but the new addition can only help.  Cinder Glade will definitely find a place in this list.

 

Working with a three color deck

Right now, it is pretty difficlut to update a three color deck with the tango lands.  With only the allied color tango lands available, the deck would need to choose the tango lands over the Mirrodin fast lands (such as Seachrome Coast).  Hopefully we get the rest of the cycle in the following set, but we can try to work with what we have now.

Finding a three color deck that was slow enough to consider the tango lands was a little more difficult.  Grixis Control seemed like the best place to start.

Grixis Control – Michael Majors SCG Modern Open in Charlotte 8-22-15
To save you the click, the lands are
2 Island
1 Mountain
2 Swamp
1 Steam Vents
1 Watery Grave
1 Blood Crypt
4 Polluted Delta
3 Scalding Tarn
2 Bloodstaine Mire
2 Creeping Tar Pit
3 Blackcleave Cliffs
1 Darkslick Shores

 

This list has Lightning Bolt, Inquisition of Kozilek, Serum Visions, and Spell Snare, meaning the mana is stretched pretty tight on the first turn of the game.   We probably have enough basics in the list already, so we won’t have to make room for more of those.  This list needs at least 1 shock land, and I really feel like it might need two.  I’m also at something of a loss as to which shock land should stay, so I’ll leave two of them for now.

2 Island
1 Mountain
2 Swamp
1 Steam Vents
1 Blood Crypt
4 Polluted Delta
3 Scalding Tarn
4 Bloodstaine Mire
2 Creeping Tar Pit
2 Sunken Hollow
1 Smoldering Marsh

 

I’m honestly not sure if this is any better than the original list.  Switching the fast lands for tango lands decreases the overall number of lands that produce mana (by adding additional fetch lands).  An increase in fetch lands is good for the delve spells, so it may be worth it.  I increased the number of Bloodstaine Mire over Scalding Tarn, since fetching a Mountain for Lightning Bolt is more important in aggressive matches where the life loss is the least acceptable.  The Steam Vents had to stay, because there is no replacement tango land.  I don’t really know if the Blood Crypt is better than a Watery Grave.  Overall, I’m only marginally happy with this list, as the fast lands look about equal in power level here.  Fetching for dual lands without pain might be important enough though.

 

Closing Thoughts

I started out by making a huge fuss about how important the new lands are, then I provided a couple lists of lands with some minor changes.  It probably wasn’t the most  fantastic thing you’ve ever read, but working on mana is never the most exciting part.  It’s hard to make the connection between the work now and a game months down the line where your Dragonlord Ojutai comes down on time with some painless mana.  The tango lands will cause a stir for a little while, even if they don’t work out.  The hierarchy for lands in Modern has been set in stone for years now, and anything capable of shaking that up is worth exploring.

Missing the enemy color tango lands is kind of a bummer, as Golgari and Izzet are two of the most popular color combinations in Modern.  For now, the tango lands might just be worse than the fast lands already available to us.  Testing now will give a lot of insight into how good a possible printing of the enemy tango lands could be.

I’m not sure I went into enough detail on the decks that will even be able to consider the tango lands.  Aggressive decks are the least concerned with damage from their own shock lands, and fetching dual lands on the first two turns of the game will often be necessary.  Combo decks are also likely to steer clear of the tango lands, as they often want to end the game before any true value can be gained from removing shock lands.  Almost all of the combo decks use special lands (Amulet Bloom, Tron) or don’t try to reach four mana (Infect, Storm, Hexproof, etc…).  The decks primed to take advantage of the tango lands are slower midrange decks (Jund/Abzan), or control decks (Grixis Control, U/W Control).  Most of these will likely be waiting on potential enemy tango lands before making the switch.  But maybe not.  Having a less painful manabase could be worth the effort now.  The most important thing is that we now have a choice.

If you have any other thoughts on the new lands, I’d love to hear them.  Feel free to post in the comments.  If you would rather, you can find me on twitter at @DoneTheMath
