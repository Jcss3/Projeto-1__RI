The Modern Compendium – Part 1

By Byron King
 @DoneTheMath
 
 
The Modern format consists of any card printed since Eighth edition and the original Mirrodin block. That means that almost any card printed since July 2003 is legal. Modern consists of several decks that were powerhouses during their respective Standard formats. Other decks have been created by exploring interactions between cards that were never possible during a Standard season. Still other decks are adaptations of Legacy decks that have been modified for the Modern format. All this leads to a format with a huge selection of decks.
 
The goal of The Modern Compendium is to present the different decks in the format. There is a brief explanation of how the decks function, along with some strengths and weaknesses. This is NOT an exhaustive explanation of each deck, as I don’t have the experience to provide that for every deck in the format. The objective is to provide information about as many decks as possible, preventing misconceptions and simple blunders when an opponent plays the deck for the first time.
 
The data used to identify the decks comes from mtgtop8.com. The data on that website is generated from recent large events, along with MTGO data. It is a fairly accurate representation of the metagame, and suitable for the goals of this compilation.

These articles will be in five deck increments. If the popularity of a deck plummets or skyrockets, the list may be slightly altered.

Twin

Metagame Percentage: 11%
Deck Category: Combo
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview:
Twin has been one of the most popular combo decks since the creation of the Modern format. It is a fairly simple combo, as either Splinter Twin or Kiki-Jiki, the Mirror Breaker can make infinite copies of Deceiver Exarch or Pestermite. The shell that surrounds this combo can be rather fluid, as the combo only needs a small number of cards to operate.
 
Game Plan
Pair either Deceiver Exarch or Pestermite with Splinter Twin or Kiki-Jiki, the Mirror Breaker. That’s the primary gameplan. Without any splash colors, the deck often functions as a control deck until it executes the combo. It can be a versatile Snapcaster Mage deck, as it often runs Lightning Bolts, Remands, Serum Visions, Gitaxian Probes, and more. Additional colors often promote a more aggressive alternate plan, as Tarmogoyf or Tasigur, the Golden Fang are capable of winning a game unaided.
 
Strengths
Twin has a lot of strengths, but the degree to which it can be customized is probably the biggest. The full combo takes only 8-16 slots in a deck, so the rest of the deck can be altered to the pilot’s choice. Some versions run a heavy control package to lock up the game until the combo is found. Others use the maximum number of combo pieces and cards like Slight of Hand to assemble the win as fast as possible. Another one of Twin’s advantages is simply the threat of the combo. Due to both creatures having Flash, passing the turn with three open mana is enough to threaten the combo. Opponents must be prepared with a removal spell, or they may not get another turn. This leads to Twin’s next strength, the ability to win on the fourth turn of the game. Modern is considered a turn four format, with any consistently faster combos being banned out of existance. Twin pushes this to the limit. Lastly, Twin has a huge advantage in sideboarding. Opponents are forced to bring in cards like Rending Volley and Torpor Orb, while the Twin player can simply board out the combo in favor of a different plan. The threat of the combo is enough to warrant sideboard hate cards, even if the targets no longer exist.
 
Weaknesses
Twin is a well known combo deck, and nobody will be caught off guard. Anyone who has put time into practicing Modern will know how the deck works and have decent practice against it. Any Modern testing gauntlet will feature Twin. Anyone who watches Modern coverage will have seen it in action. Opponents will have both practice and a solid plan against Twin, and the hate cards exist to make executing the combo challenging. In more practical terms, the deck can struggle against discard backed by a fast clock, which the Junk deck is capable of assembling. Tarmogoyf can be a problem if it resolves, because red and blue cannot easily kill high toughness creatures. Fast aggro and combo decks can sometimes pose problems too, as they put pressure on the Twin player of having the combo rather quickly. Because Twin lacks a strong card to dig for answers or combo pieces, it can lose to simply not finding the necessary cards.
 
Variations
The different variations of Twin are endless, as the name only refers to roughly twelve cards in the deck. The deck often splashes colors to shore up weaknesses. Black is one of the more prominent splashes currently, as it can add Thoughtseize, Inquisition of Kozilek, Kolaghan’s Command, Terminate, Murderous Cut, Gurmag Angler, and/or Tasigur, the Golden Fang. Green has dropped in popularity since the printing of Tasigur, the Golden Fang, but it still provides Tarmogoyf, with both Scavenging Ooze and Thrun, the Last Troll making appearances. White is the least popular splash, but it can add Path to Exile, Wall of Omens, Restoration Angel, Celestial Colonnade, and a bevy of sideboarding options.

Affinity

Metagame Percentage: 9%
Deck Category: Aggro
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview
Affinity is the result of several extremely powerful spells being printed during the original Mirrodin block. Cranial Plating and Arcbound Ravager are strong enough to warrant an entire deck, and additional artifacts have been printed over the years to provide a synergistic shell for them. Affinity is a premier aggressive strategy, with its most powerful cards providing burst damage that can reach double digits.
 
Game Plan
Affinity’s goal is to quickly deploy its resources to maximize the power of its payoff cards. Cranial Plating, Arcbound Ravager, Master of Etherium, and Etched Champion rely on a supporting cast of cheap artifacts to maximize their potential. Most of the power cards in Affinity are capable of winning the game singlehandedly, but there is a backup plan of swarming an opponent with cheap, evasive threats. The swarm approach often relies on one or more Signal Pests to provide enough damage. This is never the main plan for Affinity though, and the deck usually mulligans to one of the power cards.
 
Strengths
This keeps coming up, but Cranial Plating and Arcbound Ravager are not remotely fair Magic cards. Affinity gets to use several of the most powerful cards available in Modern, and is one of the most powerful aggressive decks available. The deck is capable of racing all but the fastest combo decks, and has the resiliency necessary to win longer games. Mox Opal and Springleaf Drum provide mana acceleration, often allowing Affinity to deploy its entire hand within the first couple turns. Affinity is the only deck in the format capable of utilizing a set of both Blinkmoth Nexus and Inkmoth Nexus, allowing the manabase to add to the onslaught of creatures.
 
Weaknesses
The hate cards available against Affinity are both powerful and prevalent. Affinity is a popular deck in Modern, and it is rare to find someone without sideboard cards dedicated to the matchup. Shatterstorm, Creeping Corrosion, Ancient Grudge, and Stony Silence are all backbreaking cards, often completely halting Affinity’s aggression. On a different axis, the two most powerful cards in the deck are both countered by Spell Snare. Tarmogoyf is also somewhat problematic, as artifacts will commonly pump it to even higher stats than it usual. Affinity can also struggle with combo decks that it cannot race, as it only has a small amount of room for disruption.
 
Variations
Affinity is built with the intent to splash one or two colors with relative ease, and each color adds some additional benefit. Blue is the most common, as it adds Thoughtcast, Master of Etherium, and Spell Pierce. Red provides additional reach in Galvanic Blast and Shrapnel Blast. Black offers Thoughtseize. Splashing both blue and black allows for Tezzeret, Agent of Bolas. White is aless common splash, but it offers Dispatch and Tempered Steel. Green does not have much to offer for synergy, but a rare Tarmogoyf will make an appearance.

Burn

Metagame Percentage: 7%
Deck Category: Aggro/Combo
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview
Sometimes referred to as a simple or little kid deck, Burn’s plan is easy to understand and brutally efficient. Burn wants to kill the opponent as fast as possible. Only the most efficient spells make the cut, attempting to win before the opponent ever gets a chance to stabilize. Defense is a secondary concern, only considered as a way to buy additional turns to deal more damage.
 
Game Plan
Can you count to 20? The only game plan with Burn is to reduce the opponent’s life from 20 to zero as fast as possible. This is achieved with a suite of extremely aggressive creatures that deal as much damage as possible until the opponent presents resistance. Direct damage spells are used to finish off the opponent. The deck run far more than 4 Lightning Bolts if it was an option, but subpar spells are necessary to fill out the deck. The most efficient spells have been identified and become standard, with deck alterations limited mostly to the number of creatures and the mana curve. Overall, Burn needs to resolve about seven spells to win the game.
 
Strengths
Some decks are simply not designed to handle the onslaught that Burn brings to the table. Game one is almost always favorable, and other decks rely on sideboard cards to improve the matchup. The manabase in Modern is also one of Burn’s strongest allies. In Legacy, players are punished for running multicolor decks by the presence of Wasteland. In Modern, Wasteland is replaced by the damage caused by the Ravnica shocklands. Getting untapped dual lands from a fetchland in Modern is a free Lightning Bolt, which Burn is happy to capitalize on. Burn also thrives on the difficulty in interacting with its individual cards. The creatures are all extremely cheap, and will often get in one or more attacks before dying. The burn spells are all roughly interchangeable, and the deck simply wins if it can resolve a critical number of them. A less noticeable advantage of Burn is how quickly it completes matches, either winning or losing. Shorter games will prevent fatigue and provide time to eat between matches.
 
Weaknesses
The hate cards exist, and they are brutal. Most Standard formats have some cards available to fight against aggressive red decks, and Modern gets to pick the best. Kor Firewalker, Timely Reinforcements, Leyline of Sanctity, and more are all huge problems, often requiring multiple cards to simply break even. Fast combo decks also present a problem. While Burn is capable of quickly winning a game, some combo decks can simply outrace it. Burn cannot heavily sideboard, due to the necessity of drawing about seven business spells to win the game. Diluting the deck with too many sideboard cards will slow Burn down dramatically, and it is designed to thrive in the beginning turns of the game.
 
Variations
The stock Burn lists play red with a white splash for Boros Charm and some sideboard cards. Green is a fairly common splash, allowing for Atarka’s Command and Destructive Revelry. Blue was a common splash color when Treasure Cruise was legal, but there are few incentives to splash blue in the current metagame. Black offers Bump in the Night and Self-Inflicted Wound, but it this splash is extremely rare. If sufficient colors are splashed, Tribal Flames also becomes a consideration.

Delver

Metagame Percentage: 7%
Deck Category: Tempo
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview
Delver variants have been a second tier option for several years. The printing of Treasure Cruise propelled Delver’s power level into the stratosphere, making it the de facto best deck for several months. After the banning of Treasure Cruise, Delver faded somewhat in popularity until people began incorporating other delve spells. The deck is now commonly Grixis, with the addition of Tasigur, the Golden Fang, Gurmag Angler, and a suite of cheap spells.
 
Game Plan
Delver is a tempo deck, aiming to resolve an early threat and then disrupt the opponent long enough to win the game. The deck has a suite of cheap creatures capable singlehandedly winning a game. Delver runs a large suite of spells to both fuel it’s creatures and to provide disruption. The deck utilizes discard spells in Thoughtseize and Inquisition of Kozilek, along with counterspells in Spell Snare, Spell Pierce, and Remand. Serum Visions and Thought Scour provide cheap card draw and fuel for the Delve spells and Young Pyromancer.
 
Strengths
Combo decks are one of the best reasons to play Delver. It is capable of generating a fast clock, and is filled to the gills with cards to interact against combo. Most of Delver’s threats can win the game unaided, and the deck has multiple burn spells capable of increasing the clock. Delver is better suited to beat unfavorable matchups than midrange decks like Junk, because Delver’s threats are generally better at quickly killing an opponent. There are few decks in the format that are better at maximizing Snapcaster Mage’s potential. The Grixis Delver lists also gain access to Kolaghan’s Command, which provides a lot of versatility, particularly when combined with Snapcaster Mage.
 
Weaknesses
Delver struggles when playing from behind. The deck needs to deploy an early threat to apply pressure before interacting with the opponent. If the first creature fails to materialize or is quickly dispatched, Delver can flounder until the opponent has sufficient time to rebuild. This is true of all Temp decks, but the cantrips in Modern exacerbate the problem. Finding a way to close out a game can be extremely difficult. Also, Delver decks that do not play black will struggle against high toughness creatures. Izzet Delver really only has burn spells, Vapor Snag, and Dismember to deal with a creature already on the battlefield.
Variations
Grixis is the primary Delver deck at the moment, but an Izzet configuration still exists. The Izzet decks are often more aggressive, as the best creatures available have an aggressive slant. Expect to see Monastery Swiftspears and Goblin Guides backed by an increase in burn spells. Temur Delver decks also exist, but they are far less common. Temur only really gains Tarmogoyf for the splash, without the discard and removal offered in Grixis.

Jund

Metagame Percentage: 7%
Deck Category: Midrange
Sample Decklist Link
 
Overview
Jund has a long pedigree in Modern. Both Bloodbraid Elf and Deathrite Shaman were banned due to the prevelance of Jund in the format. Banning Deathrite Shaman did noticeable damage to the deck, and it started to fade into the background. Junk became a more popular option, with a different suite of powerful sideboard cards and a replacement for Bloodbraid Elf in Seige Rhino. Jund has just started to make a resurgence in the format off of the power of Kolaghan’s Command.
 
Game Plan
This deck relies on the raw power level of its cards, eschewing synergy almost completely. The black discard spells Thoughtseize and Inquisition of Kozilek provide early protection from fast combo decks. Abrupt Decay is one of the most versatile removal spells available, capable of removing anything from an opposing Tarmogoyf to Cranial Plating to Pyromancer’s Ascension. Terminate provides an answer t any creature that Abrupt Decay would miss. Tarmogoyf, Scavanging Ooze, and Dark Confidant provide ample power to stabilize a board and finish a game.
 
Strengths
Jund’s matchups are almost never worse than 40%. Players who want to play competitive games of Magic will not be disappointed with Jund. The deck aims to reduce both players’ resources to nothing, and then see who can win first. Jund is built to maximize its advantage in these games, with cheap, powerful threats and a high contingency of utility lands. The individual power level of each card in Jund is astronomically high. Each removal spell is capable of handling almost any threat. Each creature is capable of singlehandedly winning the game in short order. There are options and available space to customize Jund against any predicted metagame. Jund also makes good use of Kolaghan’s Command, as it is a flexible source of card advantage.
 
 
Weaknesses
There are too many combo decks in the format and too few sideboard slots available for Jund to have a good matchup versus every deck. Whatever matchup gets ignored will usually be an unfavorable matchup. Beyond that, both Burn and the Tron decks are a poor matchup without excessive sideboarding. Even with proper sideboarding, Jund will never be a huge favorite against any given deck. That’s just how the deck is designed.
 
Jund’s matchups are almost never better than 60%. Even when Jund is operating properly, the opponent can still string together a series of cards to win the game. There are also some strategies in Modern that Jund is ill-equipped to fight. Decks like Tron are full of cards that Jund cannot favorably interact with, and heavy sideboarding is necessary to improve these matchups. Jund also struggles with the size limitation for sideboards. There are copious options to fight against the strategies in Modern, but it’s impossible to have room for them all. Improving a matchup against a certain deck could take 5-6 cards in a sideboard, but those sideboard spots could be used to improve multiple other matchups. Often, a Jund player will have to make concessions against certain decks, and will then rely on avoiding them in a tournament.
 
Variations
At this point, the Junk deck is a variation of Jund.
