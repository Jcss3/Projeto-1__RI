    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;line-height:200%'>The Complete Beginner’s Guide to
Magic Online</span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Welcome! If
        you’re a Magic player thinking about taking the Magic Online plunge, look no
        further. My name is Chas Andres, and I’ve been playing Magic—both online and
        off—for the better part of two decades. My goal in writing this guide is to
        demystify the Magic Online experience. From your initial download through your
        first tournament victory, I’ve got you covered.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Let’s not get into
        the technical stuff quite yet, though. I’d rather start with the basics. First
        of all:</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>&nbsp;</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal'><b><span style='font-size:20.0pt'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="whyplay">Why Play Magic Online?</a></span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Quick—what’s your
        biggest problem with Magic?</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Mine is this: I
        don’t get to play nearly as much as I want to! My local shop only hosts two booster
        drafts a week, and I have work one of those nights. I have a playgroup I can
        rally to the cause sometimes, but we don’t get to meet every week. That’s six
        or seven matches of a week if I’m lucky–not nearly enough.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Magic Online is
        the best answer to that problem. Love Standard? Dying to draft? Want to master
        Modern? Whatever your preferred format is, you can log onto Magic Online right
        now and find a game within minutes. You can play Legacy at 2 AM. You can battle
        Standard in your pajamas, or with friends shouting at the screen over your
        shoulder, or while sipping an ice cold drink. You can play
        Vintage—Vintage!!—despite the fact that no one you know actually owns a Black
        Lotus. Don’t like your draft deck? You don’t have to wait until next Friday
        night to play again—just join another queue. Want to figure out how G/W Midrange
        beats U/B Control? You can run that match-up again, and again, and again, and
        again, until you get it right.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Many professional
        Magic players know that this is the secret to leveling up your abilities. I’ve
        been part of the Magic community all my adult life, and I can’t count how many
        times I’ve heard a pro player tell me that they only started getting really
        good once they started playing a lot online. The best way to master Magic is to
        play as much as you can. Magic Online enables this in a way that nothing else
        does.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>There has been a
        lot of digital ink spilled about the problems with Magic Online, and many of these
        complaints do have merit. The interface is lackluster compared to the beauty of
        Hearthstone, you’ll encounter bugged cards from time to time, and there will be
        days when the program crashes on you during a crucial turn. Things aren’t as
        bad as some of the nay-sayers claim, but these problems will frustrate you if
        you play a significant amount of Magic Online.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Here’s the thing,
        though—all those ugly bugs? They’re small potatoes when compared to the benefit
        of getting to play as much Magic as you want, whenever you want. It’s the best
        game ever created, and it’s at your fingertips 24/7. </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>&nbsp;</p>
    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal'><b><span style='font-size:20.0pt'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="intro">Intro to MTGO</a></span></b></p>
    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>If you prefer, The Professor does a great job of introducing MTGO to newer players. Check out the video below!</p>
    <iframe width="942" height="530" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/j4acqXA6iWU" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe>
    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>&nbsp;</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal'><b><span style='font-size:20.0pt'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="whatis">Okay, So What Exactly
*IS* Magic Online?</a></span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>There are two
        different Magic: the Gathering computer programs, and it’s worth taking a
        moment to talk about the difference between them. </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>In addition to <b>Magic
            Online</b>, there is also a game called <b>Magic Duels</b> (formerly Duels of
        the Planeswalkers) that features digital Magic gameplay. If you’ve visited the
        Wizards of the Coast booth at a gaming convention or you’ve downloaded
        something Magic-related on Steam or XBOX Live, you’ve probably experienced some
        iteration of Magic Duels, NOT Magic Online.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'><b>Duels</b> is a
        beautiful game. It has a modern, intuitive interface. You can play it on your
        iPhone, your PC, or your XBOX. There’s great story content, unlockable cards,
        and different AI bosses you can play against. If you’re a casual Magic player,
        I highly recommend it.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>The problem is
        that Duels isn’t quite Magic. Because our favorite game was created back in
        1993, creator Richard Garfield didn’t think about how difficult it would be to
        program Magic’s rules and interactions into a digital interface. Duels cuts the
        rulebook to shreds, simplifying the game to the point where you can play it on
        your smartphone. If you’re a serious Magic player, you’ll feel constrained by
        what Duels leaves out. There are no formats. The available card pool is small. Your
        ability to build interesting decks is very limited.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'><b>Magic Online (abbreviated
            MTGO, or sometimes MODO)</b> has the opposite problem. The game’s purpose is to
        replicate paper Magic as faithfully as possible, and for the most part it succeeds.
        Barring some Un-set shenanigans, there isn’t anything you can do in a game of paper
        Magic that you can’t do on Magic Online.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>There are
        trade-offs for this level of control, though. Because Magic Online has to
        replicate the complex priority-passing experience of high level Magic, the
        program is currently limited to use on the <b>Windows PC</b>—there are no mobile
        or console versions of the program. They’ve never gotten around to building a
        Mac OSX version, either. </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>MTGO requires a
        reasonably beefy system to run, too—you need at least a Pentium 4 processor (2
        GHz or better), at least 1 GB of memory, at least 12 MB of video RAM, at least
        650 MB of disc space, a broadband internet connection, and Windows Vista or
        newer. If all these words are technobabble nonsense to you, use your best judgment.
        A PC bought in the past five years shouldn’t have any issues whatsoever. A
        cheap or older netbook can run Magic Online as long as you don’t have anything
        else running at the same time. That creaky, ten-year-old XP tower that went in
        your closet after you got a MacBook for your birthday? Not so much. If you find
        that MTGO is constantly crashing and lagging while you follow this guide, the
        problem might lie with your low end hardware.  </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Beyond that, the
        biggest difference between Magic Duels and Magic Online is that <b>cards and booster
            packs in Magic Online are treated like their paper Magic counterparts.</b>
        That’s right—a Magic Online booster pack has the same $3.99 MSRP as a pack down
        at your local shop, and you can’t just sign up for an account that contains
        four copies of every card. If you want a good Standard deck, you have to buy or
        trade for the singles just like in paper Magic. Prerelease tournaments and
        drafts cost about the same online as they do at your local shop. Magic Online
        is cheap to buy—you can get started for just $10—but it costs a lot to play.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>This is a stumbling
        block for a lot of people, and I get it. If you already have an extensive Magic
        collection in paper form, do you really have to re-buy everything in digital
        form? </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Well, uh, yeah. But
        the good news is that most digital cards are cheaper than their paper analogues.
        And there are ways to redeem sets, allowing you to give up your digital copies
        in exchange for paper cards that show up in the mail from Wizards of the Coast.
        (This isn’t just a theoretical proposition, either—I redeem sets all the time.)
        Even if you don’t want to do this, Magic Online’s cards still have real value.
        There are reputable companies out there that will pay you actual money for your
        digital objects. Paying real money for digital objects feels weird, but there’s
        no realistic risk of your Magic Online investment becoming worthless overnight.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>We’ll dive into the
        economic stuff a little bit later, but for now all I need you to grasp is the
        idea that digital cards are roughly equivalent to their paper counterparts. I
        like to imagine that Wizards of the Coast shrunk down a bunch of real cards and
        converted them into software like in <i>Tron</i>.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>&nbsp;</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>As you might imagine,

            of the Coast website</a>.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>The initial

        serious server power dedicated to letting you get started quickly. I had the
        whole thing up and running within about two minutes on my mediocre cable

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="collection">Collection Management &amp; Deck
Building</a></span></b></p>

    <br>
    <br>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Your Magic Online
        <b>collection</b> is divided into two sections: <b>Cards</b> and <b>Other
            Products</b>. </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>It’s pretty
        simple—all your cards (including basic lands) are sorted into one digital bin,
        and everything else—boosters, tickets, tokens, points, whatever—ends up in
        another. </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Let’s start by
        taking a look at our <b>cards</b>. The first thing you’ll notice is that we can
        <b>sort them all by</b> <b>format</b>. You might notice a few unusual
        choices—we’ll discuss online-only casual formats like Momir Basic and Freeform
        Vanguard a bit later—but the idea is intuitive enough. Press the little radio
        button next to “Modern,” for example, and only Modern-legal cards will show up.
    </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Above that,
        there’s a search feature that will let you modify your search even further.
        Type in “tap” and now you’re only seeing Modern-legal cards that contain the
        word “tap.” Type in “Cryptic Command” and now you’re only seeing Modern-legal cards
        that contain the word “Cryptic Command.” (Hint: there’s only one, and we don’t
        have a copy in our collection yet.)</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal'><img border=0 width=278 height=425 id="Picture 7"
                         src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image005.jpg"></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>The important thing to remember here is that <b>you are
                only searching cards you actually own.</b> So unless you actually have an
online copy of Cryptic Command, your Cryptic Command search will leave you
empty-handed.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>There are also search tabs that let you search your
collection based on <b>Format, Set, Color, Type, Subtype, Converted Mana Cost,
                Power, Toughness, and Rarity.</b> And the search tools stack—if you want to
look up Standard-legal blue rares with a toughness greater than two and a
converted mana cost no greater than five, you can.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=279 height=478 id="Picture 8"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image006.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>The cards that meet your search criteria (or, if you
don’t have search criteria, your entire collection) will show up in pictorial
form on the right side of your screen. Above the cards are a few more menus
that let you control how the cards are displayed. You can <b>increase or
                decrease size</b> with the little slider on the right. You can <b>control how
                the cards are sorted</b> with the “Sort” tab. With the “Versions” tab, you can
the program whether or not you want Magic Online to<b> group promotional cards,
                foils, and different versions in the same pile. </b>On the left, you can choose
whether or not you want <b>a pop-out “Preview Pane”</b> that you can move
around your desktop.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=624 height=390 id="Picture 9"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image007.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>The most interesting feature here is the “Quantity” tab.
Normally, it is set between 1 and 20+, which shows you all the cards in your
collection. If you set the slider back to zero, however, it will show you <b>shadow
                versions of all cards that exist on Magic Online</b>—regardless of whether or
not you own them. Here, for example, are some of the Zendikar Expeditions as
seen from my brand new account:</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=624 height=452 id="Picture 10"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image008.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>This is especially useful when building new decks,
because it allows you to fiddle around with whatever list you’d like even if
you don’t currently own the necessary cards.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Speaking of deck building, let’s get down to it! Down at
the bottom of your screen, you’ll see an “Add Deck” button. Click it, and the
Add Deck interface pops up:</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=624 height=457 id="Picture 11"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image009.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>From here, you can either <b>import a deck</b> (many
Magic websites, including WotC’s own, have decklist files you can download) or <b>create
                a new deck.</b> You can name your deck, select the format, and even pick a
nifty little deck box from a pre-selected series of pictures. Since I’m making
a U/G deck and none of the pictures feature either the Simic guild symbol or
Kiora, I’m going with a generic planeswalker deck box for now. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>After creating your new deck, Magic Online will open up a
pane at the bottom of your screen. You can add cards to your deck by either <b>double-clicking</b>
or <b>clicking and dragging</b> from your collection. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>If you add a card that you don’t currently own, little
yellow caution stripes appear on its black border</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'>. You can save the deck, but you can’t play with it until
you acquire a copy of the missing card.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Otherwise, you can sort your deck in much the same way
you can sort your collection—by name, color, rarity, or (most helpfully)
converted mana cost. Throw in some lands and you’re good to go.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Well, you’re good to go as long as you’re okay losing a
whole bunch. I wasn’t able to build the sexiest Standard deck out of the meager
collection Magic Online gave me to start with, but I tried my best:</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image"
            style='color:#222222;text-align:center;'><img border=0 width=625 height=298 id="Picture 12"
                                       src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image010.jpg"></span><span
            style='color:#222222'> </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>And that’s it! <b>Magic Online auto-saves all your decks</b>,
so you don’t have to worry about forgetting to hit a save button. All your
decks will be listed under the “Decks &amp; Binders” tab on the Collection page
unless you delete them (which you can do by right-clicking on a deck name and
selecting the “delete” option). </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Want to <b>export your deck?</b> Just <b>right-click it</b>
and select “Export.” You can save decks as a Magic Online deck file (.dek), a
text file (.txt), or a spreadsheet (.csv). </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="firstgame">Playing Your First Game</a></span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>I’m all for theory, but the best way to learn the Magic
Online interface is by jumping right into a game. Click on the <b>PLAY LOBBY</b>
header  and use the filters on the left side of the screen to select <b>Constructed
                Open Play</b> and <b>Just Starting Out</b>. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=335 height=343 id="Picture 20"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image011.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Even if you’ve been playing Magic for many years, start here—your
opponent will be much more understanding about the missclicks and interface
mistakes you’re inevitably going to make if you join a beginner game. Also, I
doubt my U/G monstrosity can handle an Abzan Midrange matchup just yet. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Once you select your deck, click the big button marked <b>PLAY</b>
on the bottom left of the screen and Magic Online will automatically match you
with a worthy opponent. Finally—let’s go!</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image"
            style='color:#222222'><img border=0 width=624 height=380 id="Picture 21"
                                       src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image012.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Once you join a game, you will finally come face to face
with Magic Online’s <b>game play interface</b>. There’s a window where your
hand will appear in just a moment, a battlefield, a chat screen, two life
totals, two graveyards, and a little row that spells out all the phases of a
turn.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>This screen is a tad busy for my tastes. If you’d like,
you can <b>hide the chat/game log</b> by pressing the little black ‘X’ next to
where it says ‘CHAT,’ allowing you to have a slightly bigger battlefield. You
can also <b>make the graveyards smaller</b> by pressing the little red X’s on
the graveyard panel. This will pop them back under each player’s avatar and
life total, freeing up yet more battlefield space. You can easily pop them back
out if you’re playing a deck with reanimation spells or flashback cards.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>After you’ve finished adjusting your interface, you’ll
notice that the first thing that the program does is simulate the die roll that
determines who gets to choose whether to play or draw. <b>MTGO also</b> <b>starts
                up your chess clock</b>—each player has one, and it is set to twenty-five
minutes per player per match. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>This is one of the biggest differences between paper
Magic and the online version—instead of each round having an overall time limit
that ends with players taking extra turns, you each have twenty-five minutes to
use how you wish. Be careful: <b>if your timer goes all the way down to zero, you
                lose the match</b> no matter what else has happened!</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>While this seems like a major departure from paper Magic,
the vast majority of games don’t involve the clock one way or the other. It
mostly acts as a way of keeping someone from making you wait around for fifteen
minutes while they run down to the store for a can of soda. Play at your normal
speed for now and you should be fine.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=440 height=368 id="Picture 23"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image013.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>If you take a mulligan, MTGO will automatically set up
your free scry once you find a hand to keep. Like most things in Magic Online, <b>right
                click on the card to open up a menu of choices.</b> In this case, I right-clicked
and then selected “Put on the bottom of your library” because I didn’t want to
draw a land off my scry.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image"
            style='color:#222222'> <img border=0 width=624 height=172 id="Picture 22"
                                        src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image014.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Once you find a hand you can keep, it shows up in the
window at the bottom of your screen. Want your hand to look bigger? Smaller?
You can click on the top bar and adjust it just like the size of a browser
window. Want to play your land for the turn? As long as it’s your main phase,
just double-click it in your hand and it’ll pop right on over to the
battlefield. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Once you’re ready to play a card with a mana cost, you’ve
got two choices—either <b>tap the lands you want to tap and then double-click
                the card in your hand</b>, or <b>double-click the card first and then tap the
                lands you want to tap when prompted</b>. Either way, the result is the same—your
spell shows up on the stack, giving your opponent a chance to respond. If they
don’t cast a counterspell, your card will automatically resolve.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.25in;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span class="center-image" style='color:#222222'><img border=0 width=624
                                                   height=410 id="Picture 25"
                                                   src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image015.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>If you have a creature in play that is capable of
attacking, <b>a red zone will open up</b> in the center of the screen at the
beginning of your attack phase. Just click on the creature(s) you want to
attack with, and it will be nudged forward to indicate an attack. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image"
            style='color:#222222'><img border=0 width=624 height=263 id="Picture 28"
                                       src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image016.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>After attackers have been declared, <b>your triggered
                abilities (if any) will automatically be placed on the stack.</b></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=624 height=407 id="Picture 29"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image017.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>If a spell or ability has a target, (a removal spell,
say, or the Separatist Voidmage below) Magic Online will prompt you to place it
onto the stack by selecting a target. This is simple enough—<b>just click on
                the creature(s) or player(s) you’d like to target with your spell or ability. </b>In
this case, I used the Voidmage to bounce my opponent’s Monastery Swiftspear by
clicking on my opponent’s only creature. Taste it!</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image"
            style='color:#222222'><img border=0 width=624 height=325 id="Picture 30"
                                       src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image018.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Dead on board? Had enough? Whatever your reasons, right-clicking
on the battlefield opens up a menu that allows you to <b>concede the game.</b>
This is especially useful when facing down a game-winning infinite combo—while
some players make their opponents play out every loop in the combo to
demonstrate proficiency or in order to try and force a miss-click, most MTGO
players consider it sporting to just offer a concession in the face of a clear loss.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=282 height=275 id="Picture 32"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image019.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Want to <b>watch a replay</b> of your game? Check the <b>Game
                History</b> tab under the <b>ACCOUNT</b> menu. You’ll be able to see exactly
when and where things went wrong.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="priority">Priority and Keyboard Shortcuts</a></span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image"
            style='color:#222222'><img border=0 width=624 height=18 id="Picture 24"
                                       src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image020.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>This little bar is what MTGO uses to represent the flow
of a Magic game. If you’re an analytical thinker, you probably know these
phases by heart—untap, upkeep, draw, etc. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>If your preferred learning style is a little more
holistic, however, some of these terms might unfamiliar. The first thing to
know about phases is <b>Magic is a game of priority.</b> You can only cast a
spell or activate an ability when you have priority—once you lose it, you have
to wait for your opponent to pass it back to you in order to respond.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>When can you give yourself priority? Check out those
little white tabs on that little bar above your hand. Those are your <b>stops</b>.
Each serves as a way to ‘stop’ the game at a given phase in order to gain
priority. <b>The stops on the bottom represent your turn, and the stops on the
                top represent your opponent’s turn.</b></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Currently, only some of the stops are on—there is no stop
set at your upkeep phase, so MTGO will always blast right past it on the way to
your first main phase. There is no stop set at the beginning of your combat
phase either—only during your main phase and at the start of your attack phase.
Conversely, there’s no stop set on your opponent’s main phase but there is a
stop set at the beginning of their combat phase—after all, that’s where you
would normally stop the game to use a tapper or other pre-combat ability anyway.
</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>Make sure you set your stops the way you want them</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'> before you start a game, and be aware of how they work
for when you want to do something in a phase when you wouldn’t normally stop.
For example, if you want to cast Vendilion Clique on your opponent after the
draw step on their turn, you can set that stop after you draw the Clique and
remove it again after you’ve made that play.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Why not just turn on all your stops just in case? You
can, but that forces you to constantly pass priority back to your opponent
during phases of the game when you aren’t going to want to do anything 99.99%
of the time. It gets old fast, believe me. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>If this is all too confusing to you, <b>just keep MTGO’s
                default stops for now.</b> They let you do what you need to do in all but the
most complex situations. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>The priority conversation doesn’t end there, however.  <b>You
                also gain priority whenever your opponent passes it to you.</b> This allows you
to respond to a spell they put on the stack with a card or ability of your own.
This is how paper Magic works too, of course, but when you’re sitting across
from someone there’s generally an understanding that they’ll say something if
they want to respond to a spell that you play. Online, you have to keep hitting
OK whenever you want to pass priority. And remember—<b>your clock is running
                whenever you have priority!</b></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=266 height=218 id="Picture 26"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image021.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Because passing priority is such a drag, Magic Online has
built-in <b>function keys</b> that you can use to speed things along. Here they
are:</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><u><span
                    style='color:#222222'>Keyboard Shortcuts Dealing With Priority/Triggers</span></u></b></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpFirst style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>F2—Pass Priority Until The Next
Thing Happens</span></b><span style='color:#222222'>. This is the same as
clicking the OK button.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>F4—Pass Priority Until Your
Opponent Does Something.</span></b><span style='color:#222222'> Done with your
turn? Have a counterspell up (or a counterspell you’d like to bluff) and/or a
removal spell you might want to use if your opponent casts another threat? F4
is your friend.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>F6—Pass Priority Until You Can Attack
or Block.</span></b><span style='color:#222222'> Similar to F4, except you
won’t get to respond to any of your opponents’ spells. Incredibly useful for
saving time, not so great if you want to bluff a counterspell.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>F8—Pass Priority For The Rest of
The Game As Long As You Can’t Cast Anything Or Activate Any Abilities. </span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'>You only have to click this once per game, and it will
yield for you as long as you’re tapped out and don’t have anything to do. Note
that F8 isn’t smart enough to figure out Force of Will and Daze, so don’t use
this if you’re running Force of Will or Daze (or want to bluff that can cast
Force of Will or Daze).</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>F7—Put All Matching Triggers On
The Stack And Yield To Them.</span></b><span style='color:#222222'> Are you
playing Monastery Mentor? Does your deck have a thousand identical Prowess
triggers waiting to hit the stack? Press F7 so you don’t have to click ‘OK’ for
each trigger individually.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>F3—Cancel All Existing Yield
Functions.</span></b><span style='color:#222222'> Did you press F6 or F8 by
accident? Press F3 to disable their functionality.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpLast style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>Hold CTRL When Casting A
Spell—Retains Priority.</span></b><span style='color:#222222'> Need an example
of why this might be necessary? Let’s pretend you have Lightning Bolt and Fork
in your hand and you want to cast Fork on your Lightning Bolt. If you just play
the Bolt without holding CTRL, it will automatically pass priority to your
opponent without giving you a chance to respond. If your opponent doesn’t have
a response of their own, the Bolt will resolve without letting you cast Fork.
By holding CTRL, it tells MTGO that you have a response of your own to
something you’re putting on the stack, allowing you to cast the Fork.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.25in;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><b><u><span style='color:#222222'>Other Useful Keyboard Shortcuts</span></u></b></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpFirst style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>F5—Look at Face-Down Cards.</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'> Important if you’re playing in a format where morph is a
thing. No, you can’t use this to cheat and look at your opponent’s face-down
cards.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>CTRL+Z—Undo.</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'> This one is especially useful if you tap your mana
incorrectly. As long as you don’t pass priority in the meantime, you can untap
and re-tap your lands with control-Z.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>ALT+Y—Chooses “YES.”</span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>ALT+N—Chooses “NO.”</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'> </span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>Z (or press down on the scroll
wheel, or hold down both mouse buttons simultaneously)—Zoom In On A Card. </span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'>This is especially useful because the ‘small’ version of
a card rarely contains reminder text for a keyworded ability or otherwise
shortcuts the rules text. Zooming in on a card allows you to see everything.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpLast style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>Hold M When Tapping Mana—Causes
Land To Tap For Whatever Mana Ability Is Printed First On The Card.</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'> This is especially useful when you/your opponent has an
Urborg, Tomb of Yawgmoth in play and suddenly you have to tell all your dang
Mountains that yes, you still want to tap them for red. M shortcuts this
process.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Got all that? No? That’s fine—just keep this guide
bookmarked so you can come back to these hotkeys after you have a couple more
games under your belt.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>&nbsp;</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="othersettings">Other Magic Online Settings and Tricks</a></span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>If you’re anything like me, you’ve probably already had a
bellyful of Magic Online’s <b>default sounds</b>. I’d rather crank up the Devo
and let Magic Online stay silent. You can adjust the sound (as well as the
default animations for both <b>summoning sickness</b> and <b>foil cards</b>)
under the <b>ACCOUNT</b> tab.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image"
            style='color:#222222'><img border=0 width=624 height=314 id="Picture 41"
                                       src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image022.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>You can also use this tab to set your avatar, change your
keyboard shortcuts, enable or disable notifications, enable or disable people’s
ability to message you, and turn off the (very aggressive) profanity filter. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>You also might want to <b>turn on auto save for your
                draft logs</b>. This will allow you to go back later and see all the decks
you’ve drafted. It’s a little check box under the “Game History” menu:</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=192 height=479 id="Picture 42"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image023.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Sometimes, you
        will encounter an MTGO bug so severe that it will cost you in terms of prizes
        or entry fees. There are thousands of cards on MTGO, and it seems like at any
        given time there’s a bug affecting one or two of them. Other times, the client
        may disconnect you in the middle of a draft without notice, causing you to miss
        a crucial pick or three. If you encounter one of these bugs and you feel like
        it made a material difference in terms of how a game or tournament played out,
        you can <b>file for reimbursement</b> on the Wizards of the Coast website. The
        link is <a
            href="http://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/mtgo-articles/bug-reportreimbursement-request-2014-06-03">here</a>,
        and I recommend keeping it handy. Note that you will need your <b>event number</b>,
        which can be found under the <b>game history</b> tab.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>&nbsp;</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="formats">Magic Online’s Formats</a></span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Once you’ve mastered the intricacies of MTGO’s interface
and currencies, it’s time to start playing some actual events. <b>The
                Constructed Open Play Room</b> is the best place to start—all of these games
are <b>free</b>, but they have <b>no prizes</b>—it’s the equivalent of sitting
down across from someone at your local game store and asking them if they’d
like to have a quick game before the tournament begins. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Just select the format you’d like to play, hit the big
PLAY button at the bottom, and wait for an opponent. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=338 height=579 id="Picture 35"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image024.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'>I’m sure
you’re already familiar with Standard, Modern. Legacy, Vintage, Commander, and
Planechase, but there are a couple of online-only formats that are worth
discussing further:</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'>&nbsp;</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span style='color:#222222'>Pauper</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'> is an eternal format where you can only play with cards
that have been released at <b>common in an MTGO-legal set.</b> There is an
established Pauper metagame and a small banned list—for more, check out </span><a
            href="http://www.mtggoldfish.com/metagame/pauper#online">the extensive Pauper metagame
            page at MTGGoldfish.</a></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'>&nbsp;</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNoSpacing style='text-indent:0in'><b>Planeswalker</b> was a format
        created to help transition people from Duels of the Planeswalkers (the older
        version of Magic Duels) using gold-bordered (not legal in tournaments) cards
        that came in special <b>“Planeswalker Packs” </b>at the MTGO store. You can
        still buy all the Planeswalker Packs there, but there hasn’t been a new Planeswalker
        Pack released in over a year and I don’t expect that to change. No big loss—the
        format was never all that popular, and it’s still difficult to find an
        opponent. </p>

    <p class=MsoNoSpacing style='text-indent:0in'>&nbsp;</p>

    <p class=MsoNoSpacing style='text-indent:0in'><b>Freeform</b> is the Magic
        Online version of <b>casual Magic</b>. Normal constructed deckbuilding
        restrictions apply, but you aren’t constrained by any format-specific banned or
        restricted lists. Want to play with four copies of Black Lotus in your 250-card
        Battle of Wits deck? Go nuts!</p>

    <p class=MsoNoSpacing style='text-indent:0in'>&nbsp;</p>

    <p class=MsoNoSpacing style='text-indent:0in'><b>Freeform Vanguard</b> is a
        casual format where players can start each game with one or more vanguards in
        effect. Vanguards are like Commanders with passive abilities that modify the
        game and cannot be destroyed. Each one encourages a slightly different style of
        deckbuilding and has a unique power.</p>

    <p class=MsoNoSpacing style='text-indent:0in'>&nbsp;</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image" style='color:#222222'><img
                border=0 width=624 height=347 id="Picture 36"
                src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image025.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNoSpacing style='text-indent:0in'><b>&nbsp;</b></p>

    <p class=MsoNoSpacing style='text-indent:0in'><b>Vanguards also double as
            avatars.</b> You know that little Birds of Paradise with our life total on it
        while we were playing our constructed game? Under <b>ACCOUNT</b>, we can change
        that to any of the five avatars that came with our account. If I select
        Chandra, she’ll appear instead of Birds of Paradise from now on!</p>

    <p class=MsoNoSpacing align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in'><img
            border=0 width=624 height=259 id="Picture 37"
            src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image026.jpg"></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'>&nbsp;</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'>You can get new avatars by winning events,
        but they no longer have special abilities for the Freeform Vanguard
        format—their only use now is purely cosmetic. If you want to play Freeform
        Vanguard, you’ll have to buy the old ability-centric vanguards on the secondary
        market.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'>&nbsp;</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b>Momir Basic</b> is another Vanguard
        format, and it requires you to own the <b>Momir Vig, Simic Visionary</b>
        vanguard card. This is what he does:</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'><img border=0 width=222 height=299
                                                        id="Picture 38"
                                                        src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image027.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='font-size:20.0pt;
color:#222222'>&nbsp;</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'>Neat, right? The idea is that both
        players begin with the Momir Vig avatar and a deck that is usually just 60 basic
        lands. You then battle it out with <b>random creatures from throughout the
            history of Magic.</b> It’s a very fun causal format, and all you need to play
        it is the Momir Vig avatar—usually available for somewhere between ten and
        twenty tickets on the secondary market.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'>&nbsp;</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'>If Momir isn’t crazy and chaotic
        enough for you, consider playing the <b>MoJhoSto</b> format. When players
        choose <b>Freeform Vanguard</b>, nine times out of ten they are looking for a
        MoJhoSto game. This combines the Momir Vig avatar with the Jhoria of the Ghitu
        (random spells) and the Stonehewer Giant avatar (random equipment) for maximum wackiness.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'>&nbsp;</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'>There are a few other popular MTGO
        formats that are only available seasonally/occasionally. <b>Legacy Cube Draft</b>
        is available every time there is a window between when a set is released in
        paper form and when it makes its way online. <b>Vintage Cube Draft</b>, a more
        combo-oriented version of the cube with Black Lotus, Moxen, etc., that usually
        shows up once a year during the holiday season. These cube formats are very popular,
        and many people have MTGO accounts exclusively for cubing purposes.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'>&nbsp;</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'>Magic Online has also recently
        introduced <b>Gauntlets</b>, which are constructed tournaments featuring a small
        subset of decks—for example, each list that made day two at a Pro Tour. You
        don’t need to own any cards to enter a gauntlet—you just sign up, pay the fee,
        and are randomly assigned a constructed deck to battle with. These tournaments
        are also very affordable, and are a great option for anyone who loves
        constructed but doesn’t want to invest in a big digital card collection.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'>&nbsp;</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'>&nbsp;</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="costs">The Costs and Currencies of Magic Online</a></span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>You can always join a causal pick-up game for free, but
Magic Online’s official tournaments all have a cost associated with them. At
some point, you’re going to want to draft or play in a constructed event. And
for that, you’re gonna need some currency.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Interestingly enough, Magic Online actually has TWO
currencies: <b>Event Tickets</b> (colloquially referred to as “<b>tix</b>”) and
<b>Play Points</b>.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=420 height=150 id="Picture 4"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image028.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>Event Tickets</span></b><span style='color:#222222'> have
a nominal value of one US dollar. If you live in the United States, you can buy
Event Tickets directly from the Magic Online store for a dollar each. I say
“nominal” because </span><a
            href="https://www.mtgotraders.com/store/10tickets.html">there are other places
            where you can also buy tickets</a><span style='color:#222222'>, and you can
often find them for less than a buck. $0.95 is about as cheap as you’ll ever
find these—that’s the price most dealers will pay </span><a
            href="https://www.mtgotraders.com/store/selltickets.html">if you decide to sell
            your extra tickets</a><span style='color:#222222'>. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>if you find a bunch of tickets being sold for less than
ninety-five cents each, I strongly advise against buying them. It is likely
that they have come from a stolen or hacked account, and you are risking an
account ban from Wizards of the Coast for participating in an illegal
transaction. As long as you buy your tickets from the Magic Online store or
from a reputable and established dealer, though, you have nothing to worry
about.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>One of the biggest quirks of the Magic Online market is
that there are no fractions of a ticket. If you have a card worth half a
ticket, I can’t chop one of my tickets in half and give it to you—it’s all or
nothing. This is a big part of why Magic Online uses <b>bots</b> in order to
facilitate card buying and selling—more about them a little bit later.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>Play Points</span></b><span style='color:#222222'> are
very similar to Event Tickets, and almost every event cost can be paid using
either currency. A Play Point is worth about 1/10<sup>th</sup> as much as an
Event Ticket, so think of them as dimes. If an event costs fourteen Event
Tickets to enter, it’ll cost you 140 Play Points.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>There’s another big difference between the two
currencies, though—while Event Tickets can be bought and sold on the secondary
market, Play Points are bound to your individual account. That means you can
use Event Tickets to enter events, or you can use them to buy singles on the
secondary market. Play Points can ONLY be used to enter events on your
account—you can’t ever buy cards with them.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>What does this mean from a practical perspective? It’s
pretty simple. Whenever you have a choice between using Event Tickets or Play
Points, use up your Play Points first!</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Any way you slice it, Magic Online is significantly
cheaper to play than paper Magic—and Vintage is almost SIXTEEN TIMES cheaper! Take
that, reserved list! Who needs cardboard anyhow?</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="events">Magic Online’s Events</a></span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Things will start to cost money once you leave the
Constructed Open Play Room. Don’t forget that your new MTGO account came with
twenty new player points, though, and you can use these to play in the
following <b>new player events:</b></span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpFirst style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>New to Magic Online Standard</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'>—a two-player constructed queue that costs <b>two</b> new
player points.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>New to Magic Online Phantom Swiss
Draft</span></b><span style='color:#222222'>—a four-player draft queue (always
the current draft format) that costs <b>four</b> new player points.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpLast style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>New to Magic Online Phantom Swiss
Sealed</span></b><span style='color:#222222'>—these are big daily events that
are scheduled under the “featured tournaments” section at the top of the <b>PLAY
                LOBBY</b> page. They require <b>five</b> new player points.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>New player events get old fast, though, and at some point
you’re going to want to swim with the big fish. Have you ever played in side
events at a Grand Prix or Open event? Most of Magic Online’s tournaments are
sort of like that—you enter a queue, and it fires as soon as it gets eight
committed players. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Here are a few MTGO terms you should know before you
enter a queue:</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>Phantom Draft</span></b><span style='color:#222222'>—this
means that <b>you do not get to keep any of the cards you open!</b> The new to
MTGO drafts are all phantom, as are all the cube drafts. Once the draft ends, so
does your access to those cards.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>Single Elimination Draft</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'>—exactly what it sounds like. Once you lose a match, you
are eliminated from the tournament. A favorite choice of experienced players,
who would rather move on after a loss. Single elimination drafts tend to have
more top-heavy prize payouts, and the events move quicker because you’re not
waiting around for nearly as many players to finish their games after round
one.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>Swiss Draft</span></b><span style='color:#222222'>—the
opposite of single elimination. In a Swiss draft, you get to play all three
rounds regardless of how well you do. Prize payouts tend to be flatter, and
these drafts tend to attract less competitive players. A great choice for a lazy
Sunday at home when you want to draft something fun and don’t care as much
about winning.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>8-4</span></b><span style='color:#222222'>—the prize
payout for the event. In an 8-4 draft, the winner gets eight packs, the player
who loses in the final round gets four packs, and nobody else gets any. A
favorite choice of competitive players.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>6-2-2-2</span></b><span style='color:#222222'>—six packs
to the winner, two for whomever loses in the finals, and two to both players
who lose in the second round.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>4-3-2-2</span></b><span style='color:#222222'>—four packs
to the winner, three for second place, two for both players who lose in the
second round. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>Fast Build</span></b><span style='color:#222222'>—fast
build events reduce deckbuilding time from ten minutes to five. This option is
good for drafters who like to build their decks on the fly and don’t wait to
wait around for everybody else to finish.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>All draft queues require eight people to play<b> </b>and
range in cost between 10 tix (flashback draft) and 14 tix (current draft). They
can also all be entered using the requisite number of play points (100 and 140
respectively) or the three proper booster packs for the chosen format alongside
a 2 tix entry fee.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>On the constructed side, you have a choice between single
elimination eight person events in Standard and Modern (six tix each) or two
person queues (one match only) that cost 2 tix to play. The two-person queues
are available across all competitive formats—Standard, Modern, Legacy, Vintage,
Pauper, and even Momir Basic</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Want to play in a tournament but don’t have four hours to
spare right now? Consider joining a league. <b>Leagues</b> might actually be
the best deal on Magic Online right now—for eight tix (constructed) or
twenty-eight tix (sealed) you get five matches of Magic spread out among
however many days you want. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Leagues run for about two months each, so just make sure
you don’t join one on the last day if you don’t have time to play that much
Magic right away. The prizes are quite reasonable, too—in a constructed league,
you just have to go 3-2 to break even. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Want to play in a big event that plays out all at once?
MTGO still has large <b>daily tournaments</b> that run anywhere between 12 and
256 players. They cost 12 tix each, they’re four rounds long, and you need to
win at least three of those rounds in order to get prizes. These were more
popular before leagues were created, but they’re still the only way to play in,
say, a large Vintage event. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Daily events not competitive enough for you? Well, you
can <b>qualify for the Pro Tour</b> right here on Magic Online! Up at the top
of the PLAY LOBBY page, you’ll see a series of featured tournaments, most of
which are <b>PTQ Preliminaries</b>. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image"
            style='color:#222222'><img border=0 width=624 height=90 id="Picture 39"

            style='color:#222222'> </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Just like the New Player Sealed queues, these are <b>scheduled
                events</b>—they don’t fire immediately once they fill up; you have to wait for
them to begin at their scheduled time.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>All these tournaments are five rounds each. Win at least
four of them and you’ll get a <b>PTQ Finals Token</b> that looks something like
this:</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>These tokens are account-bound—you can’t sell or trade
them. Once you have a finals token, you can <b>use it anytime that season to
                enter a PTQ finals</b>—constructed if you played in a constructed prelim,
limited if you played in a limited prelim. These are also large scheduled
events, so make sure you have lots of time before you join.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Lastly, you can quality for the <b>Magic Online
                Championship Series (MOCS)</b> tournament. All competitive MTGO events offer
MOCS Qualifier Points—so if you win a bunch of drafts or constructed tournaments,
you’ll start amassing QPs alongside your prize packs. Entry into one of the
monthly MOCS tournaments costs 35 QPs, and the top 8 all get invites to the
next MOCS playoff event. Get into the finals at MOCS playoff and you’ll find
yourself with an invite to the next Pro Tour! </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>&nbsp;</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="trading">Buying, Selling, and Trading on Magic
Online</a></span></b></p>
    <br>
    <br>
    <iframe width="942" height="530" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/7sV8F1qaFNc" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe>
    <br>
    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>I love trading Magic cards.</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'> There’s nothing better than that firm handshake between players
who have both gotten just a little bit closer to finishing their next Modern
deck. I love flipping through binders and pulling out obscure foils. I love
learning about promos I’ve never seen before. I love meeting new people and
hearing about their Magic-playing lives.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Trading on Magic Online is a little different. The whole
process starts back in the <b>collection tab</b>. This is where you should go
to put together your <b>trade binders</b>—without a binder, trading and selling
cards are both impossible!</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Luckily, binders are easy to make. Underneath your list
of decks in the collection interface, there’s a tab called “Binders” where all
of your trade binders are stored. Just like in paper Magic, you can either
stuff all your extra cards into a single binder or you can build different
binders with different purposes—say, one filled with expensive Modern cards and
another that only has the Standard commons you aren’t using.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=624 height=432 id="Picture 13"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image031.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>The important thing to remember here is that <b>only one
                binder can be active at a single time.</b> When you open a trade window with a
bot, the only cards they’ll see are those in your active binder. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>I recommend being conservative when choosing which cards
to put in your binders—nothing stings more than accidentally trading away half
your Modern deck by accident and having to buy it back at full price.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>Make sure that your event tickets are in your active
binder!</span></b><span style='color:#222222'>  Unless your tickets are
available for trade, you won’t be able to use them to buy packs or cards in the
trade room. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=493 height=270 id="Picture 14"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image032.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Now that we’ve got some cards and tickets in our active
binder, it’s time to start making deals. Unfortunately, <b>casual trading is
                very rare on MTGO</b>. You might find someone willing to make a deal with you,
but you’ll probably have to wait around a while and <b>I don’t really recommend
                it.</b> </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Why? Well, There are two big reasons for this. The first
is MTGO’s interface. The <b>TRADE</b> room looks like this: </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image"
            style='color:#222222'> <img border=0 width=624 height=447 id="Picture 5"
                                        src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image033.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>You can post your own classified ad if you’d like, and if
you sit around all day, you might get a few nibbles as people sift through all
three thousand posts. Trading between players isn’t impossible, it’s just very
inefficient. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>At some point, though, you’ll probably run afoul of the
second big reason why it’s hard to trade on Magic Online. Take another look at
that picture of the MTGO classified ads. See anything odd? </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>That’s right—cards are being sold for fractions of a
ticket. How is this possible? As we discussed in our currency section, Event
Tickets cannot be split up. So what’s going on here?</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Well, almost all the buying and selling on Magic Online
happens via <b>bots</b>—automated Magic Online accounts running programmable
scripts that allow them to make transactions automatically. This isn’t a new
thing—most of the major bot chains have been around for years, and some have
been around since Magic Online’s inception in 2002. They’re as much a part of
MTGO as MTGO itself.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>The bots are smart, and they’re capable of tracking every
interaction they have with your account. Crucially, they’re capable of storing
credit for future transactions. If you buy a card for 2.45 tix from a bot, it
will require you to pay 3 tix now and it will store that extra 0.65 tix in its
memory for a future deal. If you come back tomorrow and want to buy a card that
is worth just 0.10 tix, it will deduct that amount from your credit, and so on.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>This incentivizes you for buying cards from a bot over a
human, because the bot will be able to set the price at the most competitive
margin it can while still making a reasonable profit. Human accounts can’t do
this—they can only round to the nearest ticket. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>You are also incentivized to stick with a single <b>bot
                chain</b>—a group of bots run by the same person or company. All the bots in a
given chain have access to your stored credit, though they might have slightly
different contents. One bot in a chain might have high end foil sets, say,
while another specializes in Standard rares. Once you </span><a
            href="https://www.mtgotraders.com/store/bots.html">pick a bot chain you like</a><span
            style='color:#222222'>, you don’t have to worry about your stored credit going
to waste. It’ll always be there the next time you want to buy a card or a draft
set.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>There are four different kinds of bots, and you should
know the differences between them:</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpFirst style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>Sell Bots</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'>—if you want to <b>buy cards from a bot</b>, use one of
these. The prices listed by the sell bots are how much a card will cost you to
buy. These are the most common type of bot.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>Buy Bots—</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'>if you want to<b> sell cards to a bot, </b>use one of
these.<b> </b>The prices listed by the buy bots are how much they will pay you
for your copy of the card. These are the second most common type of bot.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpMiddle style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>Trade Bots—</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'>if you want to <b>trade cards with a bot</b>, there are
trade bots out there that will make deals with you. These bots are fairly rare,
but they do exist.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoListParagraphCxSpLast style='text-indent:-.25in;line-height:normal;
background:white'><span style='font-family:Symbol;color:#222222'>·<span
                style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><b><span style='color:#222222'>Free Bots—</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'>Free bots will <b>give you free cards</b>, usually
worthless commons, usually with either a daily limit or a “one transaction per
account” caveat. If you like building random casual decks, hit these bots up!
Many of these bots will also <b>take your worthless cards for free</b>—since
there is no way to delete unwanted cards from your account, you can give them
to a free bot if you’d rather keep your collection tab a little cleaner.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image"
            style='font-size:9.5pt;color:#222222'><img border=0 width=624 height=189
                                                       id="Picture 15"
                                                       src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image034.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Open a chase rare and want to sell it? Most of the bot
chains have similar prices, so it usually makes sense to stick with the dealer
where you’ve got the bulk of your credit stored. Sometimes it can be worth it
to do a quick search first, though—I typed “Ulamog Buy” into the search tab.
Most of the dealers are currently paying between 6 and 6.25 tix, but a few were
up around 6.8 and a handful of opportunists were offering as low as 4.65 tix
for the card. The moral of the story? A little research never hurts.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>What if you wanted to buy a copy of Ulamog instead? Again,
you can go to your loyal bot chain or type in “Ulamog Sell” and shop around a
little. Note that <b>not every card is listed in the classifieds</b>—if you’re
looking for a bulk rare or third tier staple, you’ll have to start clicking on
bots and poking around their trade binders. This is another reason why I like
dealing with the bigger chains—they often have five or ten bots, each with a
slightly different trade binder. If I really need four foil copes of Wavewing
Elemental, I can be reasonably sure they’re kicking around somewhere on one of
those bots.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Once you’ve found a bot you want to buy from or sell to,
you might have to <b>wait for it to be open</b>. See where it says [BUSY] or
[OPEN] next to the bot’s name on the picture above? Bots that are busy are
currently engaged with another MTGO user. Just wait a few seconds and the bot
should open right up.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Don’t know what cards you need to compete a deck? Don’t
want to go digging through a bot’s inventory looking for twelve different
spells? <b>MTGO’s trade interface search tool is here to help.</b> It allows
you to <b>select any of your decks that currently have missing cards</b>, and
it will <b>automatically add those cards to your side of the trade </b>while
also letting you know which of the missing cards the bot doesn’t currently
have. Easy, right? </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image"
            style='color:#222222'><img border=0 width=624 height=333 id="Picture 48"
                                       src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image035.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Remember our U/G deck from earlier? I think it’s a
Lumbering Falls away from breaking the format wide open. I’ve put all my tix
into my active trade binder, so it’s time to start wheeling and dealing. I type
“Lumbering Falls” into the search bar and find a reputable bot chain selling
Lumbering Falls for 1.02 tickets each—perfect. Let’s double-click on the bot’s
name and enter the trading interface.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image"
            style='color:#222222'><img border=0 width=624 height=316 id="Picture 16"
                                       src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image036.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>The first thing I’ve noticed is that the bot’s trade
binder looks a lot like my own collection, only with way more cards. The
interface is the same, so I just use the familiar search tool to find a copy of
Lumbering Falls and <b>double-click to add it to the trade</b>.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=508 height=272 id="Picture 17"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image037.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Once I double click the card, the bot automatically
calculates the costs of the deal, factor in my saved bot chain credit (none, in
this case), and offers me a final price for everything. I always double-check
to make sure that I’m satisfied with the total before completing the trade. In
this case, everything looks good.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=249 height=256 id="Picture 34"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image038.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Once I’m satisfied, I click <b>SUBMIT </b>and another
page comes up, this one showing a copy of Lumbering Falls on the left and a
pair of event tickets on the right. I click <b>CONFIRM TRADE</b> down at the
bottom, and that’s it—time to go modify my deck and enter some tournaments!</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>Selling cards to a bot</span></b><span style='color:#222222'>
is a very similar process. Just double-click on a <b>buy bot</b> and it will
automatically select all the cards from your active trade binder that it is
willing to buy. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image"
            style='color:#222222'><img border=0 width=624 height=262 id="Picture 19"
                                       src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image039.jpg"></span><span
            style='color:#222222'> </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Don’t like the price offered on a card or two? You can
remove cards from the deal by following the bot’s instructions (they vary from
bot to bot) in the chat box.  This is one of the best parts about dealing
exclusively with robots—their feelings won’t be hurt if you’d rather keep your
Monastery Swiftspears than trade them in for a penny apiece.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>If you do want to try to find a human trade partner, your
best bet is to <b>make a post on the MTGO classifieds.</b> Click on the TRADE
tab on the top of your client, and you’ll get 250 characters to work with down
at the bottom of your screen. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image"
            style='color:#222222'><img border=0 width=624 height=127 id="Picture 31"
                                       src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image040.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>Be sure to specify that you are human!</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'> No, this isn’t just <i>Bladerunner</i>-style paranoia. Since
bots are incapable of the nuances required in a human-to-human trade,
specifying that you are human lets other traders know that you’re an actual
person with the ability to negotiate and discuss terms.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'> If you do find someone you want to trade with, you can <b>right-click
                their username to open a chat or the trading page.</b> </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal;background:white'><span class="center-image" style='color:#222222'><img border=0
                                                                      width=391 height=268 id="Picture 33"
                                                                      src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image041.jpg"></span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>When interacting with another human, it is considered
proper to open a private chat with them before initiating a trade. If you’re
dealing with a bot, feel free to just hit “Trade.” </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Making a trade with a human works just like it does when
buying or selling with a bot, only you have to discuss your deal with your
trade partner in the chat window and make sure they add the correct cards on
their side of the deal. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='color:#222222'>Always double-check a trade before confirming!</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'> There is no way to negate a trade once it has gone
through, and some unscrupulous players do try to sneak a key card out of their
side of the deal (or add one on your side) at the last second, hoping you won’t
notice. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>&nbsp;</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="economy">MTGO Economy Tips and Tricks</a></span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>There’s plenty of correlation between online prices and
paper prices—cards that are good in tier one decks tend to be expensive across
all platforms—but it’s wrong to assume that the rules are the same on MTGO as
they are in paper. For example, a physical copy of Daze will only set you back
about three bucks, but you’d be lucky to find a copy on MTGO for less than 28
tickets. Paper copies of Underground Sea are worth almost four hundred dollars
each, but you can nab a copy for 22 tix online. What gives?</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Well, most of these discrepancies exist because <b>set
                releases happen very differently online</b>. The first iteration of MTGO was
launched alongside Invasion, and for many years you simply couldn’t play with
any cards older than that. More recently, MTGO began adding older cards to the
existing pool—some in their original sets, others in <i>Modern Masters</i>-style
expansions. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>At this point, every significant card in Vintage and
Legacy is available on MTGO in some form, though a few older unplayable cards
have never been released online. Underground Sea is so expensive in paper
because it’s on the reserved list and it hasn’t been printed since 1994. Online,
it has been printed in <i>Masters’ Edition II</i>, <i>Masters Edition IV</i>,
and <i>Vintage Masters</i>. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>MTGO prices also differ because of <b>flashback drafts</b>.
If you head down to your local shop, your options are probably going to be limited
to the current draft format. Online, I can draft any set currently in Standard
as well as the flashback format of the day—right now, it’s Champions of
Kamigawa. This means that brand new copies of Sensei’s Divining Top are
entering the MTGO marketplace every day until the flashback format changes to
something else. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Many frugal MTGO players use these flashback drafts a
opportunities to build up their Legacy and Modern collections. Pay attention to
the schedule of upcoming flashback drafts, sell your staples before they’re
reprinted, and buy back in once freshly-drafted copies start flooding the
marketplace. A few weeks ago, Tops were about 4 tix each. Right now, they’re
closer to 2.75 tix apiece.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'> Another big difference between paper Magic and MTGO? <b>Casual
                cards are much cheaper online.</b> While you can play Commander on MTGO, and
there’s even a multiplayer interface, casual players have mostly given the
program a pass. Chromatic Lantern, for example, is $9.50 in paper thanks to
Commander demand. Online? They’re just 0.29 tix each. This is great news if you
just want to kick back with some Commander—you can make a fun, competitive deck
for twenty or thirty bucks! </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Bulk rares also behave very differently online. In paper,
you can generally get between ten and fifteen cents each for even the worst rares.
<b>Online, bulk rares are worth less than a penny apiece.</b> This can make the
deepest bulk specs pay off in a way they can’t in paper, but without much casual
demand it’s much harder to hit on a good long term bulk buy.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>We’ll be covering set redemption in the final section of
this article, but I want to touch on the issue a little bit here because it’s
important to understand the way it warps the price of cards in Standard.
Because you can only redeem cards as complete sets, <b>bulk mythics tend to be
                worth more online than in paper</b>. This is worth keeping in mind if you’re
trying to rare draft. When in doubt, grab the mythic—uncommons and rares are
only worth something when they’re being played in competitive decks. Just make
sure you sell them before the set redemption period ends—after that, they can
drop from a few tix each to just a few cents each.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Now that we have a snapshot of the MTGO market, let’s
examine the way it moves.  If you’re used to tracking the price of your paper
Magic collection, you might be shocked at <b>how fast the Magic Online economy adjusts
                to new decks and trends.</b> Buyouts can happen quickly in paper Magic, but it
still takes a few days (or sometimes weeks) for prices to adjust and settle. After
all, paper cards still have to be mailed around the world. That takes time,
which leads to lags in value that can last for days.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>On MTGO, cards can be bought and sold worldwide in
seconds. If a chase rare falls out of favor in Standard, the online price can
go into freefall in a matter of hours. If a bulk card starts making waves in a
new Modern deck, it might not even take an hour for the price to spike across
every bot chain on the program. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Much like in paper Magic, <b>singles prices tend to be at
                their highest during prerelease week</b>. Because prerelease events are
expensive and card supply is low, I recommend selling chase singles you open at
the prerelease as soon as possible. The exception to this rule? <b>Bulk mythic
                foils tend to be at their cheapest during prerelease week.</b> People aren’t
thinking about set redemption yet, so if you want to redeem a foil set at any
point, start trying to pick up the pieces right away. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>As an MTGO newbie, the important thing is not to
overreact in either direction. Just like in paper Magic, you rarely want to buy
into a spike unless you think a card still has room to grow and you probably don’t
want to sell at the absolute bottom of the market. </span><a
            href="http://www.mtggoldfish.com/"><b>MTGGoldfish</b></a><b><span
                style='color:#222222'> is THE place to track online price trends</span></b><span
            style='color:#222222'>. If you’re not sure whether a card is heading up or
down, check there first.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>What’s the best way to play <b>the most Magic for the
                least amount of money?</b> <b>Play constructed.</b> Yes, buying cards costs
money, but constructed tournaments almost always offer better bang for your overall
buck. If you want to try to “go infinite” with your Magic Online account—winning
so much that you don’t have to keep pouring more money into event fees—constructed
is just about the only possible path. If you can go infinite playing in limited
events on MTGO, congratulations—you’re a Pro Tour-level drafter. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>You should also <b>check the secondary market price of
                booster packs before joining a draft.</b> Sometimes it makes sense to just give
MTGO your fourteen event tickets, but it’s usually cheaper to buy packs from
your friendly neighborhood bot chain and use those plus the two tix surcharge
when entering a limited event. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Last, try to abide by my biggest rule of MTGO finance—<b>never,
                ever open booster packs!</b> Magic Online’s biggest draw is the ability to join
a never-ending stream of drafts, and as we’ve just discussed, booster packs can
be used as part of the entry cost. This means that digital boosters are often
worth more than their paper counterparts, and their contents are often worth
less. </span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='color:#222222'>Cracking packs on MTGO…not even once.</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='font-size:9.5pt;color:#222222'>&nbsp;</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="redemption">Set Redemption and Selling Your
Collection</a></span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span
            style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'>&nbsp;</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Part of the way Wizards
        of the Coast ensures the stability of your digital collection’s value is by
        tying MTGO cards directly to their paper counterparts. This is done through a
        process called set redemption, where <b>you can pay a fee to transmute your
            Magic Online cards into physical cardboard.</b> </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Here’s how it
        works: </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>First, <b>you can
            only redeem cards in complete sets.</b> You can’t just buy fifty copies of
        Kozilek, the Great Distortion and ask Wizards to send them to you. You’d have
        to buy fifty copies of each card in Oath of the Gatewatch and redeem the entire
        set, commons and all.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Second, <b>you’re
            only able to redeem recent sets.</b> Buy a complete set of Battle for Zendikar
        and you’ll be able to redeem it. A set of Onslaught? Not so much. Redeemable
        sets have to have been released in paper form, and they can’t be Masters-style
        expansions—<i>Vintage Masters</i>, an online-exclusive set, was not eligible
        for redemption. <i>Eternal Masters</i> will be printed in paper, but you won’t
        be able to redeem your MTGO copies regardless.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>How long do you
        have to redeem a set? Well, Magic Online has a <b>guarantee date</b> and a <b>cutoff
            date</b> for each expansion. Sets are only guaranteed to be available for
        redemption until that guarantee date—if WotC sells out of before that, they’ll
        print more. Once that date has passed—usually about a month after it leaves
        Standard—WotC will keep allowing redemptions until the cutoff date or until
        they run out of available stock. For example, Dragon’s Maze was released on
        MTGO on May 13, 2013. The guarantee date was October 31, 2014. The cutoff date
        is November 2, 2015. </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>In my experience,
        sets are often available for a long time after their guarantee date. Gatecrash
        and Dragon’s Maze were both in stock all the way up to their cutoff date. This
        doesn’t always hold true, though—Theros was a popular set for redemption, and
        it sold out in early February 2016, three months after its guarantee date but eight
        months before its cutoff date. If you want to redeem a complete set, I
        recommend doing so before the guarantee date if at all possible.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Unfortunately, <b>set
            redemption costs money</b>. There is a twenty-five dollar fee per set, and
        there’s no way to avoid paying that cost. There is also a $3 shipping charge
        per order, but that fee doesn’t change if you redeem more than one set. Because
        of that, I highly recommend redeeming multiple sets at once to keep your
        shipping costs down.</p>

    <p class="MsoNormal center-image" style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'><img border=0
                                                                       width=624 height=421 id="Picture 43"
                                                                       src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image042.jpg"></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>There’s another
        way to get your cards off MTGO—<b>sell your collection to a </b><a
            href="https://www.mtgotraders.com/buylist/"><b>reputable dealer</b></a>. </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>I don’t recommend
        dealing with anyone who isn’t a trusted friend or one of the major bot chains—just
        like when you buy event tickets on the secondary market, you have to be
        vigilant of scammers and fraudsters. At some point during a collection sale,
        you’re going to have to hit a button that transfers your valuable cards to
        someone else in exchange for their word that you’ll get paid. Is it worth the
        anxiety of an unknown buyer for a shot at a couple of extra bucks? Remember: if
        a deal seems like it’s too good to be true, it probably is.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>In order to sell
        your collection to any of the big bot chains, you’re going to have to <b>generate
            a CSV file</b>—this is a document created by MTGO that contains the full
        contents of your collection. Dealers are able to use this in order to make sure
        they can give you the most accurate offer possible. </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Here’s how to
        generate a CSV:</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>First, go to your
        <b>COLLECTION</b> tab. Under <b>Versions</b>, make sure that <b>show versions
            separately</b> is checked.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal'><img border=0 width=624 height=241 id="Picture 44"
                         src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image043.jpg"></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Next, you’re
        going to want to <b>create a new trade binder.</b> Call it <b>Collection For
            Sale</b> (or something.) Make sure it is your <b>Active Trade Binder.</b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal'><img border=0 width=343 height=274 id="Picture 45"
                         src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image044.jpg"></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Over on the left
        side of the screen, click <b>reset all filters</b>.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Then, <b>click on
            a card in your collection</b>—it doesn’t matter which one. Hit <b>CTRL+A to
            select all your other cards.</b> Then <b>right-click and select “Add All To
            Open Binder.”</b></p>

    <p class="MsoNormal center-image" style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'><img border=0
                                                                       width=553 height=254 id="Picture 46"
                                                                       src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image045.jpg"></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Now click over
        from “Cards” to <b>“Other Products”</b> and <b>repeat this process. </b>This
        adds all your tickets, avatars, etc. to the binder as well.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Down at the
        bottom left, <b>right-click the active binder</b> and hit <b>Export</b>. </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center;text-indent:0in;
line-height:normal'><img border=0 width=459 height=340 id="Picture 47"
                         src="The%20Complete%20Beginners%20Guide%20to%20Magic%20Online_files/image046.jpg"></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'> </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Make sure you
        save the document as a <b>CSV file!</b> This is what the big boys are going to
        want to see. In my experience, it usually takes a day or two before getting an
        offer. Much easier than haggling over condition at a Grand Prix, right?</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>&nbsp;</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="resources">Suggested Resources</a></span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:
0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><span style='color:#222222'>The more
time you spend on Magic Online, the more MTGO-related media you’ll want to
consume. Here are my favorite resources:</span></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-indent:

text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'><a style="color:#000 !important" name="final">Final Thoughts</a></span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;
text-align:center;text-indent:0in;line-height:normal;background:white'><b><span
                style='font-size:20.0pt;color:#222222'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Feel like a Magic
        Online expert yet? Well, you might not be quite as proficient as Luis
        Scott-Vargas or Gaby Spartz, but I bet you know more after reading this guide than
        80% of the Joe and Jane Randoms you’ll face in the constructed two-player
        queues. </p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>The most
        important thing to remember is that you will need to be patient with yourself.
        Learning MTGO is a process—you will make miss-clicks, and some of them will
        cost you games. It happens to everyone from the goofiest amateur to the most seasoned
        pro. That’s the price we all have to pay for getting to play the greatest game
        ever created, 24/7.</p>

    <p class=MsoNormal style='text-indent:0in;line-height:normal'>Is it worth it? Heck
        yeah. In fact, I think I’m going to go join a draft right now! Want to join?</p>
